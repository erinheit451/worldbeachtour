# Marquee Beach Pages — Structural & Rhetorical Audit

**Pages audited:** Copacabana, Waikīkī, Bondi, Praia do Norte (Nazaré), Teahupoʻo
**Date:** 2026-04-19
**Scope:** What's missing, what's redundant, what the owner is sensing when they say "they don't pop."

---

## Executive Summary

The five marquee pages are **all the same page in different costumes**. Every one is a 7,500–17,000-word long-form essay built around the same 19–22-section template — *If you have two minutes → The Story → Paths → The Zones → A Day Here → History → The Calendar → The Water → The Rivalry → Where to Stay → Eat & Drink → In the Culture → Before You Go → Safety → The View Back → Honest Context → Data & Provenance.* The voice is genuinely strong (declarative, locally-validated, opinionated where it counts) and the research is real, but because the **architecture refuses to differentiate**, the signature thing about each beach gets demoted to one section among twenty rather than being the spine of the page. Copa is most coherent because the template happens to fit it; Teahupoʻo and Nazaré strain the seams; Bondi is bloated; Waikīkī is over-cautioned. The owner's "doesn't pop" intuition is correct: the pages read like a magazine cover wrapped around a Wikipedia stub of consistent shape — every beach gets a "Where to Stay" tier table whether it earns one or not, and every beach has to fight the template to express what makes it itself.

## 1. Copacabana — `/beaches/copacabana-7`

1. **Hero.** Aerial sunset arc photo, eyebrow "Rio de Janeiro, BR · A single page, deeply", H1 "Copacabana", subhead "The beach the world imagines when it imagines a beach." Strong, declarative, distinct.
2. **Section sequence & length.** ~7,500 words across 19 sections in the standard template. Story (400) → Calçadão (350) → Aterro (250) → Zones (550) → Day (400) → History (800) → Calendar (500) → Water (350) → Rivalry (300) → Stay (200) → Eat (450) → Music (400) → Culture (650) → Before You Go (500) → Safety (350) → View Back (200) → Honest Context (450) → Data. Of the five, this is the **shortest and most balanced** — no section runs away from the others.
3. **Signature components.** The Calçadão, the 1970 Aterro, the Postos 1–6 zone system, Music (bossa nova + funk carioca), Réveillon, the favela-above honest-context. Calçadão **earns** its place — it's the rhetorical center. Aterro, Postos, Music all earn it. Réveillon and favela-above earn it.
4. **Voice.** Best voice of the five. "It deserves that reputation and also undermines it in ways only Cariocas fully understand." "The most-walked work of public art in the Americas." Confidently opinionated; sourced where contested. Not a wiki.
5. **Reader.** The first-time visitor and the Carioca simultaneously, plus LGBTQ+ traveler at Posto 4, surfer at Posto 6, photographer at View Back. The page is **legible to many readers without being addressed to none**.
6. **Visual rhythm.** Hero → 12 inline images → climate graph → comparison table → timeline. Reasonable. But still mostly text-text-image, not magazine-like spread/break/sidebar interplay.
7. **Redundant.** "Where to Stay" is thin (200 words, 3 zones, generic tiers) — feels checkbox. "Data & Provenance" appears whether the reader cares or not.
8. **Missing.** A real **Calçadão piece** (the page mentions it; the page should *be it*). A **New Year's Eve / Réveillon dedicated guide** (the most distinctive event on Earth at this beach gets one paragraph). A **food-stand and quiosque crawl** as its own piece. A **Rio Beaches Compared** standalone (Copa/Ipanema/Leblon/Barra) — the Rivalry table is the seed.
9. **Overall feel.** Closest of the five to a magazine feature. The voice carries it. But the template still drags it back toward "comprehensive guide."

## 2. Waikīkī — `/beaches/waikiki-beach-1`

1. **Hero.** Diamond Head photo, same eyebrow/subhead pattern. H1 "Waikīkī", subhead "The beach that taught the world how to go to the beach." Subhead works; image is a Diamond Head shot, not a Waikīkī-as-place shot — it could be any Hawaii page.
2. **Section sequence & length.** ~13,000 words across 22 sections — **nearly double** Copa for a beach that is, structurally, simpler. Same template plus *Duke & the Beach Boys* (700), *Reading the Land* (900), *Monarchy & Memory* (900), *Mālama* (900). History bloats to 1,200; Culture to 1,100; Honest Context to 700.
3. **Signature components.** Duke, the Hawaiian place-name etymology, Mālama (sunscreen law / honu / Hōkūleʻa), Monarchy & Memory. Duke earns it. Mālama earns it. *Reading the Land* and *Monarchy & Memory* feel like they want to be one section that got split for SEO, and **History** + **Monarchy & Memory** + **Honest Context** are three different versions of the same overthrow story.
4. **Voice.** Strong but **over-cautioned**. "The reef sorts by knowledge; it always has. This is not hostility — it is how a shared resource maintains itself" is a great line. But the page also keeps stopping to *prove* it has the right to talk — kumu hula test references, "a page that tells you Waikīkī is just a nice beach is not a page telling you the truth." It defends itself more than Copa or Bondi do.
5. **Reader.** Four explicit personas listed (surfers / families / history / repeats). This is the only page that *names its readers in a UI element* — and it reads like the editorial system covering for the fact that the prose underneath doesn't actually pivot for any of them.
6. **Visual rhythm.** Excellent — most images of any page (archival Duke, statue, Liliʻuokalani, Moana 1901, Royal Hawaiian, USS Arizona, ISS night photo, 19th-century paintings). The visual layer is doing more work than the prose at differentiating.
7. **Redundant.** Three overlapping history modules (Timeline / Monarchy & Memory / Honest Context). "Where to Stay" tier table generic. The "If you have two minutes" → "Paths" → "Story" sequence stalls for 800 words before anything happens.
8. **Missing.** A **dedicated Duke page** (he's the mythic figure, he gets 700 of 13,000 words). A **"Beyond Waikīkī: the rest of Oʻahu"** explicitly framed (locals' answer to "where should I really go"). A **"Waikīkī before tourism"** as a piece, separate from the apologetic frame. A **family-with-young-kids guide to Waikīkī** as a real product (the beach is genuinely great for it; the page hides this under guilt).
9. **Overall feel.** Reads like the page is **embarrassed to be about Waikīkī.** It keeps establishing credentials. The reader who wants permission to enjoy Waikīkī isn't given it; the reader who wants a sovereignty essay gets it diluted by a hotel tier table. The bones of two great pages are inside one mediocre one.

## 3. Bondi — `/beaches/bondi-beach`

1. **Hero.** Dawn crescent photo. H1 "Bondi Beach", subhead "Australia's identity-image beach — and everything packed into one kilometer of sand." Strong subhead. Hero image is one of three Bondi shots that all look the same on Google; not a *Bondi-only* image.
2. **Section sequence & length.** ~16,500–17,000 words across 22 sections — **the longest of the five.** Standard template plus *The Lifeguards* (1,200), *Black Sunday 1938* (1,100), *Country Before Colony* (1,200), *Shark Country* (900), *Icebergs & Ocean Pools* (850). History runs 2,000 words.
3. **Signature components.** Lifeguards/Bondi Rescue, Black Sunday, Icebergs, Shark Country, Gadigal country. **Lifeguards earns it.** **Black Sunday earns it spectacularly** (it's the rhetorical center the page should be built around). Icebergs earns it. Shark Country and Gadigal both earn the space — but they pull the page in three different rhetorical directions and **none of them wins.**
4. **Voice.** Same voice as Copa/Waikīkī. "Always swim between the red-and-yellow flags. The flags are not a suggestion." "The Australians have simply built the world's most elaborate surveillance state around it." When it's good it's the best of the five. But it's diluted across so many strong sections that nothing dominates.
5. **Reader.** Same four-persona "Paths" pattern. The page actually is built for the visitor, the local doesn't really get spoken to.
6. **Visual rhythm.** 9 images, 3 tables, info boxes. Better than Copa, similar to Waikīkī. Still no real *spread* — no piece that's image-led with caption-as-essay.
7. **Redundant.** *The Lifeguards* + *Black Sunday* + *Safety* are three overlapping rip-current/rescue treatments. *History* + *Country Before Colony* + *Honest Context* are three overlapping First Nations treatments. The page says everything three times.
8. **Missing.** **"The Local's Bondi"** — the obvious one. A page written by/for someone who lives in the eastern suburbs, what they actually do, what they roll their eyes at. A **"Bondi-to-Coogee Walk"** as its own piece (it's mentioned and demoted; it's a 2-hour piece of content). A **"Bondi in Winter"** piece (the page gestures at it; should be a separate landing page). A **Black Sunday narrative essay** as a proper feature, not a section. A **Sunday Nippers** piece for the family reader.
9. **Overall feel.** Bloated. Every Bondi-distinctive thing is here, but because they're all here at equal weight, the page reads like a **dossier** rather than a **portrait.** A magazine editor would cut 5,000 words and split off three sub-pages. The owner's "doesn't pop" instinct is loudest here: there is no one image, one sentence, or one section that this page is *about*.

## 4. Praia do Norte / Nazaré — `/beaches/praia-do-norte-6`

1. **Hero.** Aerial shoreline-with-surfers (Unsplash). H1 "Praia do Norte", subhead "A 900-year-old Portuguese village above a 5-kilometer submarine canyon." **The best subhead of the five** — single sentence, two facts, perfect frame.
2. **Section sequence & length.** ~13,500 words across 21 sections. Standard template plus *The Canyon* (1,100), *The McNamara Era* (900), *The Village Beneath the Big Wave* (800).
3. **Signature components.** The Canyon, McNamara Era, the Village Beneath, Forte de São Miguel Arcanjo, tow-in surfing. Canyon **earns it absolutely** — it's the physics that makes Nazaré Nazaré. McNamara Era earns it. *Village Beneath* earns it (and is the part most likely to be skipped by surfers but most rewarding).
4. **Voice.** Same voice. "Nazaré is spectacular. It is also precarious. Those are the same fact." That sentence is the page's thesis and should be its hero subhead. "Do NOT swim at Praia do Norte. Even in summer."
5. **Reader.** Four "Paths" again — waves / village / pilgrimage / day-tripper. The day-tripper-from-Lisbon reader is the most underserved in the actual prose despite being the highest-volume real-world visitor.
6. **Visual rhythm.** Strong — six photo breakouts in *View Back*, climate graph, numbers table, timeline, comparison table. Probably the best image-to-text ratio of the five.
7. **Redundant.** *Where to Stay* feels obligatory — most people *don't* stay in Nazaré, and the page later says "day trip from Lisbon possible." The tier table fights itself. *Eat & Drink* with eight named restaurants is over-spec'd given visitor patterns. *Family* framing isn't here, which is correct, but a **"Watching from the cliff" guide** isn't either, and that *is* the family product.
8. **Missing.** **"Watching the big waves: a non-surfer's day at Nazaré"** — by far the biggest content gap on the entire site. Every spectator who comes here needs this and the page doesn't have it as a discrete piece. **"Forte de São Miguel Arcanjo"** as its own piece (it's the photo everyone takes, mentioned recurringly, never centered). **"Garrett McNamara: the email that built a town"** as a proper narrative. **"Nazaré in summer"** (when the waves are not happening — what is this place?). A **"Day trip from Lisbon: do or don't"** explicit guide.
9. **Overall feel.** Closer to magazine than Bondi, because the canyon physics + the McNamara story give it a real spine. But the template still tries to make Nazaré act like a beach you stay at and eat at, which it isn't. **The page knows what's interesting; the structure won't let it commit.**

## 5. Teahupoʻo — `/beaches/teahupoo`

1. **Hero.** Aerial wave-break across reef (Ifremer). H1 "Teahupoʻo", subhead "A Tahitian fishing village at the end of a one-lane road, and the most concentrated wave anyone has ridden." Subhead is excellent.
2. **Section sequence & length.** ~13,500 words across 19 sections. Standard template plus *The Bathymetry* (1,200) and *The Tower Fight* (2,500). *In the Culture* is empty (heading only). History runs 2,000.
3. **Signature components.** The Bathymetry, The Tower Fight, the Fenua ʻAihere wilderness zone, the Brice Taerea / Laird Hamilton / Vahine Fierro / Kauli Vaast history beats. **Tower Fight is the strongest single section on any of the five pages** — 2,500 words, real reporting, takes a position. **Bathymetry earns it** and is the only place on the site where physics carries a section. *In the Culture* being empty is a tell that the template was applied before the content was ready.
4. **Voice.** Same. "The wave they share with the world breaks just past the reef, but the wave's economic value flows mostly to people who are not from here." That's the page in one line.
5. **Reader.** The most narrowly addressed of the five — wave enthusiast + responsible-travel reader + policy-aware observer. The page **explicitly is not for families** and is better for it.
6. **Visual rhythm.** TOC sidebar, hero, multiple tables (numbers / fight in numbers / at-a-glance / rivalry), 14-point timeline, 7-image gallery. Strong.
7. **Redundant.** Same template tax: *Where to Stay* tier table for a place where almost no one stays. *Eat & Drink* with five dishes feels invented. *Calendar* logic doesn't really apply to a place defined by swell windows, not seasons. *In the Culture* is literally an empty section.
8. **Missing.** **"How a non-surfer should plan a Teahupoʻo trip"** — exactly the gap the prompt names. The page tells you not to go in the water; it doesn't tell you what your day actually is if you came anyway. **"The Tower Fight"** as a standalone explainer (it deserves its own URL — it's the best journalism on the site). **"The Channel: how to watch from a boat"** (this is the actual product for visitors and it's a paragraph). A **Teahupoʻo / Pipeline / Nazaré big-wave triangle** standalone (the Rivalry row wants to be a piece). A **"Tahiti for travelers who came for Teahupoʻo and need a week"** piece.
9. **Overall feel.** The single best journalism on the site is buried inside the most awkwardly-templated page. *The Tower Fight* should be the front door; instead it's section 8 of 19, after a Day Here rhythm chart that doesn't really apply.

## Cross-Cutting Findings

**1. The template is the brand and the brand is too rigid.** Every page hits the same 19–22 sections in nearly the same order. The eyebrow "{Region} · A single page, deeply" is the same on all five. The H2 furniture is identical: *If you have two minutes / 01 · The Story / · The Zones / · A Day Here / · History / · The Calendar / · The Water / · The Rivalry / · Where to Stay / · Eat & Drink / · In the Culture / · Before You Go / · Safety / · The View Back / · Honest Context / · Data & Provenance.* This is a CMS template, not an editorial decision per beach. **Copa happens to fit it. The other four are paying a tax.**

**2. "Where to Stay" + "Eat & Drink" are checkbox sections.** They appear on every page regardless of whether visitors stay or eat there. Nazaré (mostly day-tripped from Lisbon) and Teahupoʻo (handful of pensions, almost no restaurants) get the same tier table as Waikīkī (literally a hotel district). This is the loudest redundancy.

**3. Three overlapping history modules.** Most pages run *History timeline* + *Honest Context* + a third thematic history (Monarchy & Memory / Country Before Colony / The Village Beneath / The McNamara Era). These should be **one strong piece per beach**, not three weaker ones competing.

**4. "Paths" is admitting the problem in the UI.** The four-reader-type "Paths" element on Waikīkī, Bondi, Nazaré is the editorial system saying "we know we didn't differentiate the prose, so here's a routing widget." A page that actually pivoted for the right reader wouldn't need it.

**5. The hero never carries.** All five heroes are: full-bleed photo, eyebrow, H1 = beach name, subhead = one declarative sentence. The subheads are good. But there is no **distinctive hero treatment** per beach — no Calçadão wave-pattern hero, no Bondi flag-line hero, no Teahupoʻo lip-cylinder hero. The hero is a CMS slot, not a designed moment.

**6. The signature section is buried, not promoted.** Every page has its rhetorical center: Copa = Calçadão, Waikīkī = Duke, Bondi = the Lifeguards or Black Sunday, Nazaré = the Canyon, Teahupoʻo = the Tower Fight. **In every case it lives mid-page**, sandwiched between Zones and Calendar, instead of being a hero-adjacent feature. The page should *open* with the signature, not arrive at it.

**7. The voice is real and is being wasted.** "The most-walked work of public art in the Americas." "The flags are not a suggestion." "Those are the same fact." "The economic value flows mostly to people who are not from here." These sentences are great. They are surrounded by tier tables, climate graphs, and "Data & Provenance" footers that drown them. The voice deserves more empty space around it.

**8. Visual treatment is inline, not designed.** Images are caption-attributed and tasteful but appear as conventional inline figures. There is no full-bleed spread, no image-as-section, no caption-as-pull-quote, no diagram (the canyon section *describes* bathymetry that should be a diagram). The pages **read like blog posts with good fonts, not magazine features**.

**9. Repetition across pages is invisible to a single visitor but obvious to anyone comparing two.** "If you have two minutes" / "How much time?" / "Where on the arc?" / "When should I go?" appear identically across all five. A returning reader sees the chassis, not the beach.

## What's Missing Structurally — Sub-Page Types That Don't Exist

The site treats each beach as **one URL.** Famous beaches need to be **content clusters.** Specific gaps:

- **The Local's Guide** template — explicitly written from a local's perspective, what they roll their eyes at, what they actually do. Most needed for Bondi and Waikīkī.
- **The Spectator's Guide** template — for beaches where most visitors *watch* the thing rather than *do* the thing. Required for Nazaré and Teahupoʻo. Currently there is no answer to "I came for the wave but I'm not surfing it, what is my day."
- **The Single-Event Deep-Dive** template — Réveillon at Copa, Black Sunday at Bondi, Tower Fight at Teahupoʻo, the McNamara email at Nazaré, the 1893 overthrow at Waikīkī. Each is currently a section that wants to be a piece.
- **The Walk / Drive / Crawl** template — Bondi-to-Coogee, the Calçadão end-to-end, Waikīkī sunset stroll, Hana Highway-style for Nazaré coast. Linear-experience guides.
- **The Family Planner** template — *only* for beaches that earn it. Currently every page has a family veneer; instead, the family page should be its own thing for Waikīkī and Bondi, and absent for Teahupoʻo and Nazaré.
- **The Photographer's Guide** template — *The View Back* on every page is the seed. It should be its own piece with composition advice, golden-hour timing, vantage maps. Especially for Nazaré (Forte) and Teahupoʻo (channel).
- **The Comparison / Rivalry standalone** — the single-row Rivalry table on each page wants to be its own piece. Copa vs. Ipanema vs. Leblon. Bondi vs. Manly vs. Coogee. Teahupoʻo vs. Pipeline vs. Nazaré (this one would be enormous).
- **The "If You're Coming Anyway"** template — for places where the honest answer is "don't come" (Teahupoʻo with kids; Praia do Norte to swim). The current "Safety" section says don't; nothing says *if you're coming anyway, here's how.*
- **The "Before Tourism" / Pre-Contact** template — the indigenous and pre-tourism layer is currently squeezed into *Honest Context* on every page. It deserves its own URL with proper depth, especially for Waikīkī (Hawaiian Kingdom) and Bondi (Gadigal country).

The marquee pages should function as **the hub**, with these sub-page types as **spokes** earning their place per beach. Right now every beach is one undifferentiated hub trying to do all jobs at once, and the owner's "doesn't pop" feeling is the result: the signature thing about each beach is structurally indistinguishable from the climate graph.
