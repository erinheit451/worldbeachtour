## 1. Etymology & meaning of "Teahupoʻo"

Teahupoʻo is both **the village name and the wave's name** — the wave is named after the village at the end of the road. ([Wikipedia: Teahupoʻo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teahupo%CA%BBo))

**Tahitian morphology:** the word breaks into three roots — *te* ("the"), *ahu* ("pile, central stone, raised platform, mound, altar, heap"), and *poʻo* ("head, front end, director, father"). Combined, the literal sense is "the pile of heads" / "the heap of heads" / "the broken skull." ([SurferToday — What does Teahupoo mean?](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/what-does-teahupoo-mean))

**Common English translations:** "place of skulls," "wall of skulls," "to sever the head," "broken skull." Wikipedia favors "to sever the head" / "place of skulls"; Tim McKenna and most surf media favor "wall of skulls." ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teahupo%CA%BBo); [Tim McKenna Photography on Facebook](https://www.facebook.com/TimMcKennaPhotography/posts/in-tahitian-language-teahupoo-means-wall-of-skullshow-appropriate/746330493515570/))

**Origin story:** the name commemorates a pre-European battle in the village. One widely retold version says an ancient Tahitian king collected the heads of vanquished enemies; another version says the son of a murdered king avenged his father by eating his killer's brain. ([SurferToday](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/what-does-teahupoo-mean); [Nautical Channel](https://nauticalchannel.com/new/teahupoo)) Both are oral-tradition retellings carried in surf media — **treat as folk etymology, not academic ethnohistory.**

## 2. Geography

- **Commune:** Teahupoʻo is an *associated commune* (commune associée) of **Taiarapu-Ouest**, on the island of Tahiti. The commune of Taiarapu-Ouest contains three associated communes — Teahupoʻo, Toahotu, and Vairao — and its administrative seat is Vairao. ([Wikipedia: Taiarapu-Ouest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiarapu-Ouest))
- **Peninsula:** Tahiti Iti ("small Tahiti"), also called the Taiarapu peninsula, the southeastern lobe of Tahiti. Tahiti Iti is split between two communes — Taiarapu-Ouest (south/west) and Taiarapu-Est (north/east). ([Wikipedia: Taiarapu-Est](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiarapu-Est))
- **Coordinates of village:** 17°50′50″S, 149°16′02″W (Wikipedia infobox); decimal ≈ −17.84645, −149.26682. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teahupo%CA%BBo); [Latitude.to](https://latitude.to/articles-by-country/pf/french-polynesia/9261/teahupoo))
- **Coordinates of the wave / Passe Havae:** ~½ mile (≈800 m) offshore from the village, west-southwest. Exact takeoff coordinates **uncertain — needs verification** beyond the village latlon.
- **Distance from Papeete:** ~70 km by road; commonly described as a 1.5-hour drive. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teahupo%CA%BBo))
- **End-of-the-road / PK status:** the south-coast road of Tahiti Iti terminates at Teahupoʻo; beyond it the coast (the *Fenua ʻAihere*) is reachable only by foot or boat. The north-coast road terminates at Tautira. The "PK 0" / end-of-the-road framing is ubiquitous in surf media. ([GoNomad — Tahiti Iti: The Forgotten Island](https://www.gonomad.com/1285-tahiti-iti-the-forgotten-island))
- **Population:** 1,455 residents at the 2022 census (figure attributed to Wikipedia infobox citing French census data). ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teahupo%CA%BBo))

> **Note for the page:** Pueu is *not* the host commune for Teahupoʻo. Pueu is on the **east** side of Tahiti Iti, in Taiarapu-Est, on the road toward Tautira. Earlier briefing materials that conflated Pueu with Teahupoʻo are wrong; the right pairing is **Vairao (commune seat) → Teahupoʻo (associated commune)**.

## 3. Bathymetry & wave physics

Teahupoʻo breaks where deep ocean meets a near-vertical reef shelf with no continental shelf to slow the swell.

- **Open-ocean approach:** the seafloor drops to >1,000 ft within ⅓ mile of shore and >1 mile deep within 3 miles offshore; swells don't "feel" bottom until ~½ mile out. ([Surfline — Mechanics of Teahupoo](https://www.surfline.com/surf-news/mechanics-of-teahupoo-tahiti-surf-worlds-heaviest-wave/31797))
- **Reef-shelf gradient:** the shelf rises from ~150 ft depth at roughly a 1:1 vertical-to-horizontal slope. ([Surfline](https://www.surfline.com/surf-news/mechanics-of-teahupoo-tahiti-surf-worlds-heaviest-wave/31797))
- **Takeoff zone depth:** at the takeoff, water is in the 10 m range over a flatter "stand-up" shelf; the wave then breaks over a final reef rise where the trough can be **less than 2 m (≈6 ft) of water** under a 15+ m face. ([Surfline](https://www.surfline.com/surf-news/mechanics-of-teahupoo-tahiti-surf-worlds-heaviest-wave/31797))
- **Reef depth at the lip impact zone:** as little as ~20 in (51 cm) under the breaking lip on the inside reef. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teahupo%CA%BBo))
- **Why it folds the way it does:** because the depth changes faster than the wave can shoal normally, the wave stays "linear" — too much water remains in the crest (above sea level) and the trough scoops out below it, producing the famous below-sea-level square-lip cylinder rather than a triangular peak. ([Surfline](https://www.surfline.com/surf-news/mechanics-of-teahupoo-tahiti-surf-worlds-heaviest-wave/31797); [The Conversation — Anatomy of a wave](https://theconversation.com/anatomy-of-a-wave-what-makes-the-olympic-surf-break-at-teahupoo-unique-and-so-challenging-235301))
- **Channel:** a deep channel runs alongside the reef shelf; the wave does not break in the channel, which is what allows it to peel left (looking at shore) and what keeps photographer/safety boats safe. ([Surfline](https://www.surfline.com/surf-news/mechanics-of-teahupoo-tahiti-surf-worlds-heaviest-wave/31797))
- **Swell source:** primarily Southern Hemisphere groundswells from "Roaring Forties" storms south of New Zealand and in the Southern Ocean. ([SurferToday — 31 things you didn't know about Teahupoo](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/things-you-didnt-know-about-teahupoo))
- **Optimal wind:** light easterly / east-southeast trade is the lighting-up wind (cross-offshore on the left). Specific wind direction nuance — **partly uncertain**; surf media variously describe ESE as ideal.

## 4. Named breaks & features

- **Reef pass:** **Passe Havae** (also rendered "Pass de Havae" / "Havae Pass"). The pass is a freshwater-cut channel through the barrier reef, eroded over millennia by streams draining the ~4,000-ft peaks of Tahiti Iti. ([SchoonerMan — Havae Pass](https://www.schoonerman.com/tahiti/havae-pass.html/); [Surfline](https://www.surfline.com/surf-news/mechanics-of-teahupoo-tahiti-surf-worlds-heaviest-wave/31797))
- **"The End of the Road":** an early nickname for the spot, referring literally to where the village road ends. ([SurferToday](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/the-history-of-the-teahupoo-waves-and-surf-break))
- **Wave sections:** surf media reference a **"bowl section"** (the heaviest, thickest part of the wave on big days) and the inside reef close-out. A formally-named "End Bowl" or "Roaring Forties section" inside the wave itself is **not confirmed by sources**; "Roaring Forties" is consistently used to name the *swell source*, not a wave section. ([SurferToday — 31 things](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/things-you-didnt-know-about-teahupoo))

## 5. Brice Taerea — fatal wipeout, 2000 (not 1996)

**Date:** 27 April 2000, one week before the Gotcha Tahiti Pro. *(The 1996 date in the brief appears to be incorrect — every surf-media source places his death in 2000.)* ([SurferToday — Notable deaths in surfing, summary in search results](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/the-most-notable-deaths-in-surfing); [Wikipedia — Teahupoʻo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teahupo%CA%BBo))

**Who he was:** Tahitian local surfer, familiar with Teahupoʻo. Specific bio details (age, sponsorship history, family) **uncertain — needs verification**.

**What happened:** caught inside on a 12-ft set, attempted to duck-dive a wave, was thrown over the falls, landed headfirst on the reef. Suffered two broken cervical vertebrae and a severed spinal cord. Recovered from the water alive; remained in a coma — sources variously say "two days" or "two weeks" — before dying in hospital. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teahupo%CA%BBo); [SurferToday — Notable deaths](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/the-most-notable-deaths-in-surfing))

**Significance:** Wikipedia and most surf-media accounts treat Taerea as the **only confirmed surfing death at Teahupoʻo to date**. Quote attributions in surf media should be checked individually before publishing — no clean, sourced quote was located in this research pass.

## 6. Laird Hamilton's "Millennium Wave" — 17 August 2000

**Date:** 17 August 2000, ~11:38 am local. ([SurferToday — What is the Millennium Wave?](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/what-is-the-millennium-wave))

**Mode:** tow-in, on his backside (left-foot-forward, riding the left). ([SurferToday](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/what-is-the-millennium-wave); [The Inertia — Millennium Wave Reshapes How the World Looks at Surfing](https://www.theinertia.com/surf/laird-hamiltons-millennium-wave-reshapes-how-the-world-looks-at-surfing/))

**Photographer & water cinematographer:** **Tim McKenna** organized the trip and shot the iconic stills. McKenna's own retrospective and the Laird Hamilton store both confirm the date and the McKenna provenance. ([Tim McKenna on Facebook — 25 years ago retrospective](https://www.facebook.com/TimMcKennaPhotography/posts/25-years-ago-on-august-17-th-2000-lairdhamilton-rode-the-millennium-wave-at-the-/1323701999111747/); [Laird Hamilton store — Millennium Wave print](https://shop.lairdhamilton.com/products/photo-laird-hamilton-millenium-teahupoo-wave-2000-oh-my-god))

**Why it changed Teahupoʻo's reputation:** the still and footage of Hamilton in the cylinder were widely treated as the moment surfing's "what is possible" ceiling reset. The image dominated the magazine cycle; **the *Surfer* magazine cover often referenced for this wave is the November 2000 issue with the headline "Oh my God…"** — though **the exact issue/cover line should be verified against the magazine archive before publishing.** ([The Inertia](https://www.theinertia.com/surf/laird-hamiltons-millennium-wave-reshapes-how-the-world-looks-at-surfing/); [Oxbow — Millenium Wave](https://www.oxbowshop.com/en/blog/ocean/millenium-wave-laird-hamilton))

**Reported quote (do not present as direct):** Tim McKenna told *SurferToday*, "Laird's performance was mind-blowing." ([SurferToday — What is the Millennium Wave?](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/what-is-the-millennium-wave))

([Atlantik Surf](https://atlantiksurf.com/en/teahupoo-winners-in-history/); [SurferToday — Andy Irons 2010](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/andy-irons-wins-the-2010-billabong-pro-tahiti))

([Atlantik Surf](https://atlantiksurf.com/en/teahupoo-winners-in-history/))

([WSL — Vahine Fierro Wins](https://www.worldsurfleague.com/posts/530778/vahine-fierro-wins-shiseido-tahiti-pro-presented-by-outerknown); [SurferToday — Fierro wins 2024](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/vahine-fierro-wins-and-makes-history-at-the-2024-tahiti-pro))

([Surfline — Code Red II](https://www.surfline.com/surf-news/swell-alert/code-red-ii-teahupoo-goes-xxl/156514); [The Inertia — Rastovich Code Red wave](https://www.theinertia.com/surf/dave-rastovich-surfing-teahupoo-code-red-swell-this-time-tomorrow/)) **Code Red II — 2013**, ridden during/around the Billabong Pro window. ## 8. 2024 Paris Olympics — the controversy

**Why Tahiti / why Teahupoʻo:** Paris 2024 chose Teahupoʻo as the surfing venue, ~15,716 km / 9,765 mi from Paris — a record physical distance for any Olympic medal event from the host city. Athletes were housed on the cruise ship *Aranui 5* moored offshore to limit footprint. Competition window: **27 July – 5 August 2024**. ([Wikipedia — Surfing at the 2024 Summer Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surfing_at_the_2024_Summer_Olympics))

**The judges' tower fight:**

- **What existed:** the long-standing **wooden judges' tower** used by the WSL Tahiti Pro, ~150 m² footprint, anchored to the reef.
- **What Paris 2024 originally proposed:** a new **three-story aluminum tower**, ~$5 million / €4.6 m, with air-conditioning, toilets, and capacity for ~40 people, requiring fresh foundations drilled into the reef and a barge-served construction window. ([Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/green/2024/07/31/olympic-surfing-kicks-off-in-tahiti-why-has-teahupoo-been-a-controversial-choice-of-venue); [SurferToday — Olympic surf judging tower under fire](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/olympic-surf-judging-tower-under-fire-at-teahupoo))
- **Reef-damage incident:** **December 2023**, a barge mobilizing equipment for the new tower **snagged and broke coral** at the construction site, prompting Paris 2024 to suspend work. ([CNN — Aluminum tower construction paused after barge damages coral](https://www.cnn.com/2023/12/07/sport/olympic-tahiti-surf-environmental-damage-intl-spt/index.html))
- **Scientific assessment (pre-build):** the **MEGA Lab** (a Hawaii-based marine science nonprofit, working with University of Hawaiʻi researchers and the local association *Vai Ara o Teahupoʻo*) used 3D photogrammetry to baseline the reef. In the ~322 m² tower footprint they identified **1,003 coral colonies across 20 species**. They warned dredging and construction could affect up to **2,500 m²** of reef and result in at least **~$1.3 m USD** in direct ecosystem damage. ([Hawaiʻi Public Radio — Scientists anticipate costly damage](https://www.hawaiipublicradio.org/local-news/2024-03-20/scientists-anticipate-costly-damage-to-reef-below-teahupoo-olympic-tower); [University of Hawaiʻi News](https://www.hawaii.edu/news/2024/03/13/olympic-construction-teahupoo/); published in *Remote Sensing*)

**Local opposition — actual people and groups:**

- **Vai Ara o Teahupoʻo** — the principal community association opposing the tower as proposed. President: **Cindy Otcenasek**. ([TNTV News — Tour des juges](https://www.tntvnews.pf/polynesie/societe/tour-des-juges-ils-sont-ou-ceux-qui-sont-censes-proteger-lenvironnement-lance-cindy-otcenasek/); [France 24 — Work underway on scandal-hit Olympic surf tower](https://www.france24.com/en/live-news/20240302-work-underway-on-scandal-hit-olympic-surf-tower-in-tahiti)) *(The brief's "Vāʻavaʻa Vaʻa-i-Te-Ahupoʻo" / "Vahinerii" appear to be misremembered names; the actual organization is Vai Ara o Teahupoʻo.)*
- **Matahi Drollet** — top local Teahupoʻo surfer; led a high-visibility petition and social-media campaign. His Change.org petition (with allied campaigns) reportedly reached **200,000+ signatures**, with Drollet's specific petition cited at ~98,000 in one source. Kelly Slater publicly endorsed it. ([Surfer — Tahitian Surfers Share Petition](https://www.surfer.com/news/tahitian-surfers-petition-teahupoo-olympic-tower); [France 24 — Slater opposes Olympic surf tower](https://www.france24.com/en/live-news/20231128-surfing-great-slater-opposes-olympic-surf-tower-in-tahiti); [The Inertia — Tahitians fighting new tower](https://www.theinertia.com/surf/tahitians-olympics-teahupoo-judging-tower-protests/))
- **Hinatea Boosie** — referenced in the brief; **uncertain — needs verification**. No source surfaced this name in this pass; possible confusion with another spokesperson. Recommend dropping unless confirmed against a French-language Polynesian source (TNTV, Tahiti Infos, Polynésie la 1ère).
- **November 10, 2023 march** in Papeete against the tower. ([TNTV News — Une marche le 10 novembre](https://www.tntvnews.pf/polynesie/societe/une-marche-le-10-novembre-a-papeete-contre-la-tour-des-juges-de-teahupoo/))

**Institutional positions:**
- **ISA (International Surfing Association, then led by Fernando Aguerre):** on **19 December 2023** publicly proposed scrapping the new tower entirely and judging the event remotely from land/water/drone cameras. Proposal was **rejected by the French Polynesian government**. ([SurferToday — Paris 2024 and ISA clash](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/paris-2024-and-isa-clash-over-aluminum-olympic-judging-tower))
- **Paris 2024 + French Polynesia government:** insisted a permanent tower was needed for legacy use after the Games and pressed forward with a redesign rather than cancellation. ([France 24](https://www.france24.com/en/live-news/20240302-work-underway-on-scandal-hit-olympic-surf-tower-in-tahiti); [RNZ — Teahupoo confirmed with a lighter tower](https://www.rnz.co.nz/international/pacific-news/502840/teahupoo-confirmed-as-2024-olympic-games-venue-but-with-a-lighter-tower))

**The compromise actually built:**
- A **smaller, lighter aluminum tower** with a footprint reduced ~25 %, returning the base to roughly the original wooden tower's ~150 m². Capacity 25–30 people. Foundations were shallower than the original aluminum design but still drilled into the reef. The tower is collapsible/reusable for future events. ([Surfer — First Look at the new tower](https://www.surfer.com/news/controversial-olympic-surfing-judging-tower-built-teahupoo); [The Inertia — Tower is standing](https://www.theinertia.com/surf/teahupoos-olympic-judges-tower-is-standing/); [RNZ](https://www.rnz.co.nz/international/pacific-news/502840/teahupoo-confirmed-as-2024-olympic-games-venue-but-with-a-lighter-tower))

**Post-Games reef damage assessment:** the December 2023 barge incident is the documented physical damage event; a comprehensive published *post-Games* peer-reviewed reassessment of total reef impact **was not located in this research pass — uncertain — needs verification.** MEGA Lab's pre-Games baseline was set up specifically to enable that comparison; checking their 2024–2025 outputs is the right next step.

**Olympic gold medalists at Teahupoʻo:**
- **Men:** **Kauli Vaast (FRA)** — gold, **5 August 2024**, score 17.67. Defeated Australia's **Jack Robinson** (silver, 7.83). ([Olympics.com](https://www.olympics.com/en/news/paris-2024-surfing-men-france-kauli-vaast-gold))
- **Women:** **Caroline Marks (USA)** — gold. ([Wikipedia — Surfing at 2024 Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surfing_at_the_2024_Summer_Olympics))

## 11. Heʻe nalu / Polynesian wave-riding history at this spot

- **Polynesian wave-riding generally:** documented in Tahiti by Joseph Banks aboard HMS *Endeavour* on Cook's first voyage, journal entry **29 May 1769**, describing canoe-stern wave riding "with incredible swiftness" in Matavai Bay. ([Surfresearch.com.au — surf-riding in Tahiti, 1769 (Banks)](https://www.surfresearch.com.au/1769_Banks_Surfing_Tahiti.html); [Wikipedia — History of surfing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_surfing))
- **At Teahupoʻo specifically:** **modern surfing began in 1985**, when Tahitian **Thierry Vernaudon** and a few local friends paddled out (~15 minutes from shore) and surfed it for the first time at modest size. ([SurferToday — History of Teahupoo](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/the-history-of-the-teahupoo-waves-and-surf-break))
- **Pre-1985 Polynesian surfing of *this* break:** **uncertain — needs verification**. Surfline has published a piece titled *First Session: Teahupoo…1893?* speculating about earlier wave-riding here, but the article body could not be retrieved in this pass. The honest framing for the marquee page: Polynesians had been surfing Tahitian waves for centuries before European contact (documented 1769), but the Passe Havae break itself entered the recorded surf canon in 1985.
- **First non-Tahitians to ride it:** Hawaiian bodyboarders **Mike Stewart** and **Ben Severson** showed up the following year (~1986) and helped seed its underground reputation; in the 1990s tow-in pioneers and Kelly Slater / Tom Carroll videos took it global. ([SurferToday](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/the-history-of-the-teahupoo-waves-and-surf-break))

## 12. The village & daily life

- **Population:** 1,455 (2022). ([Wikipedia — Teahupoʻo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teahupo%CA%BBo))
- **Economy & feel:** small fishing village, single one-lane road through it, "end of the road" identity that's both literal and cultural. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teahupo%CA%BBo); [GoNomad — Tahiti Iti: The Forgotten Island](https://www.gonomad.com/1285-tahiti-iti-the-forgotten-island))
- **Drive from Papeete:** ~70 km, commonly described as 1.5 hours including the cross-peninsula transit through Taravao. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teahupo%CA%BBo))
- **Boat-only access to the wave:** the wave breaks ~½ mile offshore at Passe Havae; **you cannot see it from the village** without a boat — you can hear it on big swells from shore but the visual is reserved for those in the channel. ([Tahiti Surfari — Teahupoo Excursion Taxi Boat](https://www.tahititourisme.com/equipment/teahupoo-excursion-taxi-boat/))

## 13. The "watch from a boat" reality

- **How spectators see it:** local taxi-boat operators (Tahiti Surfari, Teahupoo Adventures, etc.) ferry visitors out to the channel adjacent to Passe Havae; spectators sit *in* the channel to watch surfers ride the left toward shore. ([Tahiti Surfari](https://www.tahitisurfari.com/en); [Teahupoo Adventures](https://www.teahupooadventures.com/))
- **Best hour:** typically morning, before the trade wind picks up; specific operator-recommended windows vary.
- **Etiquette:** the channel is a working corridor for safety boats and surf photographers; tourist boats are expected to stay clear of paddle-out lanes and not crowd the takeoff zone. Specific written code of conduct **uncertain — needs verification**.
- **Locals vs. tourists:** during contests and Code Red days, the channel is closed to non-credentialed boats; local pecking order and family-line claims to channel positions are well-known but informal.

## 15. Tahitian language & spelling conventions

- The **glottal stop** (Tahitian: *ʻeta*; functionally analogous to the Hawaiian *ʻokina*) is a true phoneme in Tahitian. The phonologically correct rendering is **Teahupoʻo** with a glottal stop between the two o's of *poʻo* ("head"). Without the *ʻeta* the pronunciation defaults to a long "oo" and the morpheme ("head") is obscured. ([SurferToday](https://www.surfertoday.com/surfing/what-does-teahupoo-mean); [Wikipedia — Tahitian language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tahitian_language))
- **Common renderings in the wild:** *Teahupoo* (no diacritic, dominant in English-language surf media and Olympic broadcast graphics); *Teahupo'o* (ASCII apostrophe); *Teahupoʻo* (proper *ʻeta*, used by Wikipedia and increasingly by Polynesian-language preserving outlets); occasionally *Te ahu poʻo* (full morphemic split, non-standard). ([Wikipedia — Teahupoʻo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teahupo%CA%BBo))
- **Government / Académie tahitienne preferred form:** the **Académie tahitienne (Fare Vānaʻa)** preserves the *ʻeta* in standard orthography. The official Tahiti Tourisme and government Polynesian-language pages have moved toward **Teahupoʻo** with diacritic, though signage on the ground still uses both forms. **Specific Académie style ruling — uncertain — needs verification** against a Fare Vānaʻa publication.
