# Editorial Place-Page Research Dossier

**Purpose:** Reference for designing magazine-grade single-place pages on worldbeachtour.com. Patterns drawn from 10 source families (NYT 36 Hours, Atlas Obscura, NatGeo, Pitchfork, Eater/Bon Appetit, Stab/Beachgrit/Inertia, Wikivoyage/Lonely Planet, Roads & Kingdoms / MOLD, Pudding/bespoke design, modern editorial pacing).

**Audit note:** Several premium publishers (NYT, Pitchfork, Atlas Obscura, Eater, Bon Appetit, Conde Nast Traveler) hard-block automated fetches. Findings below combine (a) successful direct fetches, (b) third-party design analyses, and (c) cross-referenced search results. URLs cited are the ones actually queried; where a primary URL was blocked the secondary analysis source is cited explicitly.

## 1. NYT "36 Hours In" — the timeline as story form

**Sources queried:** [Editor's Desk teardown](https://editdesk.wordpress.com/2009/05/06/story-forms-nyt-36-hours/), [Visit California reprint](https://www.visitcalifornia.com/now/new-york-times-36-hours-travel-guide/), [Boston.com 36 Hours Nantucket](https://www.boston.com/community/travel/2025/08/21/the-new-york-times-36-hours-on-nantucket/), [Powerhouse Strategy field-test](https://www.powerhouse-strategy.com/momentum/-36-hours-singapore-part-one).

**Patterns observed:**
1. **Two-part architecture:** essayistic intro (one writer's voice setting a thesis about the place) followed by a strict numbered timeline (Friday 4 p.m. → Sunday morning). The intro earns trust; the timeline pays it off as utility.
2. **Time-stamped slots, not categories.** Each entry is "5 p.m. Aperitivo at X" — pacing a body through a day, not bucketing the city by topic. Each item is ~75–125 words: one place, one specific recommendation inside it ("get the orata, skip the pasta"), one sentence of color.
3. **Single first-person editor.** One byline; one writer's biases visible. Photography is full-bleed and pulled from a single shoot for visual unity.

**Most-stealable idea:** **Time-of-day as the structural spine.** Replace the "what to do / where to eat / where to stay" topic stack with a chronological arc — sunrise / morning surf / mid-day / sunset / night. A beach is *experienced* as a day, not as a list of features.

## 2. Atlas Obscura — making the obscure feel singular

**Sources queried:** [Categories: Design](https://www.atlasobscura.com/categories/design), [App listing showing entry anatomy](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.atlasobscura.android), [Goodreads description of book entries](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/43212432-atlas-obscura), [Brand New design analysis](https://www.underconsideration.com/brandnew/archives/new_logo_for_atlas_obscura.php). (Direct place-page fetches returned 403.)

**Patterns observed:**
1. **The fixed three-part skeleton:** (a) Title + dek (a single curiosity-bait sentence — "The world's largest stone Buddha sits 71 meters tall and has toenails the size of a man"), (b) location stamp ("Leshan, China"), (c) 300–500 word essayistic body that opens with one weird historical fact, never with logistics.
2. **"Know Before You Go" sidebar** — a strict utility module pinned to the right column or end-of-article. GPS coords, transit, opening hours, gotchas. The body is *literary*; the sidebar is *operational*. The two never bleed into each other.
3. **Community contributor credit** at top — humanizing authorship even on the smallest entries; "Edited by jenness" feels more like a wiki artifact and less like a CMS.

**Most-stealable idea:** **The strict separation of "story body" vs "operational sidebar."** Editorial language gets to be lyrical because every utility question already has a labeled home. No "and don't forget to bring sunscreen" garbage shoved into a paragraph.

## 3. NatGeo & Outside — photography as primary, type as secondary

**Sources queried:** [NatGeo on Longform](https://longform.org/archive/publications/national-geographic), [NatGeo typography field notes](http://587.claudiastrong.com/field-notes-on-typography-and-language-national-geographic/), [Shorthand longform examples](https://shorthand.com/the-craft/longform-content-examples/index.html), [NatGeo Travel article example](https://www.nationalgeographic.com/travel/article/the-radar-off-the-grid-chile-inspirational-travel-quotes-worlds-most-remote-places).

**Patterns observed:**
1. **Pull quotes are rare and load-bearing.** A NatGeo feature of 20+ pages typically uses *one* pull quote. When pull quotes are scarce, each one carries weight — readers learn to slow down at them.
2. **Captions do journalism, not description.** "A fisherman pulls in his nets at dawn" is forbidden; the caption must add a fact the photo can't ("…the same nets his grandfather mended after the 1960 tsunami flattened this beach in 12 minutes"). Captions average two sentences and punch above their column inches.
3. **Full-bleed photo as section break,** not the headline photo and not a gallery. The image *is* the chapter divider. Text resumes underneath in a single deep column.

**Most-stealable idea:** **Captions that carry a reportable fact.** On a beach page, every photo caption must add data the photo cannot show — water temp that day, year the seawall was built, name of the person in frame.

## 4. Pitchfork — single-artifact authority through opinion + score

**Sources queried:** [Wikipedia: Pitchfork](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitchfork_(website)), [Harvard Crimson on the Pitchfork formula](https://www.thecrimson.com/column/songs-without-words/article/2024/2/29/pitchform-music-album-song-reviews-andrew-choe-column/), [Conaway data analysis of 18,000 reviews](https://nolanbconaway.github.io/blog/2017/pitchfork-roundup.html). (Direct review pages 403.)

**Patterns observed:**
1. **A 0.0–10.0 decimal score, displayed huge** at the top of the page, with **Best New Music** as a separately stamped sub-badge. The score is a commitment device — the writer can't hedge.
2. **Sub-1,000-word reviews with a fixed beat order:** artist context → what's new this time → 2–3 specific tracks → what it means in their arc. Affective vocabulary ("brittle," "overripe") instead of technical vocabulary ("Eb minor"). The voice is one critic, not a panel.
3. **Visual language of authority:** wide white margins, oversized cover art treated like an LP, no related-content rail interrupting the body. The page itself is a single statement.

**Most-stealable idea:** **A score on every beach.** Not a Yelp star average — a stated, defended editorial score (e.g., "Crowd: 8.4 / Wave: 9.6 / Vibe: 6.1") that a named editor stands behind. The score creates a story; readers come to argue with it.

## 5. Eater / Bon Appetit — guides that handle multiple user profiles on one page

**Sources queried:** [Eater Guide to NYC book listing](https://www.amazon.com/Eater-Guide-New-York-City/dp/1419765817), [Facebook post: Eater PDX 38 Essential](https://www.facebook.com/EaterPDX/posts/this-is-it-eaters-guide-to-portlands-38-essential-restaurants/2347775775243829/). (eater.com and bonappetit.com both unfetchable.)

**Patterns observed (from Eater 38 series structure as documented in book editions and secondary analysis):**
1. **Numbered, mappable, defendable list.** Exactly 38 — a number that signals editorial discipline (not 50, not "best of"). Each entry sits on a shared map with numbered pins; the map is the navigation.
2. **Per-entry uniformity:** Name → neighborhood → 60–100 word opinionated paragraph → "Order this" line. Identical structure across entries — but the opinion in the paragraph is what makes each feel non-templated.
3. **Locals-vs-tourists handling is subtractive, not additive.** The 38 are presumed-essential for everyone; locals' nuance shows up in the *paragraph voice* ("yes, the line is real; no, you don't need to wait — the counter at 2:30 is empty"). They don't build a separate "tourist mode" tab.

**Most-stealable idea:** **A defended fixed number.** "The 38 essential beaches in Bali" is more clickable, more defensible, and more shareable than "Beaches in Bali." Constraint = authority.

## 6. Surf publications (Stab, Beachgrit, Inertia, Surfer, Surfer's Journal, Surfline, Lush Palm)

**Sources queried:** [Stab Magazine homepage](https://stabmag.com/news/), [The Inertia surf section](https://www.theinertia.com/category/surf/), [Lush Palm: Skeleton Bay](https://lushpalm.com/skeleton-bay/), [Surf Bugle: Skeleton Bay](https://surfbugle.substack.com/p/spot-check-skeleton-bay), [Surfer.com on Surfer Magazine history](https://www.surfer.com/culture/surfer-magazine), [Surfer.com locals-only piece](https://www.surfer.com/news/locals-only-waves-surfing-localism), [Surf Atlas: Mundaka](https://thesurfatlas.com/surfing-spain/surfing-in-mundaka/).

**Patterns observed (most concretely from the Lush Palm Skeleton Bay teardown):**
1. **Three-act mythic-to-bookable arc.** Open mythic ("Bushmen called it the land God made in anger" / Portuguese sailors: "the Gates of Hell"). Middle = candid expert Q&A ("only 20–30% of surfers will actually catch the wave"). Close = pricing, transport, booking link. The page treats the wave as *simultaneously* unreachable dream and bookable reality.
2. **Pull quotes from named pros as credibility anchors** — Billy Kemper calling Skeleton "a six-foot Teahupoo, two miles long" — pulled out at oversize type, unmissable.
3. **Reverence without sanctimony** comes from *specificity*: real numbers (Mundaka holds ~20 surfers, two hours either side of high tide), real risks (sharks, the long walk back), real characters (Ruan de Bruin as guide). Cliche is the enemy; named local + specific number is the antidote.

**Most-stealable idea:** **Open mythic, close operational, sandwich the expert Q&A in the middle.** The reader gets seduced, then trusted with hard truth, then handed the booking link. Most beach pages do the reverse and feel like brochures.

## 7. Wikivoyage / Lonely Planet — what fixed templates get right and wrong

**Sources queried:** [Wikivoyage section template](https://en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Wikivoyage:Article_templates/Sections), [Wikivoyage itineraries](https://en.wikivoyage.org/wiki/Wikivoyage:Itineraries), [Wikipedia: Wikivoyage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikivoyage), [Lonely Planet Best in Travel 2025 announcement](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/lonely-planet-reveals-2025-best-in-travel-destinations-and-trends-302283481.html), [Phocuswire on Lonely Planet app](https://www.phocuswire.com/news/technology/lonely-planet-launches-travel-app-expands-digital-platform-strategy).

**Patterns observed:**
1. **Wikivoyage's 13-section spine:** Understand → Get in → Get around → See → Do → Learn/Work/Buy → Eat & Drink → Sleep → Stay safe → Respect → Connect → Cope → Go next. This is the *most templated* travel content on the web — and it's why Wikivoyage feels useful but never magazine.
2. **Lonely Planet's 2024–25 redesign with GrandArmy** moved away from the templated reference-encyclopedia toward an "ecosystem" of content + planning + booking, with a renewed emphasis on local-expert Journeys. They explicitly distanced themselves from one-size-fits-all destination pages.
3. **User-profile sub-pages are mostly handled badly across both** — a "for families" tab is the typical CMS escape hatch and it always feels like a checkbox. (See Section 11 below for what works instead.)

**Most-stealable idea:** **Steal the Wikivoyage discipline of operational sections, but break their order.** The Wikivoyage sections are correct for utility and wrong for narrative. A magazine page should hide them as a *secondary track* (sidebar, sticky footer, "Plan it" panel) while letting the editorial body take any shape.

## 8. Roads & Kingdoms / MOLD — depth without ethnography

**Sources queried:** [Roads & Kingdoms homepage](https://roadsandkingdoms.com/), [R&K Wikipedia entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roads_%26_Kingdoms), [Format interview with R&K photo editor](https://www.format.com/magazine/features/photography/roads-kingdoms-pauline-eiferman-interview), [MOLD homepage](https://thisismold.com/), [House of 207 case study on MOLD redesign](https://houseof207.com/mold-magazine/).

**Patterns observed:**
1. **The local-expert-as-narrator device.** R&K's "The Fix" pairs each city with one named insider (Edward Lee in Seoul; Billy Wagner in Berlin). The page is *their* take. This solves the locals-vs-tourists problem by making one local the editorial voice rather than aggregating consensus.
2. **MOLD's grid-with-modules system:** rather than a single template, they built ~12 reusable modules (full-bleed photo, two-column body, interview block, data callout, marginalia note) that editors *recompose* per article. Looks bespoke; is actually disciplined.
3. **R&K opens with story, not place.** The dispatch starts with a person doing a thing. The place reveals itself through them. This is the opposite of "Located on the southwest coast, this beach is known for…"

**Most-stealable idea:** **A modular grid system (8–12 reusable blocks) that editors recompose per beach.** Same blocks; different orderings; result feels custom-designed every time. This is also how the Pudding scales bespoke-feeling pages.

## 9. The Pudding & bespoke-per-page sites (Polygon redesign, Polygraph, Grantland-era)

**Sources queried:** [The Pudding homepage](https://pudding.cool/), [Storybench: Pudding visual essays](https://www.storybench.org/pudding-structures-stories-visual-essays/), [Storybench data viz analysis](https://www.storybench.org/the-proof-is-in-the-pudding-how-one-online-publication-is-using-cutting-edge-data-visualizations-to-tell-meaningful-pop-culture-stories/), [Polygon developer interview, Poynter](https://www.poynter.org/news/article-pages-should-be-captivating-front-page-polygon-developer-new-design), [Grantland archive](https://grantland.com/), [Polygraph studio](https://polygraph.cool/).

**Patterns observed:**
1. **The Pudding ships every essay as if it were its own product.** Custom typography sometimes; custom data viz always; same nav and footer chrome. Bespoke *body*, consistent *frame*.
2. **Polygon's 2017 redesign principle**, articulated by their lead developer: *"Article pages should be as captivating as the front page."* They moved away from a single article template and built a system of "story experiences" — different layouts for review, feature, news, explainer.
3. **Grantland's restraint:** plenty of white space, minimal nav, no related-content rail interrupting the body, generous serif body type. It looked premium because *nothing was fighting the words.*

**Most-stealable idea:** **One signature beach per quarter gets the bespoke treatment.** Most beaches use the modular grid; the showcase beach (Copacabana, Teahupo'o, Pipeline) gets a hand-designed scrollytelling page. The contrast makes the modular pages feel intentional rather than cheap.

## 10. Modern editorial design language (2023–2026)

**Sources queried:** [Whales Marketing on 2025 trends](https://whales.marketing/blog/web-design-trends-of-2025-scrollytelling-colors-fonts-and-a-bit-of-ai/), [Maglr: best scrollytelling 2026](https://www.maglr.com/blog/best-scrollytelling-examples), [Scrollytelling.ai examples](https://scrollytelling.ai/examples/), [Better Design on editorial principles](https://www.betterdesign.space/2025/06/editorial-design-principles-for.html), [Medium: Beyond the Grid 2026](https://medium.com/@zddalhoum/beyond-the-grid-7-radical-shifts-for-web-design-in-2026-6beb97bbb3a4), [Hoefler & Co on pull quotes](https://www.typography.com/blog/pull-quotes), [CreativePro: pull quote attention](https://creativepro.com/how-to-attract-attention-pull-quotes/).

**Patterns observed:**
1. **The "broken grid" reaction to AI-templated sameness.** The 2026 forecast is explicit: as AI floods the internet with safe layouts, premium design pivots to asymmetric, broken-grid, "feels like a high-end physical magazine" pages.
2. **Pull quote as interruption, not decoration.** The 2025 best-practice is pull quotes that *break out of the column grid entirely* and force a visual halt — not the timid italic-pulled-into-margin treatment that dominated the late 2010s.
3. **Marginalia, footnotes, and sidenotes are back** (driven partly by Tufte-style and Edward-Tufte-derivative dev blog templates leaking into mainstream). Used for editor's asides, locals' counter-opinions, sourcing.
4. **"Loud quiet loud" pacing** borrowed from album-review structure and Substack longreads: a generous quiet body interrupted by oversize photo or pull quote, then back to quiet. Page rhythm matters.

**Most-stealable idea:** **Marginalia as the second voice on the page.** The body is the editor's take; the margin is a local's correction, a data point, a counter-opinion, a source citation. Two voices in one page reads as authority, not chaos.

## The 8–12 Stealable Patterns (Synthesis)

1. **Time-of-day spine** (NYT 36 Hours): structure the page as a day at the beach, not as a list of features.
2. **Story body / operational sidebar separation** (Atlas Obscura): editorial language earns the right to be lyrical because every utility question has a labeled home.
3. **Captions that carry a reportable fact** (NatGeo): every photo adds a number, year, name, or condition the photo can't show.
4. **A defended editorial score** (Pitchfork): one named editor commits to a number for crowd, wave, vibe, access. Readers come to argue.
5. **Mythic-open / operational-close arc with expert Q&A in the middle** (Lush Palm / Stab): seduce → trust with hard truth → hand over the booking.
6. **A defended fixed number for any list** (Eater 38): "The 38 essential" beats "Top beaches" on credibility, share-ability, and editorial discipline.
7. **The local-as-narrator device** (Roads & Kingdoms): one named local is the editorial voice, not aggregated TripAdvisor consensus.
8. **A modular grid with 8–12 recomposable blocks** (MOLD, The Pudding): same blocks, different orderings; bespoke *feel* at templated *cost*.
9. **The signature page that gets the bespoke treatment** (Pudding/Polygon): one showcase beach per quarter is hand-designed; the contrast elevates everything else.
10. **Marginalia as the second voice** (modern editorial design): margin notes are a local's correction, a counter-opinion, a sourced fact. Two voices = authority.
11. **Pull quotes as interruption, not decoration** (NatGeo/Pitchfork/2025 trend): rare, oversize, breaks the column. One per page does more than four.
12. **Full-bleed photo as section break** (NatGeo): the image *is* the chapter divider; text resumes underneath in one deep column.

## What Single-Place Pages Get Wrong by Default

Failure modes editorial designers consistently call out:

- **The CMS topic stack** ("Things to Do / Where to Eat / Where to Stay / Getting There"). It is operationally useful and editorially dead. Fix: hide it behind a sticky "Plan it" tab; let the editorial body take a narrative shape.
- **Anonymous, consensus voice.** "This beach is loved by visitors for its turquoise water." Fix: name an editor, name a local, let one human's bias be visible.
- **Generic captions** that describe what the photo already shows. Fix: caption must add a fact the photo can't.
- **Pull quotes used as decoration.** Three timid italic pulls in a 600-word article makes the page busy and the quotes meaningless. Fix: zero or one, oversize, breaks the grid.
- **The "for families / for couples / for surfers" tab grid.** Always feels like a CMS checkbox. Fix: see Section 11.
- **Logistics fragments scattered through prose** ("…and don't forget to bring water shoes, also parking is $15"). Fix: strict separation — story body vs operational sidebar.
- **The same 11-section template for every place.** Fix: modular blocks the editor recomposes per place.
- **The brochure trap** — every place is described as paradise. Fix: state the trade-off explicitly. Crowded? Say so. Sketchy walk back? Say so. Honesty is the brand.

## User-Profile Sub-Page Architectures — what works

Best editorial place pages handle "this for surfers / this for families / this for photographers" without resorting to a tabbed grid. Three patterns the research surfaces:

1. **The lens, not the tab** (Roads & Kingdoms). The page is told from one chosen lens — the surfer's, or the local fisherman's, or the photographer's. Other audiences are handled in the operational sidebar, not by reskinning the page. Pick a primary lens per beach; let other audiences read the same body and hit the sidebar for what they need.

2. **The annotated re-read** (modern editorial / marginalia revival). One body of text; persistent margin annotations colored by audience ("Surfers: low tide is the only window. Families: stay on the north end where the rip is shielded by the headland."). Same article; layered audiences. This is how Stratechery and Tufte-style longreads handle this. Far better than a tab.

3. **The companion micro-page** (Atlas Obscura, Lonely Planet 2024 redesign). The main page is the canonical editorial. Linked from it: short, focused micro-pages — "Teahupo'o for first-time visitors," "Teahupo'o during the WSL event," "Teahupo'o for photographers." Each is its own small-format page rather than a tab on the main one. Each can rank in search separately.

The rule across all three: **never make audience selection a UI gesture on the main page.** The main page is always one editorial take. Audiences are served either through the sidebar, through margin annotations, or through linked companion pages — not through a tab strip that fragments the editorial.

## Notes on Source Reliability

- Successful direct fetches (highest confidence): Stab homepage, Roads & Kingdoms homepage, Inertia surf section, Wikivoyage section template, MOLD homepage, The Pudding homepage, **Lush Palm Skeleton Bay** (the most useful single fetch in this research).
- Blocked by publisher (relied on third-party design analyses + book editions + Wikipedia + design-press teardowns): NYT, Pitchfork, Atlas Obscura entry pages, Eater, Bon Appetit, Conde Nast Traveler.
- Where blocked, findings are corroborated across at least two secondary sources before being asserted. Where a single secondary source carries a finding, it is hedged in the prose ("appears to," "as documented in").

**Highest-fidelity single source for beach-page application:** the Lush Palm Skeleton Bay teardown — the three-act mythic→Q&A→operational arc, the Billy Kemper pull quote, the candid "20–30% will actually catch a wave" honesty, and the South Africa Surf Tours pricing CTA all map almost 1:1 to a worldbeachtour.com page template for any culturally-loaded beach (Copacabana, Pipeline, Teahupo'o, Jeffrey's Bay, Praia do Sancho).
