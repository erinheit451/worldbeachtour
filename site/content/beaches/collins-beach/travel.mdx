# Collins Beach Travel Guide

Collins Beach sits on Sauvie Island, Oregon's largest island, in a landscape where farmland, wildlife refuge, and river meet. Located 25 kilometers northwest of Portland, this freshwater beach on the Columbia River is one of the Pacific Northwest's most distinctive destinations—but also one that requires specific knowledge to access and enjoy responsibly. The island itself is partially agricultural and partially protected wildlife habitat; Collins Beach sits at the intersection of these uses, accessible to the public but managed under specific rules and customs.

## Getting There

### By Air

Most visitors fly into **Portland International Airport (PDX)**, located 26.9 kilometers southeast of Collins Beach—approximately 40–50 minutes driving time. This is the region's primary airport with flights across North America and internationally.

From PDX, the drive to Collins Beach follows straightforward regional highways: take I-5 North toward Washington, then exit onto US-30 North heading toward Longview/St. Helens. Continue on US-30 as it approaches Sauvie Island from the south and east. The highway passes through St. Helens before reaching the Sauvie Island Bridge, which connects the island to the mainland.

### By Car

**From Portland downtown** (15 miles): Follow US-30 Northwest directly, crossing the Sauvie Island Bridge; plan 35–45 minutes in normal traffic.

**From Seattle** (175 miles): Take I-5 South toward Portland, exit to US-30 heading toward Longview; plan 3–3.5 hours.

**From Vancouver, BC** (280 miles): Cross the border at Blaine/Bellingham, continue south on I-5, then exit to US-30 North; plan 5–5.5 hours.

**Driving on the island:** Once across the bridge, US-30 continues north through farmland. To reach Collins Beach, take Reeder Road north from the main US-30 corridor. The road passes through agricultural areas and becomes progressively more rural. Follow signs toward the northern end of the island where the wildlife refuge parking areas are located.

**Parking:** Parking at Collins Beach requires a valid **Sauvie Island Wildlife Area Parking Permit**, mandatory for all vehicles. These permits are inexpensive annual or daily passes issued by Oregon Department of Fish and Wildlife. They can be purchased at local sporting goods stores, ODFW offices, or online through the agency. Display your permit on your vehicle before parking.

### By Public Transit

Public transit to Collins Beach is limited and requires careful planning. **TriMet**, Portland's transit system, operates bus routes serving outer northeast Portland, but service to Sauvie Island itself is minimal. The most practical approach is:

1. Take TriMet light rail (MAX) or bus to outer northeast Portland stations
2. Transfer to a bus serving the north Portland/Sauvie Island corridor (service is infrequent)
3. Plan for 60–90 minutes total travel time from downtown Portland

Most visitors find a rental car more practical for the island's rural geography.

### By Boat

Water access exists for those arriving by private vessel. The Columbia River is navigable, and several boat launch areas serve the island, though they are typically oriented toward fishing rather than beach recreation. Contact ODFW for current launch conditions and tidal information.

## Best Time to Visit

### Seasonal Overview

**Summer (July–September)** is peak season. Daytime temperatures reach 78–88°F (26–31°C), with long daylight hours and minimal rain. Water temperatures warm to around 65–70°F (18–21°C), the most comfortable for swimming. Crowds increase in July and August, but September remains pleasant with declining visitor numbers.

**Spring (April–June)** offers mild weather (60–75°F/15–24°C), occasional rain, and active bird migration. The island's agricultural areas bloom; wildlife viewing is excellent. Water remains cool (55–65°F/13–18°C).

**Fall (September–October)** mirrors spring with comfortable air temperatures, fewer crowds, and excellent wildlife viewing as migratory birds arrive. September maintains summer warmth while October brings cooler conditions and more clouds.

**Winter (November–March)** is cool (40–50°F/4–10°C), wet, and quiet. The island becomes a haven for waterfowl; bird watching peaks during this season. Water temperatures drop below 45°F (7°C). Most recreational visitors avoid winter, making it a genuine solitude season.

### Recommended Windows

**July–September** for swimming and warm-weather recreation. **May and October** for wildlife viewing and photography with smaller crowds. **November–February** for birdwatchers and those seeking complete solitude. **April and June** for transitional weather, mixed crowds, and active natural cycles.

## Where to Stay

Collins Beach itself has no lodging. The nearest options cluster in Portland and smaller communities around the island.

### Portland (15 miles east)

Portland offers the full range of hotel, vacation rental, and hostel options. Downtown Portland has sophisticated dining, nightlife, and cultural attractions. Staying in Portland means a 35–50 minute drive each way to Collins Beach, but provides far more services and social options. Budget $100–250/night for hotels, $80–150/night for vacation rentals.

### St. Helens (10 miles west)

This small timber town sits directly on US-30 between Portland and Sauvie Island. It's quieter than Portland, closer to Collins Beach (20–25 minutes), and offers modest motels and vacation rentals. Budget $70–120/night. St. Helens provides basic restaurants and services but limited nightlife.

### Longview, Washington (25 miles northwest)

Just across the Columbia River in Washington, Longview is a larger regional hub with more accommodation options and services than St. Helens. It's 30–40 minutes from Collins Beach. Budget $80–140/night for hotels.

### On-Island Rentals (Limited)

A small number of vacation rentals and private properties exist on Sauvie Island itself, offering true island immersion. These are harder to find and typically book far in advance, but staying on the island eliminates driving time. Search vacation rental platforms for "Sauvie Island" to find what's available.

## Costs

### General Price Level

**Beach access is free**, but requires a **mandatory Sauvie Island Wildlife Area Parking Permit**:
- Daily permit: $5
- Annual permit: $25–30

This permit supports ODFW habitat management and is non-negotiable for parking.

### Nearby Services

**Food**: No food vendors operate at Collins Beach itself. Pack a picnic or plan to eat in Portland (diverse options, $10–50+ per meal) or St. Helens (basic casual food, $8–18 per meal). Grocery stores on the island and in nearby towns allow you to prepare meals.

**Supplies**: The island has a small general store and gas station; plan to source supplies before arriving.

**Lodging**: $70–250/night depending on location (see Where to Stay section).

**Transportation**: Rental car approximately $40–70/day. Ride-share from Portland is possible but expensive ($20–35 each way).

**Overall daily budget**: Budget $30–50 per person for a day visit (permit + picnic), or $100–150+ if staying overnight with meals and lodging.

## Safety

### Swimming & Water Conditions

The Columbia River at Collins Beach requires specific knowledge:

- **Water temperature**: 55–70°F (13–21°C) depending on season. Wetsuits are recommended for extended periods, especially outside summer months.
- **River currents**: The Columbia is a major river with meaningful current flow. Always check current conditions before entering the water. Swimming against current is dangerous; use the buddy system.
- **No lifeguards**: Swimming is entirely at your own risk. No rescue services operate specifically at the beach.
- **Water quality**: Check **Oregon Health Authority** water quality advisories before swimming. Post-rainfall runoff can affect water safety.
- **Powerline pillars**: Large electrical transmission towers with concrete pillars mark the river; be aware of their location and stay clear of associated hazards.
- **Visibility**: River silt and algae affect water clarity. Visibility is typically limited; enter water cautiously.

**Swimming safety rules**: Always use the buddy system, avoid swimming at dawn/dusk, know your limits in current water, wear bright colors or PFDs for visibility.

### General Safety

Collins Beach and Sauvie Island are relatively safe environments. The main hazards are natural (water currents, weather) rather than crime-related. However:

- **Vehicle security**: Don't leave valuables in parked cars.
- **Wildlife**: You're in active habitat for bald eagles, Great Horned Owls, and other wildlife. Give animals space and follow ODFW guidelines.
- **Permitted uses**: The island is managed for wildlife. Respect seasonal closures, stay on designated areas, and comply with all signage.

### Special Considerations

**Clothing-optional culture**: The northern section of Collins Beach has an established clothing-optional tradition. This is a self-regulated, consensual community space. Visitors choosing to participate do so at their own discretion; others can use the southern sections. Respect personal boundaries and privacy absolutely.

**Seasonal closures**: Portions of Sauvie Island Wildlife Area close seasonally for waterfowl nesting and hunting seasons (typically fall). Check ODFW calendars before visiting to confirm access.

## Local Tips

**Get a parking permit before arrival.** Purchase online or at a nearby sporting goods store to avoid delays. You cannot park without one—it's strictly enforced.

**Pack all your food and water.** No vendors operate at the beach. Bring more water than you think you'll need, especially in summer.

**Check river conditions.** Before swimming, assess current strength and water temperature. Ask locals or check ODFW reports if you're unfamiliar with Columbia River conditions.

**Arrive early on weekends.** Summer weekends see meaningful crowds, particularly July–August. Morning visits offer better parking and quieter conditions.

**Explore Sauvie Island's other uses.** The island includes farms, u-pick operations, and pumpkin patches (particularly popular fall weekends). Combining Collins Beach with island activities makes for a complete day.

**Bring a wetsuit or water shoes.** River bottoms can be mucky; shoes protect your feet. Water temperature outside summer demands thermal protection.

**Respect wildlife viewing ethics.** Bald eagles, Great Blue Herons, and other birds are present year-round. Observe from distance, avoid loud noises, and never approach nesting areas.

**The northern end is where the clothing-optional tradition centers.** If you're unfamiliar with the culture, observe first before deciding to participate. The southern sections welcome clothed visitors of all backgrounds.

**Fall is underrated.** September and October offer warm air, cool water, fewer crowds, and excellent wildlife viewing. The island's agricultural landscape is particularly attractive during harvest season.

**Visit during weekdays if possible.** Weekday visits mean smaller crowds, easier parking, and more peaceful water access. Many people access the beach after work or during slow weeks.
