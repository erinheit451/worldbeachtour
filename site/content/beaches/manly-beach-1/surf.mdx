# Surfing Manly: The Beach Where Australian Wave-Riding Got Its Footing

Manly is an east-facing ocean beach on the Tasman Sea, and the surf is the reason it matters as much as the history. Reanalysis data (Open-Meteo Marine, ERA5, 2023-2024) puts the annual mean wave height near 1.2 metres, with a typical swell period around 9.3 seconds and regular days reaching 2 metres. The biggest month is April; the calmest is August, near 0.95 metres. That is real beach-break surf along a roughly two-kilometre strand, not the sheltered swim the ferry arrival implies.

## Reading the Beach North to South

The main beach divides into three named lengths, and they do not surf the same. **Queenscliff**, at the northern end below the headland, is the most exposed to southern swell and is often larger than the rest of the beach; offshore of the northern headland sits the Queenscliff Bombora, a bommie that bends easterly swell. **North Steyne**, in the centre, runs straighter with faster walls over banks divided by troughs and rip currents. **South Steyne**, the southern corner nearest the Corso and the ferry, is the most-serviced and most-crowded stretch and usually the most forgiving for beginners.

Around the southern headland, past Fairy Bower, are the reef and point waves: **Fairy Bower**, **Winkipop**, and **Deadmans**. Deadmans is a heavy, dangerous wave that only began to be surfed regularly from the 1990s and 2000s. These are not beginner waves and they break in a different league from the open beach.

## Why the Rips Are the Real Hazard

The same banks that build the surf build the rip currents. At Manly the rips run along the beach and strengthen toward the northern end, and they are the actual danger here, not the zero recorded shark incidents. The beach is patrolled at South Steyne and North Steyne during the Australian patrol season, and the surf-lifesaving flags mark the safest swimming. If you are not surfing, swim between the flags; if you are caught in a rip, the standard advice is to stay calm and not fight it directly back to shore.

## A Birthplace, Earned in Stages

Manly, with neighbouring Freshwater, is routinely called the birthplace of surfing in Australia, and the claim is built on a sequence of datable events rather than a single legend.

The first foundation was legal. William Gocher's deliberate daylight swim on 2 October 1902 helped force Manly Council to repeal its daylight-bathing ban on 2 November 1903, which is what made daytime ocean recreation lawful in the first place. Crowds in the surf then produced the volunteer surf-lifesaving movement that organised around 1907.

The second was the board. In November 1956, American and Hawaiian lifeguard teams in Australia for the Melbourne Olympics surf-lifesaving demonstration brought their lightweight finned balsa "Malibu" boards and rode them at Sydney's northern beaches, a turning point that pushed Australian surfing from heavy hollow boards toward the modern shortboard lineage.

The third was the contest. In May 1964, Manly hosted the first **World Surfing Championships**, where Bernard "Midget" Farrelly won the men's title and Phyllis O'Donnell the women's, before a reported 60,000 spectators on the sand.

## The World Surfing Reserve

On 10 March 2012, the **Manly-Freshwater World Surfing Reserve** was dedicated, Australia's first such reserve under the Save The Waves Coalition program launched in 2009. The dedication recognised the stretch from Manly to Freshwater as a place of global surfing significance, anchored by the history above and by the active boardriders' clubs, Queenscliff, North Steyne, Manly Malibu and others, that still run out of the beach.

## When to Surf It

The data points to autumn and winter for size: April carries the biggest mean swell, and the cooler months bring the more consistent southern groundswell that lights up Queenscliff. Summer is typically smaller and more crowded, which makes the flatter months better for learning. Whatever the season, Manly is open Tasman water under hard southern light, and the rips do not take a season off.
