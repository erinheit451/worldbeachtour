# Sandymount Strand in Ulysses: The Tidal Flat Joyce Wrote Twice

Sandymount Strand carries two of the eighteen episodes of James Joyce's *Ulysses*, published in 1922. Both are set on this exact intertidal flat on the south shore of Dublin Bay, six kilometres from the city centre. Episode three, "Proteus," puts Stephen Dedalus on the sand before eleven in the morning. Episode ten hours later in narrative time, episode thirteen, "Nausicaa," puts Leopold Bloom on the rocks at the same strand at dusk. The novel is set across a single day, 16 June 1904, and the strand is one of the few locations it returns to.

## Proteus: Stephen on the Sand

The third episode of *Ulysses* is Stephen Dedalus walking Sandymount Strand alone, before eleven o'clock, thinking. It is one of the densest passages in the book and almost entirely interior. The episode opens on the line "Ineluctable modality of the visible," and proceeds as a stream of philosophical and perceptual musing while Stephen crosses the exposed flat. The setting is not decorative: the shifting sand, the tide, and the changing surface of the strand mirror the episode's theme of flux, which is why Joyce titled it "Proteus" after the shape-changing sea god of the *Odyssey*.

The physical strand does the work. A flat that floods and drains twice a day, that bares close to a kilometre of sand at low water and is sea again at high, is a literal field of transformation. Stephen's thoughts about the "ineluctable modality" of what can be seen and heard are anchored to ground that is itself never the same from one tide to the next.

## Nausicaa: Bloom on the Rocks at Dusk

Episode thirteen returns to Sandymount Strand at twilight, around eight in the evening. Leopold Bloom, resting after a long and draining day, sits on the rocks and watches a young woman, Gertie MacDowell, at the far end of the strand. The episode is written in two contrasting registers and is set against the same shoreline that carried Stephen's morning walk. The strand frames the whole encounter: the rocks, the failing light, the bay behind.

That the novel uses one location at two different hours of its single day is part of the design. The morning strand of Proteus and the evening strand of Nausicaa are the same ground seen under different light and through two different minds, the structural rhyme reinforced by the shared setting.

## Why the Geography Holds Up

What makes Sandymount unusual among literary settings is that the place on the map is the place in the book. Joyce wrote *Ulysses* from exile but with obsessive fidelity to real Dublin geography, and the strand he set Stephen and Bloom on is the strand a reader can walk today. The promenade along Strand Road, the flat that drains toward the channel, and the low northern horizon are continuous with the 1904 of the novel. Readers and scholars use the strand as a fixed reference point, grounding the text in a place rather than an abstraction.

## Bloomsday

Each year on 16 June, the single day on which *Ulysses* is set, readers mark Bloomsday by retracing the novel's Dublin locations in readings, walks, and costume. The Sandymount Strand episodes are part of that itinerary. For the rest of the year the strand is an ordinary Dublin 4 shoreline, walked by residents from the village a short way inland, but the literary association is permanent and is the reason the flat draws visitors who would not otherwise come to a tidal mudflat.

## The Strand Beyond Joyce

The literary fame sits on top of a real working shore with its own history. A Martello tower built in 1804 stands on the Sandymount shoreline, a Napoleonic-era defence later used as a tramway office and a 1960s cafe. The Sandymount Baths, roughly 40 by 40 metres, were built on the strand in 1883, with a pier and bandstand added the following year; the pier was gone by 1920 and the concrete baths remain. In 2002 a sculpture by the Mexican artist Sebastian, *Awaiting the Mariner*, was installed on the shore, donated by President Vicente Fox. Across the bay to the north stand the Poolbeg chimneys, completed in 1971 and protected since 2014, and the Great South Wall running out to the Poolbeg Lighthouse of 1768 and 1820. The strand of the novel is also this strand: a literary site, a Ramsar wetland since 1996, and a tidal flat all at once.
