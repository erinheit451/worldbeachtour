# Baker Beach: The Bonfire That Became Burning Man

On the evening of June 21, 1986, Larry Harvey called his friend Jerry James with a proposal: build a wooden man and burn it on the beach at sunset. James built the figure — roughly eight feet of scrap lumber — in his garage that afternoon. They carried it to the north end of Baker Beach and set it on fire before about twenty people, most of them friends who had been asked to come along. Passers-by on the sand stopped and drifted toward the flames. The crowd that night was larger than the one that planned it.

## The Burn That Started It All

Harvey and James returned the next summer solstice. And the one after. Each year the figure grew taller — from eight feet to fifteen, then higher — and each year more people appeared without invitation, drawn by the fire and whatever the act of building and burning a human effigy meant to them. Harvey, who became the public voice of the event, gave varying accounts of his original motivation over the years: a cathartic break from a failed relationship, an assertion of spontaneous community, something he was still working out. What he said consistently was that he had not planned an event; he had made a gesture, and the gesture accumulated people.

By 1990 the figure had reached forty feet, the crowd had grown into the thousands, and the Golden Gate National Recreation Area rangers moved to stop it. The burn lacked a fire permit, and the beach — a half-mile of sand inside a still-active U.S. Army post — was not the place for an unpermitted bonfire of that scale. The 1990 burn was halted. The organizers found an alternative: the Black Rock Desert in Nevada, 400 miles to the northeast, where the Bureau of Land Management granted a permit for the Labor Day weekend. The festival moved there that autumn and never came back.

What grew in Nevada — Black Rock City, the ten principles, the Temple burns, the global phenomenon that now draws 70,000 people to an alkaline playa every August — traces its direct lineage to five solstice fires on this stretch of sand. The exact date of that first 1986 burn is reported inconsistently in secondary sources, with some citing June 21 and others June 22; Burning Man Project's own timeline places it on the summer solstice of 1986, and the year, the solstice, the eight-foot figure, the two builders, and the roughly twenty initial witnesses are consistent across all primary accounts.

Two features of Baker Beach made it the right place. First, the beach collects driftwood, which meant fuel. Second, in 1986 it was a marginal, semi-wild edge of an active military installation — the kind of place where a small group could light a solstice fire at the waterline on a June night without official notice. That second condition is precisely what ended by 1990, once the crowd outgrew the obscurity that had sheltered it.

## Battery Chamberlin and 220 Years of Military Ground

The Presidio land that backs Baker Beach has been military territory since 1776, when Spain established a garrison at the mouth of San Francisco Bay. It passed to Mexico in 1821 and to the United States Army in 1846, which held it until October 1, 1994 — 218 years of continuous military use, ending quietly when the Army transferred the Presidio to the National Park Service within the Golden Gate National Recreation Area.

The most legible evidence of that history stands on the bluff directly above the south end of the beach. Battery Chamberlin, completed in 1904 under the Endicott coastal defense program, mounted four 6-inch rifled guns on disappearing carriages — an engineering solution to a specific tactical problem. The carriage mechanism used the recoil of firing to drop the gun barrel below the concrete parapet, where the crew could reload it out of an enemy's line of sight. A hydraulic counterweight then hoisted the gun back to firing position. The battery's range covered the outer approaches to the Golden Gate and the minefields the Army laid in the channel against an enemy fleet. The guns came out in 1948 when carrier-based airpower made fixed coastal artillery a relic.

The 6-inch disappearing gun mounted at Battery Chamberlin today is not original to the site; it is a 1977 gift from the Smithsonian Institution, installed so the mechanism could be demonstrated to visitors. The National Park Service runs the gun up and down on the first full weekend of most months, from 11 a.m. to 3 p.m., and opens the underground cartridge room below the emplacement. The room holds exhibits on Endicott-era coastal defense engineering and on the battery's specific place in the defense of San Francisco Bay. Standing at the gun and looking north along the beach toward the bridge, you are looking across the same water the battery was built to deny to an enemy fleet that never came.

## The North End: Clothing-Optional, Strongest Current, Best View

The northern section of Baker Beach, below the steep bluff and reached by the wooden Sand Ladder that climbs to the Batteries to Bluffs Trail, has been a clothing-optional beach for decades. No law established it as such; no ordinance governs it. The custom simply settled there, and it has persisted under both Army and National Park Service administration, tolerated as an informal practice on federal land where nudity is not explicitly prohibited.

The "Sun Worshippers" is the informal name sometimes used for the regulars at the north end, though it does not appear in official Presidio documentation. The area is quieter and more exposed than the main beach, set back from the parking lots and partly sheltered from view by the bluff. It also has the cleanest unobstructed sightline to the Golden Gate Bridge's south tower — closer to the span here than anywhere else on the beach — and the strongest current. The riptides off the north end are the worst on this stretch of coast, and the Presidio's guidance is unambiguous: be extremely cautious in the water. The realistic counsel is not to enter it. This is where the 1986–1990 burns happened, in the zone of best view and worst danger, which feels appropriate in retrospect.

## The Bridge as Cultural Object

The Golden Gate Bridge opened in May 1937, and Baker Beach acquired its defining character almost immediately. The sea-level angle from the sand — looking north across the mouth of the strait, bridge and breaking surf and Marin Headlands in a single unobstructed frame — is not available from any other vehicle-accessible location in San Francisco. Fort Point puts you under the span; the Marin Headlands put you across from it; the bridge walkway puts you on it. Baker Beach puts you at the waterline, looking up at it against the open sky, with Pacific swell breaking in front.

That composition is the reason photographers return. It is also the reason the beach is frequently frustrating: the marine layer that pours through the Golden Gate on summer afternoons erases the view that drew the visit. June and July are the foggiest months. The bridge disappears into grey on afternoons when the temperature inland rises and pulls cool ocean air through the strait. September and October are statistically the clearest and warmest months at Baker Beach — the local "second summer" — when the fog recedes and the light on the south tower in the late afternoon is the picture that the travel photographs have been selling all year.

The bridge entered global pop-culture imagery through decades of film and television, but Baker Beach itself has rarely appeared on screen as a named location. Its cultural weight comes from the ground rather than from representation: the Burning Man origin, the military history, the clothing-optional tradition, and the physical fact of being able to stand at the Pacific Ocean and look up at one of the twentieth century's engineering landmarks from below.

## What the Presidio Transfer Changed

Before October 1994, Baker Beach was military land. The Army's presence kept the beach from becoming a standard urban public beach — there was no commercial development, no concession stands, no lifeguard stands, no beach culture infrastructure. Those absences remain after the transfer to the National Park Service and are now features rather than oversights. The beach has restrooms and picnic tables and nothing else. Fires are not permitted — the rule that ended Burning Man's tenure here. Alcohol is restricted under GGNRA rules. No food vendor has ever operated on the sand, and the two free parking lots exist without a fee structure that would fund services.

The NPS inherited a beach that the Army had preserved by neglect, and has maintained it in roughly the same condition: public, free, minimal, and shaped by the water and the bluff rather than by any human attempt to make it more comfortable. That condition is why the place still feels wild at the edge of a city of 800,000 people, and why it was still possible, as late as 1990, to light a forty-foot bonfire on it without a permit and have several thousand people show up because the fire looked interesting from the road.
