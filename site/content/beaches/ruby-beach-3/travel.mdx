# Ruby Beach Travel Guide

Ruby Beach offers one of the Pacific Northwest's most dramatic coastal experiences—a wild, sea-stack-studded shoreline with ruby-red garnet sand nestled within Olympic National Park. Unlike heavily developed beaches, Ruby Beach maintains a raw, authentic character. It's a destination for photographers, hikers, and anyone seeking genuine Pacific coast wilderness within reach of civilization.

## Getting There

### By Air

Most visitors fly into **Seattle-Tacoma International Airport (SEA)**, located approximately 180 kilometers northeast of Ruby Beach (roughly 3.5–4 hours by car). This is the Pacific Northwest's primary international airport with connections throughout North America and beyond.

A regional alternative is **Bowerman Airport (HQM)** near Hoquiam, just 89.8 kilometers away (approximately 1.5 hours driving). While HQM handles far fewer flights than SEA, if you can access regional carriers, the drive time savings are substantial.

From SEA, the drive to Ruby Beach requires crossing the Puget Sound region. The most direct route is: take I-5 South from the airport toward Olympia, exit onto US-101 North toward Shelton, and continue north through the Olympic Peninsula for approximately 2 hours. The total drive from SEA is roughly 3.5–4 hours.

Alternatively, you can take the **Edmonds-Kingston Ferry** (35 minutes) or **Bremerton-Seattle Ferry** (60 minutes) across Puget Sound, then pick up US-101 from the Olympic Peninsula side. This eliminates I-5 congestion but adds ferry time and fees ($15–25 per vehicle).

### By Car

**From Portland, Oregon** (approximately 450 kilometers): Take I-5 North toward Seattle, exit onto US-101 North near Salem, and continue north. Plan 6–7 hours total.

**From Vancouver, BC** (approximately 350 kilometers): Cross the US border at Blaine, take I-5 South, then US-101 North toward the Olympic Peninsula. Plan 5–6 hours.

**From Forks** (26.7 kilometers away, the nearest substantial town): Take US-101 South directly to the Ruby Beach access road. Plan 30 minutes.

**From Tacoma** (approximately 300 kilometers): Take I-5 North toward Olympia, exit onto US-101 North. Plan 4–4.5 hours.

Ruby Beach is accessed via a short access road off US-101. Parking at the trailhead lot is free but limited during peak summer weekends. Arrive early or consider visiting in shoulder season.

### By Public Transit

Public transportation to Ruby Beach is limited. The nearest transit hub is **Forks**, accessible via some regional bus services from Seattle (approximately 4–5 hours). From Forks, you'll need a rental car for the final 30-minute drive to Ruby Beach, as no shuttle services operate regularly to the beach itself.

## Best Time to Visit

### Seasonal Overview

**Summer (June–August)** is peak season, with temperatures ranging 60–70°F (15–21°C). Days are long, and rainfall is minimal. However, expect crowds at popular beaches like Ruby, and weather remains cool even at summer's warmest. Water temperature reaches only 50–55°F (10–13°C)—wetsuits essential for any water activities, though swimming at Ruby Beach is not recommended regardless of season.

**Spring (April–May)** offers mild weather (50–60°F/10–15°C), greening vegetation, and far fewer visitors. Rainfall remains moderate but decreasing. This is an excellent window for hiking and photography with solitude.

**Fall (September–October)** mirrors spring with pleasant temperatures and lower crowds. September maintains summer's dryness, while October brings increased rainfall but dramatic storm-watching potential.

**Winter (November–March)** is cool (40–50°F/4–10°C) and wet, with frequent rainfall and short daylight hours. However, this is when the coast displays its most dramatic character—storm systems create powerful surf and moody skies. Winter is the true "local season" on the Olympic Coast, and hardy visitors who embrace the weather experience raw coastal beauty.

## Where to Stay

Ruby Beach itself has no lodging directly at the access point. The nearest accommodations cluster around three distinct areas, each offering different experiences and trade-offs.

### Kalaloch Lodge

**Kalaloch Lodge** sits approximately 15 kilometers south of Ruby Beach on US-101, directly overlooking the beach at Kalaloch. This is the closest lodging option to Ruby Beach—a rustic, mid-century lodge with basic but clean rooms, cabins, and oceanfront views. The lodge operates a small restaurant and provides authentic Pacific Northwest character without pretense. Expect $100–150/night for standard rooms; cabins run $130–180/night. The lodge's beach access is more sheltered than Ruby's dramatic sea stacks, but it's convenient for multiple-day stays.

### Forks

**Forks** (26.7 kilometers away, 30 minutes driving) is the nearest genuine town with diverse lodging options. This timber-industry town has experienced a tourism uptick due to popular culture references, and consequently offers more choice than its size might suggest:

- **Budget motels**: $60–100/night; functional, modest amenities
- **Vacation rentals**: $90–150/night; increasingly common through Airbnb and VRBO
- **Small inns**: $80–120/night; some with character and local hospitality

Forks also offers the area's best access to restaurants, grocery stores, and services. It's a realistic base for 2–3 day explorations of the coast and rainforest.

### Lake Quinault Lodge

**Lake Quinault Lodge** (approximately 45 kilometers south, 1 hour driving) offers a more developed resort experience on freshwater Lake Quinault. This historic lodge (built 1926) features waterfront cabins, a restaurant, and a lodge atmosphere. Rooms range $120–200/night. Lake Quinault provides a scenic alternative base, particularly appealing if you're exploring both coastal and rainforest destinations.

### Camping

**Ozette Campground** (60+ kilometers north, 1.5 hours driving) and **Kalaloch Campground** (15 kilometers south, 20 minutes) offer National Park campsites with basic facilities. Expect $24–32/night. Reservations are essential in summer; availability opens 5 months in advance.

## Costs

### National Park Access

Ruby Beach sits within **Olympic National Park**. Day-use access requires an **America the Beautiful Annual Pass** ($80, valid at all US National Parks) or a 7-day **Olympic National Park pass** ($30 per vehicle, valid for entry and parking). Individual hikers can purchase a per-person pass for $15. The pass is purchased at park entrance stations or online.

### Nearby Services

**Food and beverage**: Forks offers modest restaurants and grocery stores. Expect $8–14 for casual lunch, $15–25 for dinner entrees. Kalaloch Lodge operates a basic on-site restaurant; prices are moderate for a remote location. A picnic prepared from a grocery store costs $12–20 per person.

**Lodging**: See "Where to Stay" section above. Budget $60–180/night depending on comfort level and location.

**Parking**: Free throughout Olympic National Park once you've purchased entry.

**Fuel**: Fuel availability becomes sparse on the Olympic Peninsula. Fill up in Port Angeles or Forks before heading to Ruby Beach; prices are 10–20% higher than Seattle due to remoteness.

**Overall daily budget**: Budget $60–100 per person for a day visit (park pass + fuel + food), or $150–300+ if staying overnight with lodging.

## Safety

### Water Conditions

**Do not swim at Ruby Beach.** While the view is spectacular, water conditions are hazardous:

- **Water temperature** ranges from 48°F (9°C) in winter to 52°F (11°C) in summer. Hypothermia develops rapidly.
- **Sneaker waves** are a serious hazard. These unexpectedly large swells appear without warning and have swept visitors off rocks and sand. Never turn your back on the ocean.
- **Currents and rips** occur, particularly near the sea stacks.
- **Rocky terrain** makes quick exit difficult if caught in surf.
- **Log debris** floating and shifting creates hidden obstacles.

Wading is occasionally possible at low tide, but exercise extreme caution. The Pacific coast is unforgiving; respect its power.

### General Coastal Hazards

- **Driftwood**: Large logs create both obstacles and habitat for splinters and nails. Wear sturdy footwear.
- **Tide pools**: Accessible at low tide but slippery and home to sharp urchins and anemones. Watch your footing.
- **Weather changes**: Coastal weather is unpredictable. A clear morning can become rainy and windy by afternoon. Bring layers and rain gear regardless of forecast.
- **Isolation**: Ruby Beach is remote. Cell phone service may be unreliable. Inform someone of your itinerary before visiting.
- **Wildlife**: Black bears inhabit the coastal forests. Store food properly if camping, and make noise while hiking.

### Health and Accessibility

The Ruby Beach trail is short (about 0.6 kilometers / 0.4 miles one-way) but includes some elevation change and uneven ground. It's moderately accessible but not suitable for those with severe mobility limitations. The beach itself is sand and rock, making wheelchair access impossible.

Sunscreen is essential—UV exposure is significant even on cool, overcast days.

## Local Tips

**Arrive early.** Ruby Beach is popular; the parking lot fills by midday on summer weekends. Aim for a 7–8 AM arrival to secure parking and avoid crowds.

**Time your visit for low tide.** Low tide exposes more sand and tide pools. Tide tables are freely available online and essential for planning. The difference between high and low tide on the Olympic Coast can exceed 10 feet.

**Bring excellent weather gear.** Even in summer, expect cool winds and possible rain. Layers, a waterproof jacket, and sturdy waterproof hiking boots are non-negotiable.

**Explore the intertidal zone.** At low tide, extensive tide pools reveal anemones, sea stars, crabs, and mollusks. Bring a field guide or download an app to identify species.

**Respect the driftwood.** The massive logs scattered on the beach are part of the ecosystem and the aesthetic. Don't move them or sit dangerously on unstable pieces.

**Nearby worthwhile destinations**: 

- **Hoh Rainforest Trail** (30 minutes south): A gentle, accessible walk through old-growth temperate rainforest. The contrast between coast and forest is extraordinary.
- **Lake Crescent** (45 minutes north): A pristine alpine lake offering hiking, kayaking, and restaurant access at Lodge at Lake Crescent.
- **Hurricane Ridge** (1.5 hours east): Accessible scenic drive with mountain views and additional hiking. Bring layers—altitude and exposure mean sudden weather changes.
- **Tree of Life** (nearby via Hoh Rainforest): A massive Sitka spruce hollowed by decay but still living. A striking symbol of old-growth forest resilience.

**Photography golden hours**: Early morning light (6–8 AM) and late afternoon (4–6 PM) create dramatic shadows and color on the sea stacks. Overcast skies can provide better color saturation than bright sun.

**Winter storms are spectacular.** If you can tolerate cool, wet conditions, visiting during an active storm system (watch the forecast for atmospheric rivers) creates some of the most dramatic coastal photography opportunities in North America.
