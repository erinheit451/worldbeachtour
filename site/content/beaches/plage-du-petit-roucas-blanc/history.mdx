# A Beach Built from a Subway: The Prado and the Idea of a Public Shore

The Plage du Petit Roucas Blanc is a small piece of one of the most quietly radical beaches in France, and its story is not really about sand at all. It is about a city that decided its citizens deserved a beach and built one for them out of the rock it dug from under itself. To understand the Petit Roucas Blanc you have to understand the Plages du Prado, and to understand the Prado you have to understand Marseille.

## Made from the Metro

The Prado beaches are artificial, and that is the single most important fact about them. They were created in the late 1970s, under the long-serving mayor **Gaston Defferre**, and the material they were built from was the spoil, the excavated rock and rubble, generated by boring the tunnels for **Marseille's two metro lines**. The city was digging a subway, had to put the debris somewhere, and made a remarkable decision: rather than dump it, it would reclaim a 3.5-kilometer stretch of coast and give the result back to the public as a free beach.

The Petit Roucas Blanc, and the whole Prado seafront, is therefore leisure built directly out of infrastructure, the inside of the city excavated and laid out to the sea. It is hard to think of a more honest origin for a beach: this is the literal ground beneath Marseille, turned into Marseille's playground.

## The Anti-Riviera

That origin shaped the beach's entire character, which is the deliberate opposite of the French Riviera. Marseille is France's oldest city and its great Mediterranean melting pot, a gritty, proud working port shaped by centuries of immigration from North Africa, Italy, and beyond. It is not Cannes and it is not Saint-Tropez, and it has never wanted to be. Its beaches belong to its people.

The Prado embodies that. It is **free, open, and inclusive**, 3.5 kilometers of public sand where the whole of an exceptionally diverse city comes to swim, a world away from the private beach clubs and paid loungers of the Côte d'Azur. In an era when so much of the world's good coastline is being privatized, gated, and priced out, the deliberate construction of a large free public beach for everyone is a genuinely radical civic act. It is easy to overlook because it looks like an ordinary city beach. It is, in fact, an argument about who the coast is for.

## A David and a Prison

The Prado's landmarks match its eclectic, democratic spirit. At the roundabout at the head of the beaches stands a four-meter marble **David**, a replica of Michelangelo's, donated to the city by the sculptor **Jules Cantini in 1903** and installed at the Prado in 1949. It is improbable, slightly absurd, and entirely beloved, the meeting point and emblem of the whole seafront.

Out to sea, about 1.5 kilometers offshore in the Frioul archipelago, sits the 16th-century **Château d'If**, the island fortress and former prison that Alexandre Dumas made immortal as the setting of *The Count of Monte Cristo*. To swim at the Prado is to do so beneath a Renaissance giant and across the water from one of the most famous prisons in world literature.

## Wind, Sport, and Spectacle

The Prado is also relentlessly active, and the reason is the wind. The **Mistral**, the powerful northwest wind that funnels down the Rhône valley and over the bay, makes these some of the great sailing and windsurfing waters of the French coast, blustery for swimmers but ideal for boards and sails. It is no accident that the **Roucas-Blanc marina** here hosted the sailing events of the **Paris 2024 Olympic Games**.

And the beach is a public stage as much as a shore. It has held the **2008 FIFA Beach Soccer World Cup**, the first ever staged outside Brazil; free concerts on World Music Day each June; freestyle skating at the famous Bowl of Marseille; and political rallies drawing crowds in the tens of thousands. The beach the city built gets used for everything a city uses a public square for, swimming, sport, music, and protest alike.

## What It Really Is

The Petit Roucas Blanc earns its place not as a beautiful natural beach, because it is not one, but as one of the clearest statements anywhere of a different idea of what a beach can be. It is not a resource a lucky resort happens to own. It is **public infrastructure**, built by a working, immigrant, often-maligned city for its own people, out of the rock it dug from under itself, and then handed over to be used freely by everyone.

Under a marble David, across from the prison of Monte Cristo, swept by the Mistral, packed with the whole diverse population of France's second city, the Prado is the least exclusive and most characterful great beach in the country. It is not where you go to escape a city. It is where you go to understand one.
