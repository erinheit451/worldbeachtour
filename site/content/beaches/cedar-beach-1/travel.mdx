# Cedar Beach Travel Guide

Cedar Beach is a barrier island escape on Long Island's Atlantic coast, offering the kind of quiet beach experience that Babylon Town residents have known for decades. Situated on the Jones Beach Island strip between the Atlantic Ocean and the Great South Bay, Cedar Beach combines genuine coastal access with the slower pace that Long Island's quieter communities cultivate. Whether exploring tide pools, watching migratory birds, or simply experiencing a barrier island without the theme-park atmosphere of nearby attractions, Cedar Beach rewards those willing to navigate the specific geography of central Long Island.

## Getting There

### By Air

Most visitors fly into one of the New York area's major airports:

- **JFK International Airport (New York)**: Located approximately 35 kilometers southwest of Cedar Beach (roughly 60–90 minutes depending on traffic and route). This is the region's primary international hub with the widest flight options globally.
- **LaGuardia Airport (New York)**: About 40 kilometers away (70–100 minutes). Somewhat closer to the city center but essentially equivalent for Long Island access.
- **Newark Liberty International Airport (New Jersey)**: Also roughly 35 kilometers away but on the opposite side of the metropolitan area; typically adds 90+ minutes to the journey.

From any of these airports, getting to Cedar Beach requires driving eastward through or around Long Island traffic, which is notoriously unpredictable.

### By Car (Most Practical Option)

Cedar Beach is not accessible without a car. There is no direct public transit connection from Manhattan or major Long Island destinations.

**From JFK Airport**: Follow signs for the Long Island Expressway (I-495 eastbound) or the Southern State Parkway (eastbound). Both routes eventually merge into or connect with Ocean Parkway, which runs directly along Jones Beach Island to Cedar Beach. Allow 75–120 minutes depending on traffic conditions and time of day.

**From Manhattan**: Take the Long Island Expressway (I-495) eastbound toward Long Island. Near Babylon, exit onto the Wantagh State Parkway southbound, then transition to Ocean Parkway, which leads directly to the barrier island and Cedar Beach area. This route typically takes 90–130 minutes from midtown Manhattan, though traffic congestion on the LIE can easily extend this to 2+ hours during rush periods (weekday mornings and late afternoons).

**From other parts of Long Island**: If you're already on Long Island, access is straightforward via Ocean Parkway once you're in the Babylon area. From Port Jefferson or the North Shore, plan 60–90 minutes via cross-island routes.

**Parking**: Cedar Beach has designated day-use parking. Non-residents pay a day-use fee (check current rates with Babylon Parks Department; as of recent years, this is typically $5–10 per vehicle for beach access). Arrive early on weekends; summer days can fill lots by mid-morning, particularly on sunny days.

### By Public Transit (Limited Option)

Public transit to Cedar Beach is challenging and requires multiple transfers:

- The Long Island Rail Road (LIRR) has a station in Babylon (roughly 15 kilometers north of Cedar Beach).
- From the Babylon LIRR station, you'd need to arrange local transportation (rental car, taxi, or rideshare) to reach Cedar Beach itself, as bus service is irregular.

**Reality check**: Day-tripping to Cedar Beach via public transit from Manhattan is feasible but not convenient. Expect 3+ hours of total transit time each way. If you don't have a car, budget for a rental or rideshare costs.

## Best Time to Visit

### Seasonal Overview

**Summer (June–August)** is peak season. Daytime temperatures reach 78–85°F (26–29°C), with long daylight and minimal rainfall. Ocean water temperature reaches the high 60s to low 70s Fahrenheit (20–22°C), making swimming more comfortable than spring or fall. Expect moderate weekend crowds, though Cedar Beach never reaches the density of central Jones Beach. Humidity can be notable in July and August. Lifeguard service is available on designated swimming areas during summer weekends.

**Spring (April–May)** offers mild temperatures (60–75°F / 15–24°C), fewer crowds, and increasing daylight. Water is still cold (50s Fahrenheit / 10–15°C); wetsuits are practical for any extended time in the water. Excellent for walking, bird watching (spring migration peaks in May), and photography. Rain is possible but declining as summer approaches.

**Fall (September–October)** mirrors spring with comfortable air temperatures, declining crowds, and increasingly dramatic skies. Water gradually cools from the low 70s to the mid-50s. September maintains summer warmth with noticeably fewer visitors. October brings more overcast days and possible rain but remains pleasant for shore activities. Fall migration brings varied bird species through the barrier island system.

**Winter (November–March)** is cold (35–50°F / 2–10°C) and frequently wet, with overcast skies and occasional nor'easters. Most facilities close or reduce hours. Water temperature drops to the mid-40s Fahrenheit; only committed swimmers or those in wetsuits venture in. However, this is when the beach takes on its most dramatic character—fewer people, moody light, and the raw energy of seasonal storm systems approaching from the Atlantic. Local naturalists and serious winter beach walkers know this as the true season for exploring the barrier island's real character.

## Where to Stay

Cedar Beach itself has no hotels or vacation rentals directly on the beach. However, Babylon Town and surrounding communities offer lodging options within 10–20 minutes of Cedar Beach.

### Babylon Village

The closest town option. Babylon has modest motels, bed-and-breakfasts, and vacation rentals aimed at beach visitors. It's about 15 kilometers north of Cedar Beach (20–25 minutes drive). The village is relatively quiet compared to tourist hotspots, with local restaurants and services. Expect $80–160 per night for basic accommodations.

### Bay Shore

Located about 15 kilometers northwest of Cedar Beach (25–30 minutes by car), Bay Shore is a larger residential community with more lodging options: small hotels, motels, and Airbnb-style rentals. Slightly more amenities than Babylon, slightly more traffic and activity. $90–180 per night is typical.

### Fire Island (Adjacent, But Different)

Fire Island, just east of Cedar Beach, is car-free and accessible only by ferry from Bay Shore or Sayville. This is a completely different experience—more resort-like in some areas, more bohemian in others. If you want to stay *very* close to Cedar Beach but prefer car-free living, Fire Island is an option (but requires ferry costs and different logistics). Accommodations run $100–250+ per night.

### Jones Beach Area (Very Close)

If you're willing to stay right on the Jones Beach boardwalk proper (central Jones Beach area), it's only about 5–10 minutes west of Cedar Beach by car. However, this puts you in a completely different ecosystem—theme parks, restaurants, and tourist infrastructure. $100–200+ per night, but noisier and far more touristy than Cedar Beach itself.

### NYC Day-Trip Option

Many visitors stay in Manhattan or nearby NYC neighborhoods and day-trip to Cedar Beach. This adds 90+ minutes each direction but provides access to thousands of hotel rooms and all of NYC's dining and entertainment. Cedar Beach becomes a morning or afternoon beach excursion rather than an overnight destination. Feasible but not ideal given the travel time.

### Accommodation Types & Pricing

- **Budget motels**: $75–130 per night; functional, limited amenities
- **Bed & breakfasts**: $100–175 per night; more character, sometimes include breakfast
- **Vacation rentals** (Airbnb, VRBO): $90–200 per night; 1–2 bedroom apartments or small houses
- **Hotels**: Limited direct availability; expect to search regional chains in Babylon or Bay Shore

Book 3–4 weeks ahead for summer weekends; shoulder seasons and winter offer far more availability and discounts.

## Costs

### General Price Level

**Beach access**: Babylon Town non-resident day-use fee is typically $5–10 per vehicle (check current rates; residents get free or reduced access). This is one of the lowest beach access fees in the New York region.

### Nearby Services and Activities

**Food and beverage**: Limited food options directly at Cedar Beach itself. Plan to bring a picnic or drive to nearby restaurants. In Babylon or along the parkway, expect casual meals ($10–20) and sit-down restaurants ($20–50+ per entree). A picnic from a grocery store costs $15–30 per person.

**Parking**: Already noted above; the day-use fee covers parking.

**Lifeguard services**: Available free during designated hours (typically June–August weekends and summer weekdays).

**Rentals**: Equipment rentals (kayaks, paddleboards) operate seasonally from nearby facilities; expect $30–60 per hour or $75–120 per day.

**Transportation from NYC**: Rental car roughly $50–80 per day, or Uber/Lyft from JFK roughly $60–100 depending on surge pricing. Drive time costs should be factored in if you're coming from the city.

**Overall day-visit budget**: Budget $60–100 per person for a beach day (parking + food + potential rentals), or $150–250 if staying overnight and including lodging.

## Safety

### Swimming Conditions

Cedar Beach's water is generally safe for swimming during daylight hours in summer. Key considerations:

- **Water temperature**: Ranges from roughly 45°F (7°C) in winter to 72°F (22°C) in peak summer. Hypothermia is a real risk in spring, fall, and winter; wetsuits are practical for anything other than brief summer dips.
- **Currents and rip tides**: Atlantic barrier beaches can develop strong currents, particularly near inlets. Never swim alone. If caught in a rip, swim parallel to shore rather than fighting the current directly.
- **Lifeguards**: Present during summer weekends and some weekday periods; check with Babylon Parks for current lifeguard schedules. Winter swimming is unguarded.
- **Shark incidents**: Zero recorded shark incidents at Cedar Beach (the entire Jones Beach Island system has no significant shark incident history).
- **Visibility**: Often limited due to suspended silt and seasonal plankton blooms; turbidity varies daily based on recent weather.

Avoid swimming after dark. Always use the buddy system. Check tide tables before visiting—barrier island beaches have significant tidal ranges (5–7 feet between low and high tide).

### General Safety

Cedar Beach and the immediate area are generally safe. Babylon Town is a residential community with routine police presence. Standard urban precautions apply: don't leave valuables unattended in your vehicle or on the beach. Petty theft from cars does occur in beach parking areas.

The barrier island itself is stable, but nor'easters and tropical storm systems can create hazardous conditions; avoid the beach during severe weather warnings.

### Water Quality

Water quality is monitored by Suffolk County. Most summer days are safe for swimming, but after heavy rainfall, beaches may be closed temporarily due to stormwater contamination. Check Suffolk County health department notifications before visiting.

Sun exposure is significant; apply sunscreen regularly. The Atlantic side's exposure to direct ocean swells means that wind and weather change quickly; bring layers and be prepared for temperature swings.

## Local Tips

**Understand the barrier island geography.** Cedar Beach sits between the Atlantic and the Great South Bay. The bay side (north) is calmer, shallower, and warmer; the Atlantic side (south) has open ocean conditions, stronger currents, and more wave action. Choose your activity accordingly.

**Low tide exposes more sand** and reveals tide pools and shell beds. Tide tables are freely available online; plan your visit around low tide if you want to explore the strand line or search for shells and beach life.

**Robert Moses State Park is minutes away** (drive west from Cedar Beach). If you want complementary activities—fishing, historic lighthouse, trail walking—Moses is a natural pairing with a Cedar Beach visit.

**Fire Island Lighthouse** is accessible from Fire Island proper (ferry from Bay Shore). It's a half-day trip from Cedar Beach and offers a different perspective on the barrier island ecosystem.

**Jones Beach boardwalk and central facilities** are a short drive west. If you want food options, entertainment, or changing facilities, the main Jones Beach area has all of it—but it's busier and less quiet than Cedar Beach proper.

**Bring your own food and water.** Cedar Beach has minimal food infrastructure compared to major recreation areas. Pack a lunch and plenty of fresh water.

**Bird watching peaks during migration seasons** (May and September). If you're interested in coastal species, the barrier island system is on the Atlantic Flyway; bring binoculars and a field guide.

**Winter storm watching is underrated.** When Atlantic nor'easters or tropical systems approach, Cedar Beach becomes dramatic—powerful swells, moody skies, and raw coastal energy. Locals and storm enthusiasts know this season intimately.

**Respect posted restrictions.** Some sections of the barrier island are closed seasonally for nesting or conservation. Pay attention to signs and barriers.

**The beach is wider at low tide and quieter in the north sections.** If you want solitude, explore the less-developed areas away from the main parking and facility clusters.
