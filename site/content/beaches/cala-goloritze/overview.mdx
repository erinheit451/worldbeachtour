Cala Goloritzè is one of Sardinia's most iconic and dramatically situated beaches, accessible only by foot or boat. Perched beneath towering limestone cliffs on the island's eastern coast, this small crescent cove represents everything that makes the Sardinian coastline legendary—pristine waters, rugged geology, and a sense of remoteness that feels increasingly rare.

The beach sits in the Gulf of Orosei, about 8.8 km inland from the village of Baunei. It's not a destination for those seeking convenience; there's no road access, no facilities, and no lifeguards. What you get instead is authenticity and solitude. The beach itself is composed of pebbles and smooth stones, backed by a narrow strip of sand that changes with the seasons. The water is crystalline and deep, shelving steeply—characteristic of this limestone-dominated coastline.

The surrounding landscape is dominated by the dramatic Supramonte plateau, a limestone massif that rises directly from the shore. This geological feature creates some of the most striking views in the Mediterranean: sheer cliffs hundreds of meters high, punctuated by caves and grottoes that have fascinated visitors for centuries. Local mythology and archaeological evidence suggest the area has drawn human attention since prehistoric times.

**What to Expect**

Plan on a 2-3 hour hike from Baunei through maquis scrubland and along cliff-edge trails. The reward is immediate: the view down into the cove is breathtaking, and the water justifies the effort. Swimming and snorkeling are excellent, though the lack of sand means you'll be navigating pebbles and potentially sharp rocks. In summer, the beach fills with hikers and day-trippers arriving by boat from nearby coastal towns—but it never feels crowded in the way tourist beaches do.

**Relevant Lenses**

**Photography** is perhaps the strongest draw here. The cliff-and-cove composition has been photographed thousands of times, yet the landscape rewards both iconic shots and intimate details.

**Hiking and outdoors** enthusiasts will appreciate the approach itself—the trail is moderately challenging and offers constant views of the coastline and Supramonte.

**History and geology** matter too. The limestone formations represent millions of years of marine sedimentation and erosion. The caves hint at human habitation stretching back millennia.

**Environment** is key to understanding the beach's charm: it remains relatively wild and undeveloped, though this also means less protection from the elements.

Cala Goloritzè demands respect for effort, but offers extraordinary returns for those willing to venture beyond Sardinia's more polished, accessible beaches.
