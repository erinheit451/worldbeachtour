# Martins Beach Travel Guide

Martins Beach is one of the most legally and culturally significant beaches on California's coast—famous not for vacation amenities but for a landmark lawsuit over public access rights. Located 10 kilometers south of Half Moon Bay on the San Mateo County coast, Martins Beach combines dramatic geology, cold-water conditions, and ongoing litigation that makes it a compelling destination for photographers, coastal advocates, and those interested in California's ongoing relationship with its public shoreline.

## Getting There

### By Air

Most visitors fly into **San Francisco International Airport (SFO)**, located approximately 60 kilometers north of Martins Beach (roughly 1.5 hours of driving depending on traffic). SFO is the region's primary international hub with extensive flight options across North America and the world.

A closer alternative is **San Jose International Airport (SJC)**, 80 kilometers south of Martins Beach (approximately 1.5–2 hours by car via Highway 17 and Highway 1). SJC handles fewer international flights but has good regional connections and is worth comparing for price and schedule.

### By Car

Martins Beach is accessible via California State Route 1 (Pacific Coast Highway/PCH), though access depends on the current status of ongoing litigation regarding public entry. The access road historically has been controlled or gated by the private landowner, so **you must verify current access conditions before driving to the beach**.

**From San Francisco (60 km / 1.5 hours):**
- Take I-280 South toward San Jose briefly, then exit onto CA-1 South at Pacifica
- Continue on CA-1 South along the coast for approximately 50 kilometers through Half Moon Bay
- Martins Beach is signed on the east side of CA-1, roughly 10 kilometers south of Half Moon Bay
- Turn east off CA-1 onto the Martins Beach access road (currently may be gated—do not trespass)

**From San Jose (80 km / 1.75 hours):**
- Take I-280 North toward San Francisco, then exit onto CA-1 North at Pacifica
- Continue north on CA-1 approximately 45 kilometers to Martins Beach (south of Half Moon Bay)

**From Monterey / Big Sur (100+ km / 2.5+ hours):**
- Take CA-1 North, continue through Carmel, Big Sur, and San Simeon
- This is a scenic but lengthy drive; plan a full day

**Critical Access Note:** Because of ongoing litigation, **do not assume the access road will be open**. The beach access gate has been variously open and closed depending on court rulings. Before making the drive, verify current conditions through:
- Half Moon Bay visitor information
- California State Parks regional office
- Recent online reports about Martins Beach public access status

Parking is not available at the beach itself (if the access road is open, limited parking exists near the gate). There is no commercial parking infrastructure.

## Best Time to Visit

### Seasonal Overview

**May through October** represents the most practical window for Martins Beach visits. Daytime temperatures range from 55–70°F (13–21°C), with better chances of clear skies and visibility. This period offers the most stable weather for driving the PCH and exploring the rocky shoreline safely.

**June through August** is peak season along the California coast, with longer daylight and warmer air temperatures (65–70°F / 18–21°C). However, ocean water temperatures remain cold (52–55°F / 11–13°C) and don't change significantly across the year.

**November through April** brings frequent fog, overcast conditions, and occasional rain. Road conditions are generally safe, but visibility along the PCH can be reduced. Winter swells are larger, making the coastline more dramatic but also more dangerous.

### Why Not to Swim

The water at Martins Beach is not suitable for swimming except in the most specialized circumstances:
- **Water temperature** is consistently 50–55°F (10–13°C), even in summer
- **Swell and currents** are powerful and unforgiving; the beach receives open Pacific Ocean energy
- **No lifeguard service** exists; the beach is unstaffed
- **Rock hazards** include underwater obstacles, sharp formations, and dangerous conditions during surge
- **Sharks** have not been documented at Martins Beach specifically, but the cold, rich Northern California waters support wildlife throughout the region

The beach is occasionally used by specialized cold-water surfers in winter when swell is largest, but recreational swimming is not practicable.

## Where to Stay

Martins Beach is not a destination for coastal lodging—no accommodations exist at or near the beach itself. Instead, base yourself in one of the nearby towns:

### Half Moon Bay (10 km / 15 minutes north)
Half Moon Bay is the closest developed town, offering multiple hotel options, restaurants, and services:
- **Half Moon Bay Lodge**, mid-range oceanfront hotel with pumpkin festival connections
- **The Ritz-Carlton Half Moon Bay**, luxury oceanfront resort with golf and spas
- **Numerous bed-and-breakfasts** throughout the downtown area
- Budget: $120–$350+ per night depending on season and amenities

Half Moon Bay combines easier access to Martins Beach with a full range of visitor services, farmers markets, and coastal small-town character.

### Pescadero (20 km / 25 minutes south)
Pescadero is a small, quieter village with charm and fewer crowds:
- Limited but pleasant lodging options (mostly inns and short-term rentals)
- Pescadero State Beach (immediately in town) offers an alternative beach with more amenities
- Budget: $100–$200+ per night

Pescadero offers a slower-paced coastal village experience without Half Moon Bay's tourist infrastructure.

### San Francisco (60 km / 1.5 hours north)
For broader accommodation options, restaurants, and urban amenities, San Francisco is a viable base for day trips to the Martins Beach area, though the drive is longer:
- Countless options across all budget ranges ($80–$400+)
- Requires 1.5–2 hours of driving each way

## Visiting Martins Beach Itself

### Costs

If access is available (verify beforehand), visiting Martins Beach is free. There is no entrance fee or parking infrastructure. However:
- Driving from SFO or SJC incurs gas costs
- Parking near the access gate may be limited or nonexistent

### What to Bring

- **Layers and wind protection** — even in summer, the coastal breeze is cold and can be strong
- **Sturdy footwear** — the rocky terrain and slippery kelp-covered rocks are hazardous
- **Camera** — the Shark Fin Rock formation and dramatic coastline are highly photogenic
- **No swimming gear** — do not assume the water is swimmable under any conditions
- **Tide information** — consult tide tables before visiting; some areas are inaccessible at high tide

### Safety Considerations

- **Sneaker waves** — the Pacific can produce unexpected large waves that run far up the beach. Never turn your back to the ocean while on the rocks
- **Cold water immersion** — if you enter the water unexpectedly (via wave or slip), hypothermia can set in rapidly in 50-degree water
- **Rocks and sharp formations** — the beach is hazardous underfoot; move carefully
- **No rescues** — no lifeguard service operates here. Cell service may be unreliable in the cove
- **Private property boundaries** — respect any posted signage regarding property limits

## Combining with Nearby Beaches

Martins Beach is most worthwhile as part of a larger coastal driving day:

### Half Moon Bay State Beach (10 km north)
A fully developed, family-friendly beach with restrooms, restaurants, and sand suitable for walking. A strong alternative if Martins Beach access is unavailable. Features the famous Pumpkin Patch and Fall festival.

### Pescadero State Beach (20 km south)
A beautiful sandy beach with more facilities (restrooms, parking, picnic areas) than Martins Beach. Known for tide pools and seal watching. Often less crowded than Half Moon Bay.

### Pigeon Point Lighthouse (35 km south)
Located south of Pescadero, this historic lighthouse sits dramatically on a rocky point. A short walk offers coastal views without requiring beach access. The lighthouse itself is under restoration but remains iconic.

### Año Nuevo State Park (50 km south)
A rugged, protected area famous for elephant seal breeding grounds. Accessible only via guided tours during breeding season (December–April). A powerful wildlife experience but requires planning and reservation.

## Practical Tips

1. **Verify access status before driving** — the most important preparation. Do not assume the road will be open.
2. **Drive the PCH deliberately** — Highway 1 between San Francisco and Monterey is one of the world's most scenic drives, but it's also narrow, winding, and subject to slides and closures. Allow extra time and drive during daylight hours.
3. **Combine with Half Moon Bay and Pescadero** — make a full coastal day rather than a single-beach trip. Three beaches in a day is achievable and worthwhile.
4. **Visit off-season** — May, September, or October offer good conditions with fewer crowds than summer.
5. **Understand why you're visiting** — Martins Beach is not a resort beach. It's a destination for photographers, coastal advocates, geology enthusiasts, or those interested in California's public-access debates. Set realistic expectations.
6. **Bring your own supplies** — no food, water, or facilities exist at the beach. Stock your car before leaving Half Moon Bay.

Martins Beach rewards preparation and realistic expectations. It's a powerful example of California's wild coast and a living case study in how Californians think about public land, private property, and the future of their shoreline.
