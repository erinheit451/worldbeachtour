# Muzhappilangad: The Culture of a Beach You Can Drive On

Muzhappilangad Beach sits in Kannur district on the Malabar Coast of Kerala, about 7 km from Thalassery (Tellicherry). It is the longest drive-in beach in Asia, and that single fact has shaped its culture more than any temple, festival, or fishing tradition. The local relationship to this shore is organized around a surface the sea takes back twice a day.

## The Drive-In Identity

The defining feature is geological before it is cultural. Muzhappilangad's quartz-dominant sand packs hard when wet, and a line of black basalt rocks running parallel to the shore breaks the strong currents of the Arabian Sea. That barrier keeps the sand firm enough to carry road vehicles along roughly 3.4 km of the 3.8-km beach. In 2016, BBC Autos named it among the six best beaches in the world for driving, and that recognition turned a Kannur-local beach into a national destination. Kerala Tourism now markets it explicitly as Asia's longest drive-in beach. The driving is not an add-on to the beach culture here; it is the beach culture.

## The April Beach Festival

Every April the village hosts the Muzhappilangad Beach Festival on the sand itself. The program is built around the drive-in character: car and bike stunts run on the firm low-tide beach, alongside cultural performances and local programs that draw crowds from across Kerala. The festival is among the most attended events on this stretch of coast, and it leans directly on the one thing the beach offers that almost nowhere else in India does legally — a coastline you can drive.

## The Malabar Table

Kannur belongs to the Malabar culinary region, and the food around the beach reflects it rather than a tourist menu. The day's catch — mackerel, sardine, prawn — is cooked with coconut and the sour fruit kudampuli at eateries near the beach and in Thalassery and Kannur town. Thalassery, 7 km away, gives its name to a distinct biryani made with short-grain kaima rice instead of basmati, a documented regional dish rather than a recent invention. Strong sweet Kerala tea and fried snacks from roadside stalls fuel a day spent on the sand.

## Dharmadam Island and the Green Name

About 100 to 200 meters south of the beach lies Dharmadam Island, which locals call Pacha Thuruthu — literally "Green Island" in Malayalam. At low tide it can be reached on foot from the adjacent Dharmadam beach across exposed sand and rock, and the crossing floods again as the tide returns. The island is small and largely undeveloped, and its low-tide access is itself a piece of the local rhythm: like the driving and the swimming, it runs on the tide table rather than the clock.

## A Birding Shore

Beyond the cars, this is a documented birdwatching site. More than thirty migratory species have been recorded along the shore. In 2013, a pectoral sandpiper and a Caspian plover were reported here for the first time in Kerala — both long-distance migrants well outside their usual range. The quiet early mornings at low tide, before the day's vehicles arrive, are when this side of Muzhappilangad's character shows.

## The Honest Local Truth

The culture of driving on Muzhappilangad is also a culture of managing a tidal beach shared by fast vehicles and people on foot. Beach wardens and lifeguards enforce speed limits after past incidents, because the hazards are specific: cars bogged in the soft wet margin near the water or caught by the returning tide, and swimmers carried past the end of the rock barrier into the open-sea currents it holds back. The local custom of reading the tide before driving or swimming is not folklore — it is the practical knowledge a drive-in beach requires.
