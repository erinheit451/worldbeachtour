# Surfing at Waikiki Beach

Waikiki is the birthplace of modern surfing. It was here that Duke Kahanamoku, Olympic swimmer and waterman, brought the ancient Hawaiian art of he'e nalu (wave sliding) to the world's attention in the early 1900s. Today, Waikiki remains one of the best places on earth to learn to surf, and its long, gentle reef breaks continue to deliver rideable waves for surfers of every level.

## The Breaks

Waikiki's south-facing reef creates a series of distinct surf breaks stretching roughly a mile along the coast. The reef is relatively flat and shallow in places, producing slow-rolling, long-period waves rather than the hollow, powerful barrels found on the North Shore. From east to west, the main breaks are:

### Canoes

Named after the outrigger canoe concessions that launch from this stretch of beach near the Moana Surfrider hotel, Canoes is the most popular beginner break in Waikiki and arguably in all of Hawaii. The wave breaks over a wide, sandy-bottomed reef shelf in water that is typically waist to chest deep at the takeoff zone.

Canoes produces long, slow right-handers that can carry a rider 100 yards or more on a good day. Wave faces are typically one to three feet, occasionally reaching four feet during larger south swells. The gentle slope of the wave makes it extremely forgiving — you have time to stand up, adjust your footing, and ride the face without being punished for mistakes.

The downside of Canoes is crowding. On any given summer day, expect 50 to 100 surfers and surf students in the water simultaneously, plus outrigger canoes, stand-up paddleboarders, and swimmers. Collisions are a real hazard. Practice basic surf etiquette: do not drop in on someone already riding, paddle wide around the break when going out, and keep your board under control at all times.

### Queens

Located just east of Canoes, Queens (also called Queen's Surf Break, not to be confused with Queen's Surf Beach on shore) offers a step up in wave quality. The break is named after Queen Liliuokalani, who reportedly surfed here. Queens produces both lefts and rights that are slightly steeper and faster than Canoes, with a more defined peak.

Wave faces at Queens typically run two to four feet, reaching six feet during solid south swells. The takeoff zone is slightly deeper than Canoes, and the wave has more push, making it better suited to intermediate surfers. Longboards still dominate, but shortboarders can find workable faces here when the swell is up.

Queens is less crowded than Canoes but still busy. The crowd tends to be more experienced, and the unwritten lineup etiquette is more strictly enforced. Wait your turn, respect the locals, and you will have no trouble.

### Populars (Pops)

Further east, Populars — commonly called "Pops" — is a favorite of experienced longboarders and local surfers. The wave breaks over a shallower section of reef and produces long, clean right-handers with more wall and speed than Canoes or Queens.

Pops works best on medium south swells in the three-to-five-foot range. The takeoff is more critical, and the wave can section and close out during larger swells. This is not a beginner break — the reef is shallower, the crowd is skilled, and the wave demands good positioning and timing.

### Other Breaks

- **Threes**: A deeper-water break further offshore that picks up larger swells. Produces long walls suitable for longboarding and SUP. Less crowded because of the longer paddle.
- **Kaisers**: A right-hander near the Ala Wai harbor channel. More exposed to wind but can produce clean, fast walls on a southwest swell with light trades. Popular with shortboarders.
- **Walls (Ala Moana Bowls)**: Technically just west of Waikiki proper, near Ala Moana Beach Park. A faster, hollower wave that breaks over a shallow reef. For experienced surfers only — it can produce overhead barrels and the reef is unforgiving.

## Conditions

### Swell

Waikiki's south-facing exposure means it picks up south swells generated by storms in the Southern Hemisphere. The primary swell season runs from April through October, with the largest and most consistent swells typically arriving in June through September. Swell periods are long — 14 to 20 seconds — which means the waves have traveled thousands of miles and arrive as clean, organized lines.

During winter (November through March), the south shore goes largely flat. Occasional northwest wraps or Kona storm swells can produce small, short-period surf, but serious surfers head to the North Shore in winter.

### Tides

Tides matter at Waikiki because the reef is shallow. Low tide can expose sections of reef at Canoes and Pops, making the water too shallow to surf safely. Mid to high tide is generally preferred. Waikiki experiences a mixed semidiurnal tide pattern — two high tides and two low tides per day of unequal height. The tidal range is modest, typically around two feet.

### Wind

The prevailing trade winds blow from the northeast at 10–20 knots. Because Waikiki faces south, the trades are offshore or side-offshore, which grooms the wave faces and keeps conditions clean. When the trades stall or Kona winds blow from the south, Waikiki can get choppy and blown out. Early mornings (before 9 AM) and late evenings tend to be the glassiest.

## Surf Lessons and Rentals

Waikiki has one of the highest concentrations of surf schools in the world. Most operate from the beach in front of the Royal Hawaiian and Moana Surfrider hotels. Expect to pay:

Reputable operations include Hans Hedemann Surf School, Ty Gurney Surf School, and the various beach boy concessions that have operated in Waikiki for generations. All provide soft-top beginner boards, rash guards, and reef booties if needed.

For more experienced surfers, board rentals are available from numerous shops along Kalakaua and Kuhio Avenues. Longboards (8–10 feet) are the standard choice for Waikiki's mellow waves. Shortboards work at Queens and Pops during larger swells but are frustrating on small days.

## Etiquette and Safety

- **Right of way**: The surfer closest to the breaking part of the wave has priority. Do not drop in.
- **Paddle around**: When paddling out, go around the break — not through the lineup where people are riding.
- **Reef awareness**: Sections of Waikiki's reef are shallow enough to scrape your fins or your body on a wipeout. Reef booties help. Do not stand on the reef.
- **Respect the beach boys**: Waikiki's surf instructors and beach boys are part of a tradition that goes back over a century. They know the conditions better than anyone. If they tell you to move, move.
- **Leash up**: Always use a leash. A loose board in a crowded lineup is dangerous.
- **Check conditions**: The Surfline Waikiki cam and NOAA buoy 51201 (Waimea Bay, which indicates south swell energy) are useful resources. The City and County of Honolulu Ocean Safety division posts daily condition reports.
