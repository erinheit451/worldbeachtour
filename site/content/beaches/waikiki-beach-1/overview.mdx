# Waikiki Beach

Waikiki Beach is a two-mile crescent of sand on the south shore of Oahu, stretching from the Hilton Hawaiian Village on the west end to the base of Diamond Head crater on the east. It is the most visited beach in Hawaii, drawing roughly 4.5 million visitors per year to its warm, shallow waters and consistent surf breaks.

The name "Waikiki" translates loosely to "spouting fresh water" in Hawaiian, a reference to the springs and streams that once flowed through the area's taro fields and fishponds before modern development filled them in. For centuries, Waikiki served as a retreat for Hawaiian royalty. King Kalakaua maintained a beach house here in the late 1800s, and it was on these waves that Duke Kahanamoku popularized the sport of surfing in the early twentieth century.

Today Waikiki is not a single beach but a series of interconnected sections, each with its own character. From west to east, the main sections include Kahanamoku Beach (fronting the Hilton lagoon), Fort DeRussy Beach, Gray's Beach, Royal Hawaiian Beach (sometimes called Waikiki Beach Center), Kuhio Beach, and Queen's Surf Beach near the Waikiki Aquarium. The western and central sections tend to be the most crowded, while Queen's Surf Beach on the eastern end attracts a more local crowd and offers better snorkeling near the reef.

The beach faces south, which means it catches the long-period south swells that arrive between April and October, producing the gentle, rolling waves that make Waikiki one of the best places in the world to learn to surf. In the winter months, the south shore calms considerably while the north shore of Oahu picks up massive north Pacific swells.

Waikiki's reef-protected waters stay warm year-round, averaging 77°F, and visibility typically ranges from 15 to 50 feet depending on conditions. The beach is fully public with free access, though much of the sand itself has been imported over the past century to combat chronic erosion — a topic that remains one of Waikiki's most significant environmental stories.

Whether you come for surfing, swimming, sunbathing, or simply watching the sun set behind the Waianae Mountains from the seawall at Kuhio Beach, Waikiki remains one of the most iconic beach destinations on earth.
