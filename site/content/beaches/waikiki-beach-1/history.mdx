# History of Waikiki Beach

Waikiki's story stretches from ancient Hawaiian settlement through royal retreat, colonial transformation, military staging ground, and mid-twentieth-century tourism explosion to the densely developed resort strip that exists today. It is a history of radical environmental alteration — wetlands drained, reefs dredged, sand imported, streams channelized — all in service of creating one of the most profitable stretches of coastline on earth.

## Ancient Waikiki

Before Western contact, Waikiki was a fertile wetland fed by streams flowing down from the Manoa and Palolo valleys. The area was laced with fishponds, taro lo'i (irrigated terraces), and spring-fed pools — the "spouting water" that gave the place its name. The coastal plain supported a significant population, and Waikiki was one of the most productive agricultural areas on Oahu.

The shoreline itself looked nothing like the wide sand beach visitors see today. Historical and archaeological evidence suggests the original coast was a mix of narrow sandy stretches, rocky reef flats, and muddy shoreline near stream mouths. The broad, manicured beach is a twentieth-century creation.

Waikiki held strategic and spiritual importance for Hawaiian chiefs. It was the site of significant battles during the wars of unification, and the area's productivity made it a prize for whoever controlled Oahu. Hawaiian royalty surfed the breaks off Waikiki for centuries — the sport of he'e nalu was practiced by both commoners and ali'i (chiefs), though certain breaks and styles were reserved for the ruling class.

## The Royal Era

In the nineteenth century, as the Hawaiian Kingdom formalized under a constitutional monarchy, Waikiki became the preferred retreat of Hawaiian royalty. King Kamehameha IV and Queen Emma maintained a residence in the area. King Kalakaua, the "Merrie Monarch" who reigned from 1874 to 1891, built a seaside cottage in Waikiki where he entertained dignitaries and promoted Hawaiian culture, including surfing, which had been suppressed by missionary influence.

Queen Liliuokalani, the last reigning monarch of Hawaii, was known to spend time at Waikiki as well. The break known as "Queens" offshore is associated with her, though the exact historical attribution is debated.

The overthrow of the Hawaiian Kingdom in 1893 by American businessmen and the subsequent annexation by the United States in 1898 set the stage for Waikiki's transformation from royal retreat to commercial beach resort.

## The Moana and Early Tourism (1901–1920s)

The Moana Hotel opened on March 11, 1901, as the first major hotel on Waikiki Beach. Built by Walter Chamberlain Peacock, it was a colonial-style structure with broad verandas, a banyan courtyard (the original banyan tree still stands), and rooms that cost $1.50 per night at opening. The Moana established Waikiki as a destination for wealthy travelers arriving by steamship, though tourism remained modest — Hawaii was remote, and the voyage from San Francisco took five days.

In 1906, the Seaside Hotel opened nearby, and Waikiki began to develop a small hospitality cluster. But the area remained largely a swampy wetland behind a narrow beach. Mosquitoes bred in the standing water, and sanitation was a persistent problem.

## The Ala Wai Canal (1921–1928)

The project that made modern Waikiki possible was the construction of the Ala Wai Canal between 1921 and 1928. Conceived by Lucius Pinkham (Governor of the Territory of Hawaii) and executed by the territorial government, the canal was designed to divert the streams feeding Waikiki's wetlands and drain the area for development.

The Ala Wai Canal runs roughly two miles along the inland boundary of Waikiki, intercepting water from Manoa Stream, Palolo Stream, and the Makiki watershed and channeling it into the ocean near the present-day Ala Wai Small Boat Harbor. The project drained the taro fields, fishponds, and wetlands that had sustained Hawaiian communities for centuries, replacing them with developable land.

The ecological consequences were profound. The fishponds were destroyed. The freshwater springs dried up. The natural sand supply from stream sediment was cut off. And the canal itself became a conduit for urban runoff that continues to pollute Waikiki's nearshore waters to this day.

But from a development perspective, the canal was transformative. It opened hundreds of acres of land behind the beach for construction, setting up the hotel-building boom that would follow.

## Duke Kahanamoku and the Surfing Renaissance

No figure is more closely associated with Waikiki than Duke Paoa Kahanamoku (1890–1968). Born and raised in the Waikiki neighborhood, Duke was a champion swimmer who won Olympic gold medals in the 100-meter freestyle in 1912 (Stockholm) and 1920 (Antwerp), along with additional silver and bronze medals across four Olympic Games.

But Duke's most lasting contribution was popularizing surfing worldwide. He demonstrated surfing during travels to the US mainland, Australia, and New Zealand, introducing the sport to audiences who had never seen it. In Australia, Duke gave surfing exhibitions at Freshwater Beach in Sydney in 1914-1915 using a board he shaped from local timber, an event widely credited with launching Australian surf culture.

In Waikiki, Duke was a central figure in the beach boy culture — a community of local watermen who surfed, swam, dove, and guided visitors in the water. The Waikiki beach boys became legendary figures, combining athletic prowess with hospitality and showmanship. Duke spent decades on Waikiki Beach as a surf instructor, lifeguard, and ambassador for the sport.

Today, a bronze statue of Duke Kahanamoku stands on Kuhio Beach with his arms outstretched. It was erected in 1990 on what would have been his hundredth birthday. The statue faces inland rather than toward the sea, a point of some controversy — many feel a waterman should face the ocean.

## The Royal Hawaiian and Matson Era (1927–1941)

The Royal Hawaiian Hotel opened on February 1, 1927, built by the Matson Navigation Company to serve passengers on its luxury steamship line from San Francisco. Painted pink and designed in Spanish-Moorish style, it was nicknamed the "Pink Palace of the Pacific" and became an instant icon.

Matson's strategy was vertically integrated: the company operated the ships that brought tourists to Hawaii, the hotels that housed them, and the tours that entertained them. This model established tourism as a major Hawaiian industry for the first time. Through the 1930s, Waikiki attracted a wealthy, glamorous clientele — Hollywood stars, industrialists, and society figures — though total visitor numbers remained small by modern standards.

## World War II

The Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor on December 7, 1941, transformed Waikiki overnight. The Royal Hawaiian Hotel was taken over by the US Navy as a rest and recreation facility for submarine crews. The beach was strung with barbed wire. Hotels were commandeered or shuttered. Tourism ceased entirely.

The military presence fundamentally changed Waikiki's demographics and infrastructure. Thousands of servicemen passed through during the war, and many returned as tourists in the postwar years, having experienced Hawaii's climate and beaches during their service.

## The Jet Age and the Tourism Explosion (1959–1970s)

Two events in 1959 reshaped Waikiki permanently: Hawaii became the 50th US state on August 21, and jet airline service to Honolulu began, cutting travel time from the West Coast from twelve hours (propeller aircraft) to five hours.

The result was an explosion of tourism and hotel construction. Through the 1960s and 1970s, Waikiki's low-rise hotel district was demolished and replaced with the high-rise towers that define its skyline today. The International Market Place opened as a tourist shopping village. Kalakaua Avenue was widened and commercialized. The population of Waikiki swelled with workers serving the tourist economy.

The pace of construction generated significant community backlash. Local residents protested the loss of public beach access, the obstruction of ocean views, and the displacement of long-time Hawaiian families from the neighborhood. In 1976, the City and County of Honolulu established height limits and setback requirements for new Waikiki development, slowing the building frenzy.

## Modern Waikiki

Today, Waikiki is a 1.5-square-mile district containing approximately 30,000 hotel rooms and hosting roughly 72,000 visitors on any given day. It generates an estimated $2 billion in annual visitor spending and is the economic engine of Hawaii's tourism industry, which itself is the state's largest sector.

The beach continues to evolve — and erode. Major sand replenishment projects occurred in 2012 and 2022, importing thousands of cubic yards of sand to maintain the beach width that visitors expect and the shoreline that protects the hotel infrastructure. The tension between natural coastal processes and the economic imperative to maintain a wide, photogenic beach defines Waikiki's ongoing story.

Waikiki's history is, in many ways, a concentrated version of the broader story of tourism development in fragile coastal environments worldwide: natural beauty attracts visitors, which attracts development, which degrades the very features that made the place attractive in the first place. Understanding this history enriches the experience of visiting — and raises important questions about what sustainable tourism looks like in the century ahead.
