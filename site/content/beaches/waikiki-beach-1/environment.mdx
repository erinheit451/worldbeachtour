# Waikiki Beach Environment

Waikiki Beach sits within one of the most heavily modified coastal environments in Hawaii. What visitors experience today — a wide ribbon of sand fronted by calm, turquoise water — is the product of more than a century of engineering, dredging, sand importation, and reef alteration. Understanding Waikiki's environmental story means reckoning with both its natural beauty and the significant ecological challenges that come with maintaining a beach that hosts millions of visitors per year.

## The Reef

Waikiki's fringing reef extends several hundred yards offshore and is the reason the beach exists in its current form. The reef breaks incoming wave energy, creating the calm, shallow lagoon that makes Waikiki so appealing for swimming and beginner surfing. Without it, the waves would reach shore with far more force, and the imported sand would wash away even faster than it already does.

The reef itself is in a degraded state compared to reefs on less-developed Hawaiian coastlines. Decades of runoff from the Ala Wai Canal, sedimentation from construction, anchor damage from boats, and physical impact from millions of swimmers, surfers, and snorkelers standing on live coral have taken a toll. Studies by the University of Hawaii's Hawaii Institute of Marine Biology have documented significant coral loss in the nearshore zone directly fronting Waikiki's hotel district.

However, the reef is not dead. Patches of healthy coral persist, particularly in deeper water further from shore and near the eastern end of Waikiki closer to Diamond Head. Species commonly found include lobe coral (Porites lobata), cauliflower coral (Pocillopora meandrina), and rice coral (Montipora capitata). The reef also supports a surprisingly diverse fish community given its urban setting, including yellow tang, convict tang, parrotfish, wrasses, and the occasional green sea turtle.

## Water Quality

Water quality at Waikiki is one of the beach's most persistent environmental concerns. The primary source of contamination is the Ala Wai Canal, an artificial waterway built in the 1920s to drain the wetlands behind Waikiki for development. The canal collects urban runoff from a 19-square-mile watershed that includes residential neighborhoods, golf courses, and commercial areas. During heavy rains, the canal overflows and discharges untreated stormwater — carrying sediment, nutrients, bacteria, and chemical pollutants — directly into the ocean near Waikiki.

The Hawaii Department of Health monitors water quality at multiple Waikiki beach sections. The testing program measures enterococci (indicator bacteria for fecal contamination) and issues brown water advisories when bacterial counts exceed safe swimming thresholds. These advisories are most common after heavy rains, particularly between October and March when rainfall is highest.

In recent years, the City and County of Honolulu has invested in improvements to the Ala Wai watershed, including:

- Upgraded storm drain infrastructure to reduce overflow frequency
- Installation of gross pollutant traps to capture debris before it enters the canal
- The long-planned Ala Wai Flood Risk Management Project, a joint effort with the US Army Corps of Engineers, which aims to reduce flood risk and improve water quality through detention basins and improved drainage

Despite these efforts, the fundamental problem remains: Waikiki sits downstream of an urban watershed that funnels contaminated water through a single canal outlet near the beach. Complete resolution would require massive infrastructure changes to Honolulu's stormwater system.

## Beach Erosion

Waikiki's sand is not naturally stable. The original shoreline was much narrower than what exists today, and historical accounts from the early 1900s describe sections of rocky reef and mudflat rather than the wide sand beach visitors now expect. The broad, sandy beach that defines modern Waikiki is largely artificial, maintained through repeated sand importation projects (discussed in detail in the Sand lens).

Chronic erosion is driven by several factors:

- **Seawalls and groins**: The hardened shoreline infrastructure built to protect hotels disrupts natural sand transport. Sand that would naturally shift along the coast gets trapped or deflected, creating areas of accretion and erosion side by side.
- **Reef degradation**: As the reef loses coral cover, it becomes less effective at dissipating wave energy, allowing more force to reach the beach and carry sand offshore.
- **Sea level rise**: Tide gauge records at Honolulu Harbor show sea level rising at approximately 1.5 millimeters per year over the past century. Climate projections suggest acceleration, with potential for 1–3 feet of additional rise by 2100. For a low-lying beach like Waikiki, even modest sea level rise translates to significant shoreline retreat.
- **Loss of natural sand supply**: The streams and rivers that historically delivered sediment to the coast have been channelized or filled in. The Ala Wai Canal intercepts much of what would have been natural sand and sediment input.

## Endangered Species

Waikiki's waters are part of the habitat range for several protected species:

- **Hawaiian green sea turtle (honu)**: Commonly seen grazing on algae on Waikiki's reef. Protected under the Endangered Species Act and Hawaii state law. Maintain a minimum distance of 10 feet. Do not touch, chase, or feed them.
- **Hawaiian monk seal**: Occasionally hauls out on Waikiki beaches to rest. One of the most endangered marine mammals in the world, with a population of approximately 1,400. NOAA and volunteer monitors set up barriers when a seal is resting. Stay at least 50 feet away.
- **Humpback whales**: Visible from shore between December and April as they migrate to Hawaiian waters to breed and calve. They do not approach the nearshore reef but can be seen breaching and spouting from the beach.

## Sunscreen and Reef Protection

Hawaii's Act 104, effective January 1, 2021, banned the sale and distribution of sunscreens containing oxybenzone and octinoxate, two chemicals shown in laboratory studies to contribute to coral bleaching and reef damage. Visitors to Waikiki should use mineral-based sunscreens containing zinc oxide or titanium dioxide as active ingredients. These products are labeled "reef-safe" or "reef-friendly," though it is worth checking the ingredient list, as some products marketed this way still contain other chemicals of concern like avobenzone.

## What You Can Do

- Use reef-safe mineral sunscreen
- Do not stand on or touch live coral
- Dispose of trash properly — litter on Kalakaua Avenue washes into storm drains that feed the Ala Wai
- Report injured or distressed marine animals to NOAA's hotline (888-256-9840)
- Support organizations working on Waikiki's environmental health, including the Waikiki Beach Special Improvement District and the Surfrider Foundation's Oahu chapter
- Avoid single-use plastics — Hawaii has banned plastic bags and polystyrene food containers statewide
