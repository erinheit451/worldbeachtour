# Gold Beach: the British centre and the harbour that fed the invasion

Gold was the middle beach of the five, the British centre of the D-Day line, between American Omaha to the west and Canadian Juno to the east. The fighting here on 6 June 1944 was real but, by the standard of that morning, contained: the 50th (Northumbrian) Division got ashore and pushed inland the same day. What sets Gold apart is not the assault but what came after — an entire prefabricated harbour, towed across the Channel and assembled off Arromanches, because the planners had decided not to capture a port at all.

## The plan: don't take a port, bring one

The logic behind Gold began with a problem. An invasion is sustained by tonnage landed day after day, and the deep-water ports that could land it had all been fortified into garrison-fortresses; the Dieppe raid of August 1942 had tested seizing one by assault and proved it murderous. So Overlord's planners inverted the question: rather than fight for a harbour, they would manufacture two in Britain, in secret, and float them to Normandy behind the assault — the Mulberry harbours. Mulberry "B" was assigned here, off Arromanches, to serve the British and Canadian beaches.

That decision shaped the assault: Gold did not need to deliver a port on the first tide, only to be opened and held so the engineers could build one behind it. The beach was split into the Jig sector to the west, toward Le Hamel and Asnelles, and King to the east, toward La Rivière and Ver-sur-Mer, with the 231st and 69th Brigades landing under Major General Douglas Graham. Facing them were elements of the German 716th static division, thinly spread and second-rate — though units of the better-trained 352nd Division had moved into the area, which Allied intelligence underweighted.

## The assault, and Hollis's Victoria Cross

The first waves touched down around 07:25, later than the American beaches because of the local tide. The bombardment had been heavier and more accurate than at Omaha, the ground was gentle, and the specialised armour of the 79th Armoured Division — the "Funnies," flail tanks and bridge-layers — came ashore largely on schedule. But the strongpoint at Le Hamel, on the western edge of Jig, was built around a 75-millimetre gun behind a thick wall, sited to fire in enfilade down the length of the beach rather than out to sea, and the bombardment had not destroyed it. It held out most of the morning, raking the sand, and fell only in the afternoon, taken from the blind landward side.

It was on Gold, in King sector, that the only Victoria Cross awarded for actions on D-Day was earned. Company Sergeant Major Stanley Hollis of D Company, 6th Battalion, Green Howards, landed at 07:32. Moving inland toward the Mont Fleury battery, he saw a German pillbox his company had walked past unawares and rushed it alone with a Sten gun and grenades, clearing it and a connected trench and saving the company from being shot in the back. Later, at Crépon, he went back alone into fire to bring out two men he had left pinned in a forward position. His citation rested on those two acts. He survived the war and died in 1972.

By nightfall the division had landed 24,970 men and pushed several kilometres inland; Bayeux, the first French town liberated, fell the next day almost intact. British casualties at Gold on 6 June are usually given as roughly 1,000 to 1,100, of whom about 350 were killed — lighter than Omaha's, but every figure from that day is an estimate.

## The Mulberry harbour, explained

The grey blocks standing in the water off Arromanches are the surviving skeleton of a system worth understanding piece by piece: a set of nested breakwaters protecting an inner anchorage with floating roads to shore. Furthest out lay the **Bombardons**, floating steel breakwaters moored offshore to flatten the swell. Inside them ran the main barrier: lines of **Phoenix** caissons — hollow reinforced-concrete boxes, some the height of a five-storey building, designed by the Welsh engineer Hugh Iorys Hughes, built across Britain, towed over, then deliberately flooded so they sank onto the seabed in line to form a solid wall. Those are the blocks visible today. The anchorage was sheltered further by **Gooseberries**, old ships scuttled bow to stern as a reef of hulls.

Within that calm water came the two pieces that solved Normandy's specific problem — its tidal range, here about six and a half metres at springs, which strands ships far out at low water. The **Spud** pierheads were floating platforms on legs resting on the seabed, sliding up and down with the tide so ships unloaded onto a deck at constant height; from them ran the **Whales**, floating steel roadways on pontoons designed by the engineer Allan Beckett to flex with the sea rather than snap. The whole assembly was, by area, the size of the harbour at Dover.

Then the weather tested it. On 19 June 1944, thirteen days after the landings, the worst Channel storm in forty years struck the coast and ran for three days, destroying Mulberry "A," the American harbour off Omaha, beyond repair; the Americans reverted to landing supplies over the open beach. Mulberry "B," in more sheltered water, survived; reinforced with parts salvaged from the dead American harbour, it was renamed **Port Winston**, for Churchill, who had pressed the artificial-harbour idea for years. Designed to last three months, it ran for about ten, until the captured port of Antwerp took over the burden late in 1944. Over its life it landed on the order of 2.5 million men, 4 million tons of stores, and half a million vehicles — the reason a quiet resort is a place of pilgrimage.

## The guns at Longues-sur-Mer

Just west of Gold, on the cliff between this beach and Omaha, stands the one piece of the German defence still at its post. The Longues-sur-Mer battery — four naval guns of about 150 to 152 millimetres (sources differ), built by the Organisation Todt in 1943–44, with a range of roughly twenty kilometres — is the only battery in Normandy that still holds its original guns in their casemates. The barrels in the concrete are the ones that fired.

On D-Day morning the battery opened fire around 06:05, throwing shells toward the invasion fleet and the headquarters ship HMS *Bulolo*, which was forced to shift to safer water. It fired on the order of 170 rounds before the warships answered: the British cruisers HMS *Ajax* and HMS *Argonaut* disabled three of the four guns that morning, with the French cruisers *Georges Leygues* and *Montcalm* and the US battleship *Arkansas* also firing along this coast. The garrison surrendered the next day. To stand in a casemate now and look through the embrasure at the sea the guns were built to deny is the German side of D-Day, still pointed at the water.

## The memorial, and how this coast is remembered

For most of the postwar era there was no British national memorial in Normandy naming the dead — a gap that sat oddly beside the American cemetery above Omaha. The **British Normandy Memorial** above Ver-sur-Mer, on high ground about 700 metres inland from Gold, closed it only on 6 June 2021, the seventy-seventh anniversary. Designed by the architect Liam O'Connor with the sculptor David Williams-Ellis, it records on 160 columns of Burgundy stone those killed under British command in the campaign between D-Day and the end of August 1944. The figure is usually given as 22,442, though "lost" names have since been added and some sources cite a slightly higher total, so the count is best read as exact-to-the-best-knowledge rather than final. A separate wall carries the D-Day dead.

The memorial states in stone the cost behind the engineering triumph in the bay. Port Winston was built to sustain a campaign that killed more than twenty-two thousand men under British command before the summer was out, and the 50th Division, which had the good morning of 6 June, went on to take among the heaviest divisional losses in Normandy. Gold holds both at once, in plain sight: the harbour ruins in the water that fed the liberation of a continent, and the names on the hill above of the people it was built to keep supplied.
