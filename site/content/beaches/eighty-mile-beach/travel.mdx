# Eighty Mile Beach Travel Guide

Eighty Mile Beach stretches across 220 kilometers of Western Australia's remote Pilbara-Kimberley boundary, offering a raw wilderness experience for travelers equipped for isolation and challenging conditions. This is frontier beach travel—no towns, no services, no lifeguards, no mobile networks. Access requires planning, preparation, and respect for the environment.

## Getting There

### By Air

**Broome International Airport (BME)** is the most practical arrival point, located approximately 250 kilometers north of Eighty Mile Beach. Broome has regular flights from Perth (2 hours), Darwin, and other Australian cities, plus international connections. From Broome, the drive south via the Great Northern Highway takes roughly 4–5 hours.

**Port Hedland Airport (PHE)** is the alternative, positioned 180 kilometers south of the beach. Port Hedland has fewer flights than Broome but is closer; the drive north takes approximately 3–4 hours.

Perth International Airport (PER) is Western Australia's major hub, 1,600+ kilometers away. Flying into Perth and driving 18+ hours is possible but typically not practical unless combining this beach with other Pilbara/Kimberley exploration.

### By Car

The only realistic access route is via the **Great Northern Highway** (AUS-1), which runs north-south along the coast. From Broome, drive south on the Great Northern Highway for approximately 250 kilometers; the turnoff to Eighty Mile Beach Caravan Park is marked and leads west off the highway. The final approach is an unsealed road suitable for conventional vehicles in dry conditions but challenging after rain.

From Port Hedland, drive north on the Great Northern Highway for approximately 180 kilometers to reach the same turnoff.

**Road conditions vary dramatically by season.** During the dry season (May–September), the Great Northern Highway and caravan park access road are generally well-maintained. During the wet season (November–April), heavy rain can make the unsealed approach impassable, and travel is not recommended without 4WD and extensive outback experience.

**Distances and driving times:**
- Broome to Eighty Mile Beach turnoff: 250 km, 4–5 hours
- Port Hedland to Eighty Mile Beach turnoff: 180 km, 3–4 hours
- From turnoff to Eighty Mile Beach Caravan Park: 20–30 km, 45 minutes to 1.5 hours (unsealed)

**No fuel or supplies are available at the beach itself.** Fuel up in Broome or Port Hedland before heading to the caravan park.

## Best Time to Visit

### Seasonal Overview

**May to September (Dry Season)** is the only recommended window for reliable access. Daytime temperatures range from 25–30°C with minimal rainfall, clear skies, and stable conditions. Roads are passable, and the beach is accessible. This is when the vast majority of visitors come.

**October to April (Wet Season)** brings heat, humidity, intermittent rain, and dangerous wildlife. Avoid this period unless you are highly experienced with remote outback travel. Road access becomes unpredictable after heavy rain, and conditions can deteriorate rapidly.

### Shorebird Migration Peak

**December to March (Austral Summer)** is when migratory shorebirds from the Arctic begin arriving. Peak numbers occur in **late December through February**, when tens of thousands of birds occupy the beach and mudflats. This is the prime window for birdwatchers, though **overlap with the wet season means challenging access and weather.** December and early January offer the best compromise between bird numbers and road conditions.

## Where to Stay

### Eighty Mile Beach Caravan Park

**Eighty Mile Beach Caravan Park** is the only developed facility directly serving the beach. Located just inland off the Great Northern Highway, it offers:

- **Powered caravan/campervan sites** (hookup for electricity, water)
- **Unpowered camping** (basic campsites with minimal amenities)
- **Basic facilities** (toilets, showers, water)
- **Camp store** with limited supplies (fuel, ice, basic groceries)
- **Cost**: AUD $20–40/night for unpowered camping; AUD $35–60/night for powered sites

The park is modest by developed standards—expect functional, not luxurious, facilities. Booking ahead is essential during dry season months and absolutely critical around December–February if timing with bird migration.

### Broome

**Broome** (250 km north) is a historic pearling port and the nearest major town with full services. It offers hotels, hostels, vacation rentals, restaurants, supermarkets, and fuel. Broome is a genuine destination with significant tourism infrastructure; staying here and day-tripping to Eighty Mile Beach is common, though it adds 5+ hours of driving each way.

- **Hotels/Accommodations**: AUD $80–250/night
- **Restaurants and cafes**: Wide variety, AUD $15–40 per meal
- **Fuel and supplies**: Full range available

### Port Hedland

**Port Hedland** (180 km south) is an industrial port town serving the iron ore industry. It's smaller and less tourist-oriented than Broome but has functioning hotels, basic restaurants, fuel, and supermarkets.

- **Hotels/Accommodations**: AUD $70–150/night
- **Restaurants and cafes**: Limited, AUD $12–35 per meal
- **Fuel and supplies**: Available but less diverse than Broome

### Pardoo Roadhouse

**Pardoo Roadhouse** sits roughly midway between Broome and Port Hedland on the Great Northern Highway, approximately 100–120 km from Eighty Mile Beach. It offers basic accommodation (motel rooms, AUD $90–130/night) and a restaurant. It's a pit-stop rather than a destination but useful if you're en route.

## Costs

### Beach Access and Parking

Eighty Mile Beach itself has no entrance fee or day-use charge. Parking at the caravan park or along the beach is included in site fees or is free if you're camping.

### Caravan Park Fees

- **Unpowered camping**: AUD $20–40/night
- **Powered sites**: AUD $35–60/night
- **Camp store supplies**: 20–30% above supermarket prices (expect to pay premium for convenience)

### Fuel

Fuel is essential—there are no service stations near the beach. Plan to buy in Broome or Port Hedland.

- **Perth to Broome**: ~AUD $120–180 in fuel (return trip)
- **Broome to Eighty Mile Beach**: ~AUD $40–60 in fuel (return trip from caravan park turnoff)

### Food and Supplies

Bring most supplies from Broome or Port Hedland. The caravan park's limited store will charge premium prices.

- **Self-catering from supermarket**: AUD $15–30/person/day
- **Camp cooking fuel (gas): AUD $10–20/bottle

### Overall Budget

**Budget estimate for a 3–5 day visit:**
- **Accommodation** (4 nights, caravan park): AUD $100–200
- **Fuel** (round trip from Broome): AUD $80–120
- **Food and supplies**: AUD $100–150
- **Total**: AUD $280–470 per person (camping/self-catering)

**With accommodation in Broome instead** (and day-tripping), costs double due to Broome accommodation and daily driving fuel.

## Safety

### Remote Location and Self-Sufficiency

Eighty Mile Beach is genuinely remote. There is **no mobile phone service**, **no emergency services nearby**, **no medical facilities**, and **no regular traffic**. You are completely self-reliant. This is not hyperbole.

- Carry a **satellite communicator** (Garmin InReach, SPOT device) or ensure someone knows your itinerary and expected return.
- Carry a **full-size spare tire, jack, tools, and repair supplies.** Roadside breakdowns can be life-threatening.
- Carry **ample water** (minimum 5 liters per person) and **emergency rations.**
- Ensure your vehicle is in excellent condition before departing.
- **Travel with others when possible.** Solo travel to remote beaches carries compounded risk.

### Wildlife Hazards

**Saltwater Crocodiles**: Present in the region, particularly in estuaries and deeper water bodies. They are increasingly common moving north. Avoid swimming in any water that might connect to saltwater systems, and never approach crocodile habitat.

**Box Jellyfish and Stingers**: October–May ("stinger season"), box jellyfish and other venomous jellyfish are present in tropical waters. Swimming is dangerous without protective clothing. Avoid swimming during this period entirely.

**Sharks**: While rare, sharks are present in Indian Ocean waters. Avoid dawn and dusk swimming; stay in groups.

**Snakes and Spiders**: Present in the spinifex scrub. Wear boots, watch where you step, and be aware of your surroundings.

### Tidal Hazards

The beach experiences a **9-meter tidal range**—one of the largest in Australia. This means:

- **Low tide exposes vast mudflats** that can become disorienting if you wander far offshore.
- **High tide can trap unwary travelers** on sandbars or mudflats if they don't monitor timing.
- **Currents** can be strong around tidal transitions.
- **Quicksand-like conditions** can occur in certain mudflat zones. Never venture far into mudflats without local knowledge.

Always carry a tide table and plan activities around tidal cycles. Never camp on the beach at high tide.

### Beach Camping

If camping directly on the beach (rather than at the caravan park):

- Camp above the **high-tide mark** (ask locals or check tide tables carefully).
- Be aware that beaches can be isolated; establish a communication plan.
- Check weather forecasts before settling in; wind and swell patterns change quickly.

### Water Quality and Drinking

Do not drink seawater or untreated water from the beach. Carry treated drinking water or a water filtration system.

### Driving Hazards

- **Unsealed roads** can be deceptive. What looks passable in dry conditions becomes impassable after rain.
- **No road markings or shoulders** on remote stretches; drive slowly and cautiously.
- **Wildlife crossing**: Occasional camels, cattle, or kangaroos may cross the highway. Drive defensively.
- **Fatigue**: The drive from Broome or Port Hedland is long. Take breaks and do not drive while fatigued.

## Local Tips

**Plan your timing around the tides.** A 9-meter range creates dramatically different beaches at high and low tide. Low tide exposes mudflats where shorebirds feed; high tide creates a narrow sand strip. Time your exploration accordingly.

**Bring binoculars and a bird guide.** If visiting December–March, you're witnessing one of the world's great bird migrations. A basic field guide and binoculars transform the experience from "empty beach" to "continental staging area for Arctic-breeding warblers and sandpipers."

**Respect Traditional Owner country.** Eighty Mile Beach is Karajarri and Nyangumarta Traditional Owner land. Seek permission before exploring inland areas, and respect cultural sites and practices.

**Fishing is permitted with a valid Western Australia fishing license.** The beach offers tarwhine, mulloway, and threadfin salmon. Bring a fishing license application form from fisheries WA or apply online before your trip. This requires advance planning.

**Don't rely on the caravan park for everything.** Stock up on fuel, food, and supplies before arriving. The park's store is minimal and expensive.

**Check road conditions before driving.** Call ahead to the caravan park or local authorities to confirm access, especially if traveling outside the peak dry season or after recent rain.

**The beach is best explored on foot.** The 220 kilometers is too vast to see in a single visit. Pick a section and walk slowly, watching for shorebirds and enjoying the solitude.

**December–March is when the beach comes alive ecologically.** If you can tolerate the heat, humidity, and higher risk of seasonal hazards, the shorebird migration makes this an extraordinary time.

**Winter (June–August) offers the most comfortable weather and reliable conditions.** Migratory birds are absent, but the beach's rawness and solitude are incomparable.
