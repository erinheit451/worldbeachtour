# An Island's Double Life: The Croatian Ibiza and the Island of Lace

Zrce is the loudest beach in Croatia, and the most interesting thing about it is how quiet everything around it is. The beach is a 24-hour electronic-music resort, the "Croatian Ibiza," grafted onto an island otherwise defined by rock, wind, salt, sheep, cheese, and centuries-old lace. To understand Zrce you have to understand both halves of the island it splits in two.

## The Party Beach

Zrce sits about two kilometers from the town of Novalja on the island of Pag, and on the surface it is an ordinary good beach: a long stretch of white pebbles, clear turquoise Adriatic water, a Blue Flag held since 2003. What it is famous for is what happens on top of that.

The beach is lined with four large open-air nightclubs, **Papaya, Aquarius, Kalypso, and Noa**, and with recent 24-hour permits the round-the-clock model has become literal. Aquarius runs non-stop: a beach bar and restaurant by day, foam parties in its central pool in the afternoon, sunset aperitifs to a dance soundtrack, and theme nights and international DJ sets through the dark. The soundtrack is house, EDM, hardstyle, trance, hip-hop, and r'n'b at the peak of summer, wrapped in the full party-resort kit of jet-skis, party boats, bungee jumping, and inflatable catapults.

## The Festivals

A regional party beach became a global one through the festival calendar. Across the summer Zrce hosts a dense run of major electronic-music events, **Hideout, Sonus, the Big Beach Spring Break, Wild & Free, Summer Peak**, and others, and the headliners are the genuine top tier of the dance world: **Swedish House Mafia, Armin van Buuren, Eric Prydz, Marshmello, Don Diablo, Timmy Trumpet**. On festival nights the crowd runs to tens of thousands. The explicit pitch is an Ibiza-grade experience at a fraction of Ibiza's prices, and for a stretch of the European summer that is exactly what this remote pebble beach delivers.

## The Other Pag

Now turn away from the clubs, and the island transforms completely. Pag, beyond this one strip, is one of the most distinctive traditional landscapes in Croatia: a stark, rocky, almost **lunar** island scoured by the **bura** wind, with some 35,000 sheep and a craft culture centuries deep.

That same harsh wind is the secret of the island's most famous product. The bura carries sea spray and salt inland, settling on the meadows where the sheep graze, and that salt-cured grass gives **Paški sir**, Pag's sheep's-milk cheese, its prized herb-and-sea-salt character; it is regarded as one of the finest cheeses in Croatia. The island's **salt pans** still produce around two-thirds of the country's salt, a trade central to Pag's economy for centuries. And **Pag lace** has been worked by the Benedictine nuns of Pag Town since the **15th century**, a craft recognized today as part of Croatia's cultural heritage. Salt, stone, sheep, cheese, and lace: this is the deep identity of the island that hosts the rave.

## Two Worlds That Barely Touch

The striking thing is how separate the two Pags are. Many visitors who know the party island have never seen the salt pans or tasted the cheese; many who know the traditional island have never been near a foam party. The party economy is real money and real fame for Novalja, but it is an intensive, seasonal, narrow use of one strip, set against a slow, ancient, craft-based whole. The two halves coexist on the same island and have almost nothing to do with each other.

That is not a criticism of Zrce. A party beach is a perfectly honest thing to be, and Zrce is very good at being one. It is simply not the "real" Pag, and to its credit it does not pretend to be. The most rewarding way to take the place in is to see both: spend a night at the clubs, and a day among the lace, the salt, and the sheep, and let the contrast do the work.

## The Frame at Dawn

If there is a single image that captures Zrce, it is the dawn. The 24-hour club is still going, the music still playing, as the sun comes up pink over the bare lunar hills and the clear Adriatic, and a few swimmers slip into the water while the last of the night's crowd drifts out. In that one frame you can see the whole strange arrangement: the lasers and the foam in the foreground, and behind them the silent, salt-scoured, thousand-year-old island that Pag has always been. The loudest beach in Croatia is best understood for the quiet world it sits inside.
