Kitsilano Beach, known locally as Kits Beach, is the busiest swimming beach on the south shore of English Bay, on Vancouver's west side. It faces north across the water to the downtown peninsula and the North Shore mountains. Its Squamish name is X̱epx̱páy̓em, "having red cedar," and the flats immediately east of the sand were Sen̓áḵw, a Squamish village and the core of Kitsilano Indian Reserve No. 6.

## Why this beach matters

The beach's name is the tell. "Kitsilano" is an anglicization of the name of Squamish chief August Jack Khatsahlano; the Canadian Pacific Railway adopted it around 1905 to sell the district it was subdividing. The reserve behind the beach was cleared in 1913, when the provincial government arranged an eviction that federal law did not permit, paid roughly twenty Squamish men about $11,250 each, barged the residents to other reserves, and burned their homes.

The Squamish recovered part of the land through the courts, accepting a $92.5-million settlement in 2000 that restored 11.7 acres. On that ground the nation is now building Sen̓áḵw, eleven towers and about 6,000 rental homes at the foot of the Burrard Bridge, under construction since 2022 and described as the largest Indigenous-led development in Canadian history. The towers are visible from the east end of the beach.

At the east end sits Kitsilano Pool, a 137-metre saltwater outdoor pool opened in 1931 and heated since a 1979 rebuild, the longest outdoor pool in North America. It is a seasonal outdoor pool, filled each spring with seawater from English Bay and open roughly from late May to September; storm damage delayed its 2024 and 2025 openings, and the city has begun planning a replacement.

The beach itself has banks of volleyball courts, a long seawall path for cyclists and walkers, and the free Kitsilano Showboat stage, which has run summer performances beside the pool since 1935. The water is calm, averaging under half a metre of wave height, and cool, staying in the mid-teens Celsius even in summer. After heavy rain, Vancouver Coastal Health posts E. coli advisories that can close the bay to swimming.

## Most relevant lenses

**History** — The reserve under the beach: the clearing of Sen̓áḵw in 1913 and the Squamish Nation's return to the land. This is the beach's defining story.

**Family** — The heated saltwater pool, shallow beach entry, summer lifeguards, and the free Showboat performances make Kits a practical family beach.

**Photography** — The downtown skyline and North Shore mountains across English Bay, with the Sen̓áḵw towers rising at the east end; the light is best in the long west-coast evenings.
