# The Fence at the End of the Runway: How Maho Beach Became Aviation's Most Dangerous Pilgrimage

When people who have never heard of Sint Maarten see a photograph of a Boeing 747 clearing a chain-link fence by the height of a two-story building while sunbathers scatter on the sand below, their first assumption is that it is fake. It is not fake. The geometry that produces the image is permanent, structural, and has been in place since the United States Army carved a wartime airstrip into the same sand bar in 1942 as a base for anti-submarine search flights. The planes were always going to cross low. What took decades longer to develop was the culture that gathered to watch them.

## How a Bar Built the Ritual

For most of the airport's civilian life, the low approaches were a local fact of the geography, noticed by anyone on the beach but not organised around. That changed in 1996, when Neil Rooney and a partner built the Sunset Bar and Grill on the sand directly facing the Runway 10 threshold — one year after Hurricane Luis had flattened much of the island's tourist infrastructure. Rooney's bet was that the planes were the attraction, not a nuisance. He was right.

The bar's practical innovation was the Sunset Surfboard: a hand-lettered board posted daily with the inbound flight schedule, updated throughout the day and broadcast over a speaker. Before smartphones and FlightRadar24, this board was how the crowd knew when to stop drinking and look up. The ritual it produced — watching the schedule, staking out ground near the fence as an inbound cleared the horizon, bracing for the overhead wash — became the organising rhythm of Maho Beach as a spectator event. The bar also named its drinks for the spectacle: the Jet Blast shot, the Runway Rum Punch, pizzas whose names rotate with the airlines serving the airport. The whole menu is a joke about the fact that your lunch arrives underneath an Airbus.

By the mid-2000s, aviation media had found Maho. Plane-spotting forums circulated photographs that were difficult to believe. YouTube, when it arrived, turned those photographs into video, and Maho's approach became one of the first aviation clips to accumulate millions of views — the belly of a 747 filling the frame, the crowd flinching at the noise, the sand whipping. The comment sections filled with people arguing about whether the footage was real and planning trips to see for themselves. That loop — astonishment, disbelief, verification in person — is the engine that has driven Maho tourism ever since.

## The KLM Era and What It Meant

The aircraft that made Maho's reputation global was a Boeing 747-400, specifically the KLM flight from Amsterdam Schiphol, which operated for decades and deposited European passengers onto an island whose airport could barely contain them. The 747 on a 3-degree glide slope over a 4-metre-high threshold is a different visual proposition from a regional turboprop: a double-deck aircraft with a 64-metre wingspan blocking the sky. The crowd on the sand below an arriving 747 was close enough to read the registration on the nose gear door. Aviation photographers who had shot planes at hundreds of airports described the Maho 747 as uniquely surreal — a widebody behaving as though it were landing in a stadium.

KLM's last scheduled 747-400 visit to Princess Juliana was 28 October 2016. The Amsterdam route moved to an Airbus A330, which crosses the beach on the same glide slope at the same height but with a different silhouette: narrower, less visually overwhelming. Among regulars this transition was discussed as a genuine loss, not of safety or access but of spectacle. The 747's departure closed a chapter in Maho's cultural history that no other aircraft had opened elsewhere.

## Fence-Surfing and the Dangerous End

The approach end of the beach is where you watch. The departure end is where people die.

When an aircraft lines up on Runway 28 — the departure direction that puts the exhaust aimed directly at the beach perimeter fence — and runs its engines up to takeoff thrust, it generates a jet blast that has thrown adults off their feet, stripped sunglasses and shoes from the crowd, and, at the departure-directed fence, produced sustained hurricane-force wind at close range. The local name for the practice of gripping the chain-link fence to ride that blast is fence-surfing. The airport authority has bolted warning signs to the fence in plain language for years, stating that jet blast and approaching aircraft can cause extreme bodily harm and death. The signs became, for a segment of the crowd, a scenic element rather than a directive. Holding the fence during a departure is filmed and uploaded. The footage circulates. More people come to do it.

On 12 July 2017, at approximately 6:00 PM, a 57-year-old woman from New Zealand was gripping the perimeter fence as a Caribbean Airlines Boeing 737 spooled up for departure to Trinidad. The blast tore her and others off the fence. She struck her head on concrete and was taken to hospital, where she died. Sint Maarten authorities confirmed the death; it was widely reported by the BBC, ABC News, NBC News, and New Zealand outlets. This was the first death directly attributable to jet blast at Maho Beach.

The response from the airport and local authorities was to add more visible warnings. The response from tourists was, in aggregate, to continue fence-surfing. Both facts are true simultaneously, and the tension between them sits at the centre of what Maho Beach has become: a place that is globally famous for a practice that its own warning infrastructure tells you can kill you. The crowd keeps calculating the odds in its favour. Sometimes the odds lose.

## Hurricane Irma and the Year the Beach Went Dark

Eight weeks after the July death, on 6 September 2017, Hurricane Irma made landfall over Sint Maarten as a Category 5 storm with sustained winds exceeding 295 km/h. It tore the roof off the Princess Juliana terminal, blew in the entrance doors, and flooded the interior with rain and seawater. The same storm stripped Maho Beach back toward bare rock. An island whose economy depends on air arrivals had lost its airport; an airport whose fame depends on a beach had lost the beach.

Irma was not the first hurricane to reshape Maho's sand — Hurricane Luis in 1995 had preceded the Sunset Bar's construction — but it was the first to take out the terminal infrastructure simultaneously. The airport reopened on temporary facilities on 10 October 2017, five weeks after the storm. The full terminal reconstruction was not completed until 2024, a seven-year process financed in part through international reconstruction funding. COVID-19 arrived before that reconstruction was finished, closing borders and grounding the Caribbean's tourist traffic from early 2020 through most of 2021. For roughly four years — from Irma in September 2017 through the pandemic recovery — the Maho crowd was a fraction of its pre-2017 peak.

What the interruption revealed was how durable the underlying draw is. When flights resumed at full capacity and the terminal reopened, the plane-spotters came back. YouTube channels now run live-streamed feeds of the approach with air traffic control audio, accumulating viewers in the tens of thousands for ordinary weekday arrival windows. The audience did not age out of interest while the beach was diminished; it grew, online, and then showed up in person when the airport could accept them.

## What the YouTube Era Changed

The Maho that existed before viral video was a destination known to aviation enthusiasts and Caribbean regulars. The Maho that exists now is on the bucket list of people who have never been to the Caribbean and would not otherwise visit Sint Maarten. The visual grammar of a Maho approach clip — camera tilted to fill the frame with descending fuselage, crowd visible at the bottom edge, the percussion of engines hitting physical pressure — is one of the most reproduced formats in travel video. Content creators from YouTube to TikTok have staged appearances at the fence for the blast video, including high-profile influencers. The loop that began with early aviation forum posts has compounded into a self-sustaining media ecosystem that generates its own arrivals.

What that ecosystem has not meaningfully changed is the physical reality at the fence. The jet blast that killed in July 2017 is the same force that the videos stage as entertainment. The signs are still on the fence. The 737s and A320s are still running up to departure thrust at the same distance from the same concrete. The culture around Maho Beach has become extraordinarily sophisticated at spectacle and extraordinarily poor at internalising what the warning signs are actually describing.

## The Honest Geography of the Thing

Maho Beach works as a cultural phenomenon because the geometry behind it is non-negotiable. The runway cannot be moved inland — Simpson Bay Lagoon sits on the other side. The threshold cannot be displaced further from the beach without shortening an already-constrained 2,300-metre runway. The glide slope is standard, 3 degrees, and the airport sits only 4 metres above sea level. Every aircraft that lands on Runway 10 will cross the sand at 10 to 20 metres overhead for as long as the airport exists. There is no engineering solution that produces a safer approach without closing Princess Juliana International Airport, which would end Sint Maarten as a tourist destination.

That permanence is what separates Maho from other spectacles. It is not a scheduled event or a seasonal attraction. It is a consequence of geography that aviation has been serving for over eighty years, and that a bar with a handwritten schedule board turned into one of the most-photographed beach experiences on earth.
