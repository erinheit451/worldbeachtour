Kehena Beach is Hawaiʻi Island's most distinctive volcanic shoreline, a black sand cove carved into the Puna district's dramatic coastline. Located near Leilani Estates, approximately 38 kilometers southeast of Hilo, Kehena occupies a unique ecological and cultural niche—a clothing-optional beach with a thriving Sunday drum circle tradition that has persisted for decades. The beach's formation traces directly to the 1955 lava flow that reshaped this section of the Big Island's southeast coast, creating the dark volcanic sand that defines the entire cove.

The setting is unmistakably Hawaiian, yet distinctly counterculture. Kehena's steep descent—you navigate a challenging trail down the cliff face to reach the sand—filters out casual visitors and creates an environment where a dedicated community gathers. The black volcanic substrate, still warm to the touch in summer, contrasts sharply with the clear Pacific waters that often host spinner dolphins and other marine visitors. The bay provides relatively protected waters despite its exposed location on the island's windward side.

What makes Kehena culturally significant is the sustained Sunday drum circle tradition that draws musicians, dancers, and spiritual seekers from across the island. This gathering, typically running from late morning through sunset, represents one of Hawaiʻi's few remaining intentional counterculture communities—a place where alternative lifestyles and local Hawaiian traditions coexist. The beach's clothing-optional nature is not an explicit policy but rather an established cultural norm that visitors are expected to respect and understand.

The ecological context deserves attention as well. Kehena supports diverse marine life despite its active volcanic history. The bay frequently attracts spinner dolphins and other cetaceans, particularly in early morning hours. The surrounding lava rock supports hardy coastal plants and seabirds, reminding visitors that even this young geological landscape hosts thriving ecosystems.

**Most Relevant Lenses:**

**Alternative Culture** — Kehena is fundamentally a gathering place for alternative and spiritual communities. The Sunday drum circle is not a tourist attraction but a genuine cultural institution where visitors are welcomed only if they respect the community's norms and values.

**Marine Life** — The bay's regular dolphin sightings and clear waters make it attractive to snorkelers and marine observers. The black volcanic sand environment supports unique coastal ecology adapted to recent lava substrate.

**Geology** — The beach's 1955 origin story and ongoing volcanic geology make it a living classroom for understanding Hawaiʻi's dynamic landscape. The black sand itself is geologically young and chemically active.

**Coastal Access** — The steep trail descent creates a significant barrier to access, making Kehena one of Hawaiʻi's more intentionally isolated beaches. This isolation defines both the challenges and the appeal.

**Community & Culture** — The Sunday drum circle represents sustained cultural practice and community building in a way few beaches in Hawaiʻi still do. Understanding this requires respecting local protocols and recognizing the difference between visiting and intruding.

Kehena Beach succeeds because it remains fundamentally a local community space that permits visitors—not a visitor destination that tolerates locals. This distinction shapes every aspect of the experience.
