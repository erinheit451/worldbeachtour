# Photographing Platges de Comte

Platges de Comte is a west-coast Ibiza beach in Sant Josep de sa Talaia, built around one repeatable picture: clear turquoise shallows in the foreground, three protected islets on the horizon, and a sun that sets directly behind them. This guide covers where the frame is, when the light works, and the rules that govern what you can and cannot shoot.

## The frame: west cove, islets on the sightline

The beach is two coves on a low headland. **Platja de Ponent faces due west**; **Platja de Tramuntana faces north**. The signature composition belongs to Ponent, because the sun and the offshore islands sit on the same west sightline. Three islands close the horizon: **Sa Conillera** (the largest, about a mile long, with a lighthouse built in 1857), **S'Illa des Bosc** (nearer in), and **S'Espartar** (farthest). At sunset they flatten into hard dark silhouettes against bright water — the reliable layered foreground-to-horizon picture this beach is known for.

The dividing headland carries the Sunset Ashram restaurant and is the higher vantage if you want the coves and islets in one elevated frame rather than shooting from the sand.

## The water color, and why it photographs

The turquoise is not a filter artifact. A constant flow of sea currents through the bay keeps fine sediment in suspension moving rather than settling, so the shallow water stays clear over pale sand. The EU bathing-water register classifies the site (station ES531M0481213) as **"excellent,"** its top band, and Cala Conta held a Blue Flag in 2010. Optically this is the full recipe for turquoise: a bright, reflective sand bottom, shallow depth, and a clear water column. Where the bottom drops away, the same water reads cobalt — the foreground gradient is the strongest graphic element in any midday shot here.

For the cleanest color, shoot the shallows when the **sun is high** (late morning to early afternoon), which lights the sand bottom and saturates the aqua. A polarizing filter cuts surface glare and deepens the gradient.

## Timing the light

- **Dawn:** The coves are empty, the parking is open, and the water is flattest before the wind builds — the clearest turquoise of the day, with the islets sharp on the horizon.
- **Midday:** Highest sun, brightest shallows, strongest aqua-to-cobalt gradient. Also the most crowded sand and the hardest light for people.
- **Golden hour / sunset:** The signature window. The sun lowers behind Sa Conillera and its neighbors; the islets go to silhouette; the foreground shallows hold the last light. This is the most-watched sunset position on Ibiza, so expect a full crowd on the sand and boats anchored offshore in the frame — use them as scale, or shoot wide to clear them.

Because Ponent faces west, there is **no usable sunrise over the water** here; the morning advantage is clarity and emptiness, not color.

## What you cannot shoot — the reserve

The islets that make the composition are a working nature reserve, not an open backdrop. Since **February 2002, under Balearic Decree 24/2002**, Sa Conillera, S'Illa des Bosc, S'Espartar and Ses Bledes are part of the Es Vedrà, Es Vedranell i els Illots de Ponent protected area — roughly **798 hectares**, a seabird and bird-of-prey breeding ground in the EU Natura 2000 network. **Landing on the islets is prohibited.** Shoot them from the sand, the headland, or the water; do not plan to set up on them. Drone use over a protected breeding area is restricted, so check current local rules before flying near the reserve.

## Practical notes

- **Parking** is limited and fills by late morning in July and August. For the empty dawn frame, arrive before 10 AM or accept a walk from the access road.
- **No entrance fee.** The beach is free; the cost is timing.
- For people-and-water portraits, the **Tramuntana (north) cove** is the quieter alternative away from the sunset crush, though it loses the direct west sightline.
