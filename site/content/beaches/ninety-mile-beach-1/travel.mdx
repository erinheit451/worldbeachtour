# Ninety Mile Beach Travel Guide

Ninety Mile Beach extends 150 kilometers along Victoria's Gippsland coast, forming one of the Southern Hemisphere's most extensive and undeveloped sandy shorelines. This is not a developed tourist beach with facilities and crowds; it's a wilderness coastal landscape where the experience revolves around scale, solitude, and serious ocean conditions. The beach's primary towns—Seaspray to the west, Lakes Entrance to the east—serve as gateways to access this remote stretch of Australian coast.

## Getting There

### By Air

Most international and domestic visitors arrive through **Melbourne Airport (MEL)**, located 250 kilometers west of Ninety Mile Beach. From MEL, the drive to the beach takes approximately 3 to 3.5 hours via the Princes Highway (M1) heading south and east.

A closer regional option is **Bairnsdale Airport (BSJ)**, located just 40 kilometers south of the beach near the town of Bairnsdale. Regional flights from Melbourne reach Bairnsdale in under an hour. This significantly shortens total travel time if you prioritize gateway convenience over flight frequency.

### By Car

Ninety Mile Beach is most commonly accessed via the **Princes Highway (M1)**, which runs along the coast east from Melbourne toward the beach.

- **From Melbourne**: Take the Princes Highway (M1) south and east toward Gippsland. Route through Warragul, then continue to Sale. From Sale, the journey is approximately 3 hours. The total distance is roughly 290 kilometers.
- **From Sale**: The closest town to the western sections of the beach, Sale sits 30 kilometers north of Seaspray. From Sale, take the road south toward Seaspray and the coast (approximately 45 minutes). Sales provides fuel, supplies, and accommodation options if you prefer not to stay directly at the beach.
- **From Bairnsdale**: Located on the eastern side of the beach area, Bairnsdale is approximately 50 kilometers from Lakes Entrance. From Bairnsdale, take the road to Lakes Entrance (approximately 1 hour), which provides eastern access to the beach.

**Driving conditions**: The Princes Highway is well-maintained, but coastal roads around Seaspray and Lakes Entrance can be narrow and winding. Winter weather (June–August) brings occasional rain and poor visibility. Plan extra time during winter months.

**Parking**: Parking is free and generally available along much of the beach. Western sections near Seaspray have dedicated parking areas; eastern sections near Lakes Entrance also provide parking. However, most of the beach's 150-kilometer length is accessed via walking from these gateway towns or by 4WD on sections where beach driving is permitted.

### By Public Transit

Limited public transit serves Ninety Mile Beach itself. **V/Line regional buses** operate from Melbourne and Bairnsdale to Sale and Lakes Entrance, but not directly to beach access points. From these towns, you'll need a rental car, taxi, or rideshare to reach the beach. This makes independent car rental from Melbourne or Bairnsdale strongly recommended for flexibility.

## Best Time to Visit

### Seasonal Overview

**Summer (November–March)** is the peak beach season across southern Australia. Daytime temperatures range from 22–26°C (72–79°F), with generally calm mornings and afternoon sea breezes. The ocean is at its warmest (18–20°C / 64–68°F) but still cold by northern hemisphere standards. The extended daylight (sunset around 8:45 PM in December) is ideal for long beach walks. Expect moderate crowds, particularly around Lakes Entrance and Seaspray on weekends, though the beach's 150-kilometer length means you can easily find solitude by walking away from entry points.

**Spring (September–October)** brings warming weather (18–22°C / 64–72°F) and increasing daylight. Swell tends to build in spring as Southern Ocean weather systems intensify. This is excellent for surfers and walkers, with fewer crowds than summer. Rain is possible but infrequent.

**Autumn (March–May)** mirrors spring with comfortable temperatures (18–22°C / 64–72°F) and autumn light. Swell remains reliable. The beaches progressively empty as school holidays end. Excellent visibility and photographer conditions.

**Winter (June–August)** is cool (12–17°C / 54–63°F) and wet, with frequent rain and strong winds. However, Southern Ocean storms create spectacular swell for experienced surfers and dramatic seascape photography. The beach is nearly empty except for serious recreationalists. This is genuinely a "local season" where crowds vanish and you'll often have vast stretches entirely to yourself.

## Where to Stay

Ninety Mile Beach itself has no accommodation directly on the sand. The primary gateways are Lakes Entrance (east) and Seaspray (west), each 30–50 kilometers away. Additionally, Sale (inland, 30 kilometers north of Seaspray) and Bairnsdale (30 kilometers north of Lakes Entrance) provide accommodation with better facilities and services.

### Lakes Entrance

The largest town serving the eastern beach, Lakes Entrance (population ~3,500) sits where the Gippsland Lakes connect to Bass Strait. This is a working fishing town with substantial tourist infrastructure.

- **Hotels & motels**: $100–180/night; moderate comfort, varying quality
- **Holiday apartments & vacation rentals**: $120–200/night for 1–2 bedrooms
- **Backpackers/budget lodges**: $50–90/night
- **Caravan parks**: $40–80/night for powered sites

Lakes Entrance offers restaurants, a small supermarket, fuel, and boat tours of the Gippsland Lakes. It's the most developed option and best for families or those wanting town amenities nearby.

### Seaspray

A smaller, quieter coastal settlement west of Ninety Mile Beach (population ~700), Seaspray is the gateway to the western beach sections.

- **Holiday homes & vacation rentals**: $90–150/night; charming, beach-community feel
- **Caravan parks**: $35–60/night; several options
- **Small guesthouses & B&Bs**: $80–120/night

Seaspray has a single general store, fuel, and a few casual cafes, but far fewer services than Lakes Entrance. It appeals to visitors seeking a quieter, more isolated experience.

### Sale

Sale (population ~15,000), located inland 30 kilometers north of Seaspray, is the regional service hub for accommodation, fuel, dining, and shopping.

- **Hotels & motels**: $80–150/night; better quality and variety than beach towns
- **Rental cars**: Available locally; strongly recommended

Sale is a logical base if you prefer town amenities and daily beach day-trips. It's only 45 minutes from Seaspray and provides all standard services.

### Bairnsdale

Similar to Sale, Bairnsdale (~12,000) provides regional services and is approximately 1 hour from Lakes Entrance. Hotels, rental cars, and restaurants are available, making it suitable as an alternative inland base.

### Caravan & Camping

Much of the beach is accessible to self-contained campervans and caravans. Caravan parks exist in both Lakes Entrance and Seaspray, and some sections of the beach permit unpowered beachside camping. Check local council regulations before camping on the beach itself; some sections are restricted.

## Costs

### General Price Level

Ninety Mile Beach itself is **free to access**. There is no entrance fee, parking charge, or day-use cost. This is one of Australia's most affordable coastal experiences.

### Accommodation Costs

- **Budget**: Caravan parks ($40–60/night) or backpacker lodges ($50–90/night)
- **Mid-range**: Holiday apartments in Lakes Entrance or Seaspray ($120–180/night)
- **Upscale**: Limited options; most accommodation is casual coastal or caravan park

### Food & Dining

- **Grocery shopping**: Seaspray and Lakes Entrance have small supermarkets; plan major shopping in Sale or Bairnsdale (30–50 km away). Groceries are typically 15–20% more expensive than Melbourne.
- **Restaurants and cafes**: Limited options; mostly casual ($15–25 per meal). Lakes Entrance has slightly more variety.
- **Picnic supplies**: Budget $12–20 per person for a beach picnic from local shops

### Supplies & Activities

- **Fuel**: Stations in Sale, Bairnsdale, Lakes Entrance, and Seaspray; prices typically 3–5% above Melbourne
- **Parking**: Free throughout
- **Rentals (if needed)**: Kayak or boat rentals available at Lakes Entrance ($40–60/hour)
- **4WD beach access**: Some commercial operators offer 4WD tours; approximate cost $80–150 per person

### Overall Daily Budget

- **Solo traveler**: $60–100/day (budget accommodation, self-catering, free beach access)
- **Family of four**: $200–300/day (vacation rental, groceries, local dining)

## Safety

### Water Conditions & Swimming

Ninety Mile Beach's ocean conditions are substantially more serious than sheltered bays:

- **Rip currents**: Significant rip currents are present, particularly near reef structures and headlands. These are capable of carrying swimmers offshore quickly. Only swim if comfortable with open water and aware of rip current escape techniques (swim parallel to shore).
- **Swell**: Bass Strait regularly delivers meaningful swells, especially in autumn and winter. Waves typically range 1–2 meters year-round, with consistent larger swells in winter. This makes the beach unsuitable for weak swimmers.
- **Water temperature**: 16–20°C year-round depending on season. Wetsuits (3mm minimum) are essential for swimmers staying longer than 20–30 minutes; hypothermia develops quickly in cold water.
- **Shark incidents**: Zero recorded shark incidents at Ninety Mile Beach according to GSAF data.
- **Lifeguards**: No lifeguard presence anywhere on the beach. Swimming is entirely at your own risk.

Avoid swimming unless you are an experienced open-water swimmer or have professional supervision.

### Remoteness & Mobile Coverage

The critical safety issue at Ninety Mile Beach is remoteness. Large sections have **no mobile phone coverage**, particularly west of Seaspray and in the central 80-kilometer stretches. If you walk away from vehicle access:

- Inform someone of your route and expected return time
- Carry water (at least 2 liters per person)
- Bring a personal locator beacon (PLB) or satellite communicator if traveling remote sections
- Never hike alone in remote sections without backup communication
- Weather can change rapidly; storm conditions develop within hours

### General Safety

- **Vehicles**: Don't leave valuables in your car. This is a quiet area, but petty theft from vehicles occurs.
- **Isolated areas**: The beach is very quiet, particularly west of Seaspray. Solo travelers, especially women, should exercise discretion on isolated sections.
- **Tides**: Tidal range is approximately 1–1.5 meters. Low tide exposes more sand; always check tide times before committing to long walks.

## Local Tips

**Start in Lakes Entrance or Seaspray.** Before walking the beach, visit one of these towns to get supplies, fuel, weather forecasts, and local knowledge. Both have tourist information centers.

**The Gippsland Lakes are more accessible than the ocean beach.** If you want a gentler water experience, take a boat tour from Lakes Entrance. The lake is sheltered and far more manageable than the open ocean.

**Point Hicks Lighthouse marks the eastern extreme.** Located roughly 50 kilometers east of Lakes Entrance, Point Hicks is Australia's most remote inhabited lighthouse and accessible by 4WD only. If lighthouse tourism interests you, arrange 4WD tours locally rather than attempting independent access.

**Wilsons Promontory is a logical day trip west.** Located approximately 100 kilometers southwest of the beach near the Western Port entrance, Wilsons Prom is one of Victoria's premier coastal parks. Many visitors combine Ninety Mile Beach with a Wilsons Prom visit as part of a broader Gippsland coastal tour.

**Carry communication devices.** If venturing beyond 5 kilometers from your vehicle, carry a personal locator beacon or satellite communicator. Mobile coverage is unreliable.

**Respect access restrictions.** Some sections of beach are periodically closed or restricted for environmental management. Check local council websites before planning extended walks.

**Winter storms are spectacular but dangerous.** Southern Ocean swells create dramatic seascapes in winter, but conditions are genuinely hazardous. Only experienced coastal navigators should be in the water during storms.

**The Gippsland Lakes supply fishing towns and boat culture.** Lakes Entrance is a working fishing port, and the waterway system supports commercial operations. This is not a pristine wilderness but a working coastal landscape with human history and continued industrial use.
