# Eagle Beach Travel Guide

Eagle Beach is Aruba's quintessential Caribbean destination—white powdery sand stretching for a mile, turquoise water remaining bath-warm year-round, and iconic wind-sculpted fofoti trees creating instantly recognizable tropical scenery. Located on the northwestern coast, Eagle Beach has become Aruba's signature beach, favored by first-time visitors and returning travelers alike for its remarkable consistency, accessibility, and the sense that this is exactly what a Caribbean beach should be.

## Getting There

### By Air

Most international visitors arrive at **Queen Beatrix International Airport (AUA)**, located 7 kilometers east of Eagle Beach. Flight times from North American gateways vary: Miami to Aruba (3.5 hours), Houston (4 hours), New York/Newark (5 hours), and most major Caribbean hubs connect via direct daily service. AUA is a straightforward gateway—relatively small and uncongested compared to major US hubs.

From the airport:

- **Taxi**: Taxis operate on fixed rates from the airport. To Eagle Beach costs approximately $18–22 USD (or 30–38 AWG). Journey time is 15–20 minutes. Taxis are reliable and ubiquitous.
- **Car rental**: Major agencies (Avis, Hertz, Budget, Enterprise) maintain airport counters. A compact sedan costs $40–65/day. Aruba's roads are well-maintained, driving is straightforward, and the island is small enough that unlimited exploration is feasible from one rental.
- **Shuttle services**: Hotel-affiliated shuttles offer mixed pricing; confirm with your accommodation before arrival.

### By Car

Aruba is a small island (32 kilometers long), making road travel viable for multi-beach exploration. Eagle Beach lies on the northwestern coast; the road system connects the entire island efficiently.

- **From Oranjestad** (the capital): 8 kilometers north via the main coastal road; 15 minutes
- **From Palm Beach** (the northern resort hub): 3 kilometers south; 10 minutes
- **From Savaneta** (the eastern fishing village): 22 kilometers; 25 minutes

Parking at Eagle Beach is available at multiple public lots and informal parking areas along the shore. There is no formal fee structure; donations or informal arrangements are common.

### By Public Transit

**Arubus** operates public bus service throughout the island. **Line 10** runs north from Oranjestad along the western coast, with regular service to Eagle Beach. Fares are approximately $2.50 USD per ride (exact fare required; bring coins). Buses are frequent during daylight hours (every 15–30 minutes), less frequent in evening. This is the budget option for those without a rental car.

## Best Time to Visit

### Year-Round Reliability

Aruba's greatest asset is **consistency**. The island sits well south of the Atlantic hurricane belt and experiences minimal seasonal variation. Water temperature remains between 78–85°F (26–29°C) year-round. Daytime air temperature averages 82–88°F (28–31°C) with little seasonal change.

### Seasonal Breakdown

**January–April (Peak Dry Season)**: The busiest period. Temperatures are warmest (highs 85–88°F), rainfall is minimal, and the northeast trade winds keep humidity manageable. European winter visitors arrive in volume, making this peak season for crowds and pricing. Accommodation rates are highest. Water temperature peaks at 83–85°F—genuinely swimmable for extended periods.

**May–June (Pre-Summer Transition)**: Weather remains excellent with lower rainfall. Crowds decline markedly; prices drop. An excellent window for those avoiding peak season. Still warm and sunny.

**July–September (Summer)**: Warm and humid, with occasional afternoon showers. Rainfall increases but rarely persists all day. Crowds are light and pricing is lowest. Water temperature is warmest (85–87°F). This is the season for budget-conscious travelers and those seeking fewer crowds, accepting slightly more variable weather.

**October–November (Shoulder Season)**: Humidity decreases, weather stabilizes, and rainfall becomes occasional rather than daily. Crowds build as winter approaches. An underrated period offering good weather with fewer visitors than January.

**December (Holiday Season)**: North American winter break travelers arrive in volume. Weather is ideal and Christmas atmosphere is celebratory. Accommodation prices rise, though not to peak-season January levels.

**Bottom line**: Visit anytime. January–April offers peak conditions with guaranteed sunshine. May–September trades some crowds for equivalent sunshine and warmest water.

### Turtle Nesting (March–September)

Loggerhead and green sea turtles nest on Aruba's beaches March through September. Eagle Beach itself and nearby shores host nesting activity. If wildlife viewing is important, this window overlaps with your visit. Protected nesting zones near Eagle Beach may be roped off during peak nesting hours (typically dusk).

## Where to Stay

### Low-Rise Hotel Zone (Eagle Beach Area)

The neighborhoods directly adjacent to Eagle Beach—known as the **Low-Rise zone**—offer numerous small-to-medium hotels, resorts, and vacation rentals within walking distance. This area has the most beach-centric amenities: restaurants, bars, watersports outfitters, and casual retail. Expect mid-range pricing and beachfront atmosphere.

- **Divi Aruba Beach Resort**: On Eagle Beach itself; all-inclusive option
- **Eagle Aruba Resort**: Direct beach access
- **Vacation rentals**: Numerous studios and 1–2 bedroom units; $120–220/night

Walking the Low-Rise zone reveals that Aruba's tourism infrastructure is developed but not excessive. Hotels are modest compared to Caribbean megaresorts.

### Palm Beach (North)

**Palm Beach**, located 3 kilometers north, is Aruba's northern resort hub with larger, more upscale properties. Beach conditions are nearly identical to Eagle Beach (same calm water, same sand). This area has more nightlife and higher-end dining.

- **Marriott Surf Club**
- **Amsterdam Manor**
- **Aruba Marriott Resort & Stellaris**

Costs are 15–30% higher than Low-Rise properties. Driving to Eagle Beach from Palm Beach takes 10 minutes.

### Oranjestad (Capital City)

**Oranjestad**, 8 kilometers south, is Aruba's capital and cultural center. Accommodation here is more urban and less beachy; prices are typically lower than beachfront locations. This option suits those wanting to experience local Aruba culture while day-tripping to Eagle Beach.

### Accommodation Price Ranges

- **Budget hostels/guesthouses**: $60–100/night
- **Mid-range hotels**: $100–200/night
- **Vacation rentals** (1 bedroom): $120–220/night
- **Upscale resorts**: $200–400+/night
- **All-inclusive resorts**: $200–350/night (meals and drinks included)

**Booking advice**: Book 4–6 weeks ahead for January–April; last-minute deals are common for May–November.

## Costs

### Beach Access
**Free.** No entrance fee or day-use charge for Eagle Beach.

### Accommodation
$100–250/night (mid-range); $60–100 (budget); $250+/night (upscale). All-inclusive resorts simplify budgeting by including meals and beverages.

### Food & Dining

**Beach vendors and casual restaurants**: $10–20 for lunch (arepas, empanadas, grilled fish, fresh tropical juice)

**Mid-range restaurants**: $20–40 per entrée

**Upscale beachfront dining**: $35–60 per entrée

**Groceries** (self-catering): Fresh fruit and vegetables are moderately priced; imported goods are expensive. A grocery store meal costs $15–25 per person.

**Overall budget**: Budget $80–120 per day for food if eating casually, or $150+ for mixed dining.

### Activities & Rentals

- **Snorkel or dive boat**: $40–70 per person
- **Paddleboard or kayak rental**: $20–35/hour; $50–80/day
- **Motorized watersports** (jet ski, speedboat): $50–120
- **Island tours/excursions**: $60–100 per person

### Transportation

- **Arubus**: $2.50 per trip
- **Taxi from airport**: $18–22
- **Car rental**: $40–65/day
- **Scooter/ATV rental**: $30–45/day

## Safety

### Water Conditions

Eagle Beach's water is exceptionally calm and safe for swimming. Key facts:

- **Water temperature**: Constantly 78–87°F (26–31°C)—no wetsuits needed; comfortable for children and elderly swimmers
- **Entry slope**: Exceptionally gentle; water reaches waist-depth after 100 meters
- **Currents**: Minimal; the beach's protected position eliminates strong alongshore currents
- **Visibility**: Generally 60–80 feet; excellent for snorkeling
- **Shark risk**: Extremely low; Aruba has zero recorded shark incidents at Eagle Beach or nearby shores
- **Lifeguards**: Not formally stationed, but beach patrols and tourism police maintain presence during daylight hours

**Swimming guidance**: Swim during daylight. Avoid swimming immediately after heavy rainfall (rare, but runoff can briefly affect visibility). The calm conditions make Eagle Beach ideal for swimmers of all abilities.

### Turtle Nesting Protection (March–September)

During nesting season, sections of beach may be closed to protect nesting turtles. Respect these barriers—turtle nesting is a protected activity on Aruba. Hatchling release programs sometimes allow public observation; check with your hotel or visitor information for timing.

### General Safety

Aruba is a very safe island with low violent crime rates. Standard tourism precautions apply:

- Don't leave valuables unattended on the beach
- Avoid isolated areas after dark
- Use common sense regarding whom you interact with

Petty theft from rental cars is uncommon but possible; don't leave electronics or passports visible.

### Health Considerations

- **Sun exposure**: UV index is high; sunscreen SPF 30+ is essential, even on cloudy days. Reapply every 2 hours
- **Water quality**: Monitored and safe; no post-rain closures as with some Caribbean beaches
- **Mosquitoes**: Minimal in daytime. Use repellent if visiting during evening hours, particularly during rainy months
- **Drinking water**: Tap water is safe and drinkable throughout Aruba
- **Food safety**: Standard precautions; eat fresh seafood at established restaurants

## Local Tips

**Arrive early for parking**: While Eagle Beach has ample parking compared to some Caribbean beaches, summer weekends and holidays can fill lots by mid-morning. Arrive before 10 AM for optimal parking and the calmest beach conditions.

**Bring reef-safe sunscreen**: Aruba's reefs are recovering and fragile. Use zinc oxide-based sunscreen (not oxybenzone), which is mandated for reef protection.

**The trade winds are consistent**: Northeast trade winds blow reliably and can create choppy moments, particularly in afternoon hours. Morning water is calmest; afternoon can be slightly rougher but remains safe.

**Fofoti trees are photo gold**: The wind-sculpted trees inland and along the shore create iconic silhouettes, especially during golden hour. Sunset photography here is legitimately stunning.

**Snorkeling is accessible right from the beach**: Eagle Beach itself has reasonable snorkeling—no boat necessary. Green Iguanas, rays, and reef fish are visible from shore with minimal swimming. Dedicated snorkel boat excursions access better reefs offshore.

**Fresh fruit vendors visit the beach regularly**: Walking vendors sell fresh pineapple, papaya, and coconut throughout the day. Prices are very reasonable ($2–3 per fruit). Accept these transactions—they're legitimate local commerce.

**The island's dryness is real—bring a water bottle**: Despite Caribbean location, Aruba receives minimal rainfall. The landscape appears semi-arid. Bring hydration and protect against dehydration despite the warm water.

**Explore nearby Palm Beach and Malmok beaches**: While Eagle Beach is superb, Palm Beach (north) and Malmok (south) are comparable and slightly less crowded. All three are essentially identical in quality; proximity and mood vary.

**Visit Oranjestad for culture and local flavor**: While the beaches dominate most Aruba trips, the capital city (8 km south) has Dutch colonial architecture, local restaurants, and markets that provide context for island culture. A morning visit pairs well with an afternoon at Eagle Beach.

**Turtle hatchling releases (seasonal)**: If visiting March–September, inquire about organized hatchling release programs. These evening events allow you to witness newly hatched sea turtles making their first journey to the ocean—a profound wildlife moment.
