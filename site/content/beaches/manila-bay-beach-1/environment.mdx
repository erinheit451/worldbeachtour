# The Dolomite Question: Manila Bay's Artificial White Beach

Manila Bay Beach is an environmental project that became an environmental argument. In September 2020 the Department of Environment and Natural Resources (DENR), under Secretary Roy Cimatu, laid a 500-meter strip of crushed white dolomite over the gray seawall along Roxas Boulevard. It was presented as part of the Manila Bay Rehabilitation Program, which Cimatu had launched in January 2019. Within days it was being attacked by marine scientists, public-health officials, and the government of the province that supplied the dolomite.

## The Bay the Beach Sits On

Manila Bay is the drainage basin of a metropolis of more than 13 million people and one of the most polluted bodies of water in the Philippines. In December 2008, in *MMDA v. Concerned Residents of Manila Bay*, the Supreme Court issued a continuing mandamus ordering more than a dozen government agencies to clean up, rehabilitate, and preserve the bay. That order is the standard the dolomite project is measured against: the legal mandate was to improve water quality, and the dolomite did not touch it.

The ₱389-million dolomite overlay covered the look of the shoreline along a 500-meter stretch, roughly 60 meters wide, between the US Embassy and the Manila Yacht Club. The water behind it remained the same Manila Bay, fed by untreated sewage and solid waste from the surrounding city. Critics, including a string of environmental groups, argued the money would have done more applied to sewage interception and waste management, the unglamorous infrastructure that actually governs the bay's water quality.

## The Material and the Health Warning

The white surface is not sand. It is crushed dolomite, a calcium-magnesium carbonate rock, barged in from Alcoy, Cebu, about 600 kilometers south. Crushed fine, it resembles white sand but lacks the rounded, water-sorted grains of a natural beach, and it sheds a fine dust.

The Department of Health flagged that dust as a hazard. DOH Undersecretary Maria Rosario Vergeire stated that crushed dolomite could cause chest discomfort, shortness of breath, and coughing. A toxicology expert warned the material might contain toxic heavy metals and silica dust. These warnings, issued as the beach opened to crowds during a COVID-19 surge, were part of why the project was criticized as a public-health risk on top of an environmental one.

## "It Will Wash Out to Sea"

The University of the Philippines Marine Science Institute warned that the dolomite would be carried off by storm surges. Manila Bay sits in a typhoon-exposed region; the wave data for this stretch shows the biggest seas in July, and the broader bay records dozens of cyclone passages over decades.

The warning was tested almost immediately. In November 2020, two months after the opening, Typhoon Vamco (locally Ulysses) struck, and the new dolomite beach was left covered with garbage washed in from the bay. The episode became the visual shorthand for the project's critics: a white beach meant to symbolize a cleaner bay, buried under the bay's own trash within weeks of opening.

## The Cost in Alcoy, Cebu

The dolomite was quarried by the Philippine Mining Service Corporation and the Dolomite Mining Corporation in Alcoy, Cebu. The extraction had consequences at the source. A Provincial Environment and Natural Resources Office inspection reported that crushed dolomite spilling into the sea as it was prepared for transport had destroyed corals covering about 2.5 hectares in Barangay Pugalo through heavy siltation.

The provincial government of Cebu and the municipality of Alcoy said they had not been informed of the Manila beautification project, and that the extraction and transport of dolomite to Manila were not covered by the required Environmental Impact Study. Cebu Governor Gwendolyn Garcia issued a cease-and-desist order. On 25 September 2020, Cimatu himself verbally ordered the suspension of two large-scale dolomite quarrying and processing firms in Alcoy. The DENR that built the beach suspended the mining that supplied it.

## The Defense

Cimatu and the project's defenders did not claim the dolomite cleaned the water. The argument was that the white space was a morale and behavioral measure: Cimatu said he hoped the white sand would change people's behavior on waste management, giving the public a clean space worth keeping clean, alongside the rehabilitation work continuing in parallel. The crowds that came for the Manila Bay sunset along Roxas Boulevard were, in that framing, evidence the space had public value. Whether a cosmetic overlay changes anyone's dumping habits has not been demonstrated, but the project was never officially sold as a water-quality fix.

## The Argument That Outlasted the Project

The beach was declared complete in October 2022, but the controversy did not close with it. The Duterte-era dolomite project became the subject of a House of Representatives inquiry, with former DENR chief Cimatu invited to account for the ₱389-million spending and the environmental record of the overlay. Years after the dolomite was laid, the same questions, cost, pandemic-era priorities, the damaged reef in Alcoy, and whether the bay itself was any cleaner, were still being asked in the legislature.

Manila Bay Beach is genuinely popular and a genuinely good place to watch a famous sunset. It is also a thin white layer over an argument the country has not settled: whether you rehabilitate a polluted bay by cleaning the water, or by changing the color of the shore in front of it.
