# A Beach Built from a Dump: Hietsu and the Finnish Year

Hietaniemi beach, which all of Helsinki calls Hietsu, looks in high summer like the most natural thing in the world: a city emptying itself onto a strip of pale sand by the sea. It is, in fact, a deliberate and slightly improbable creation, and understanding it means understanding the northern year it lives inside, a year in which the sea is cold, the summer is brief, and the deepest relationship with water happens in steam and ice.

## A Made Beach

Start with the origin, because it sets the tone. In the early twentieth century the Hietaniemi shore was not a beach at all. The area was used as a **landfill**, and then as a place to store sand, which was brought in by barge from the bottom of the sea. In **1929**, Helsinki residents simply adopted the resulting sand area as a public beach, and Hietsu has been the city's central beach ever since. A 1931 stone sundial by Gerda Qvist on a hill above the sand is among its oldest civic fixtures.

There is something very telling in that. A northern capital that wanted a city beach effectively built one out of a dump and some dredged sea-sand, and then loved it for the better part of a century. The beloved, natural-seeming strand is, underneath, an act of municipal will. It is a useful corrective to the romance of beaches: some of the best-loved ones are made.

## The Ten-Week City

The defining fact of Hietsu's life is the season, which is short and fierce. Helsinki monitors the water quality of its public beaches only from **15 June to 31 August**, a window of about ten weeks, and Hietsu lives almost its entire public existence inside it. When the brief Nordic summer arrives, the daylight near midsummer stretching to nearly around the clock, the beach fills completely. Swimmers take the cold Baltic; the **Hietsu Beach Volley** tournament, played here since 1995, runs on the courts; there is ultimate frisbee, picnicking, and, across the 2010s, full-scale music festivals on the sand, including Rock the Beach, Sonisphere, and the Weekend Festival.

Finns are famous for not wasting summer, and Hietsu is the proof in central Helsinki. It is a city compressing months of beach life into a handful of warm, luminous weeks, knowing exactly how few of them there are.

## The Catch in the Water

The honest complication is the Baltic itself. The sea off Helsinki is cold and brackish, and as it warms in late summer it is prone to blooms of **blue-green algae**, cyanobacteria, which can cause nausea, fever, and skin problems and which periodically make the water at Helsinki beaches, Hietsu included, unsafe for swimming. This is precisely why the city tests its beaches throughout the season: the quality can deteriorate quickly as the water heats up. The most-loved beach in central Helsinki is, on a bad algae day, a beach the authorities will tell you not to swim at. The short season is not the only limit; the water is the other.

## The Real Water Religion

To see Hietsu clearly you have to zoom out to the largest fact, which is that for Finns the real relationship with water is not the beach. Hietsu sits right beside the **Hietaniemi Cemetery**, the resting place of much of the national pantheon, the composer Jean Sibelius and Marshal Mannerheim among them, a solemn neighbor to the cheerful summer sand. And it belongs to a culture whose central water ritual is the **sauna** and the cold plunge.

That alternation of intense heat and freezing water is a year-round devotion, and in winter it becomes **avantouinti**: swimming through a hole cut in the ice, often straight from a sauna heated to eighty or ninety degrees Celsius into water barely above freezing. The ice-swimming season in southern Finland runs roughly December to March. So the summer beach, sociable and sunlit and crowded, is only the bright, ten-week, extroverted face of a people whose deepest engagement with water is practiced hottest and coldest in the dark months. Hietsu is the two bright months; the sauna and the ice hole are the other ten.

## A Triumph of Enthusiasm

Put it all together and Hietsu is a small masterpiece of making the best of difficult conditions. It is a made beach, on a cold and sometimes toxic sea, open for swimming barely a quarter of the year, beside a cemetery, in a country whose truer water culture happens in steam and ice. And it is genuinely, intensely beloved, packed every available summer hour by a city that decided it would have a beach and has wrung every drop out of the brief window the climate allows.

That is the lesson Hietsu teaches better than almost any warm-water beach could: that a beach is not really about ideal conditions at all. It is about a community deciding that this stretch of water and sand is theirs, and using it for everything it is worth, for as long as the year will let them.
