# Coney Island: The Rise, Fall, and Rebirth of America's Playground

The history of Coney Island is mostly a history of things that are no longer there. Three amusement parks, a 122-foot hotel shaped like an elephant, a steel tower that once dropped people on parachutes, the Pavilion of Fun. Almost all of it is gone, lost to fire, to closure, to the wrecking ball, in roughly that order. What stayed is a short list of survivors and a few million people who keep showing up on hot days anyway. To walk the boardwalk now is to walk across a layer of demolitions, with the receipts still legible if you know where to look.

This is the story of how a sandbar at the end of the Brooklyn shore became the template for the American amusement park, and then spent a century losing nearly everything that made it famous.

## Why anyone built here at all

Coney Island had no natural reason to become anything. The sand is ordinary lithic glacial-and-river sediment moved by longshore drift, not coral; the waves average two-thirds of a meter and rarely break in a way worth surfing. What it had was access. Through the late 19th century, Brooklyn pushed rail, and eventually the subway, all the way to the water, which meant a tenement worker could escape a brutal city summer for the price of a few cents. That created a captive market of millions, and where there is a captive market, someone builds a machine to monetize it.

The early version of that machine was loud and a little disreputable. The most outrageous artifact of the era was the Elephantine Colossus, a seven-story, roughly 122-foot hotel built in the shape of an elephant in 1885 to James V. Lafferty's design, with 31 rooms inside the body and legs. (Some accounts give its height as 150 feet; the figures conflict.) Within a few years it had decayed into a notorious brothel, lending the period euphemism "seeing the elephant." It burned in September 1896, after sitting unused, and was never rebuilt. That cycle — build something absurd in wood, run it hard, watch it burn — would repeat at much larger scale.

## The three parks, and the seven years they overlapped

The enclosed, gated, themed amusement park is, before it is anyone else's invention, a Coney Island one. George C. Tilyou opened Steeplechase Park in 1897 and turned a beach day into a ticketed, repeatable spectacle. In 1903 Frederic Thompson and Elmer Dundy opened Luna Park and wired it with something close to a quarter-million electric bulbs, an electric night-sky that most Americans had simply never seen at scale. The next year, in 1904, William H. Reynolds opened Dreamland a block away, deliberately whiter and brighter, chasing a more respectable clientele, its 375-foot Beacon Tower modeled on the Giralda of Seville and lit by a reported million lamps.

For seven summers, three full amusement parks ran at once, stealing each other's acts, undercutting each other's prices, all of them built of wood and packed with crowds, paint, and pitch. It was the most concentrated entertainment ground on earth, and it ran on exactly the materials that make fires catastrophic.

The end came as wood-and-crowd economics usually end it. In the early hours of May 27, 1911, just after Dreamland's eighth season opened, a worker overturned a bucket of hot pitch during overnight repairs. Low water pressure let the fire take the park; the Beacon Tower came down in a pillar of flame, and Dreamland was never rebuilt. The site eventually became, of all things, the New York Aquarium.

## The rides that outlived the parks

What survived is a short list, and that scarcity is the whole point. The Wonder Wheel, 150 feet of steel built in 1920 by the Eccentric Ferris Wheel Company to Charles Hermann's design, still turns, its sliding cars lurching on serpentine tracks the way they did a century ago. The Cyclone — designed by Vernon Keenan, built by Harry C. Baker, opened June 26, 1927 for the Rosenthal brothers at a cost of around $175,000 — has run on its famously rough first drop for nearly a hundred years and was made a New York City landmark in 1988.

The strangest survivor is the Parachute Jump. It was erected in 1939 for the World's Fair in Queens, sponsored by Life Savers, then moved to Steeplechase Park in 1941. At 262 feet, the "Eiffel Tower of Brooklyn" dropped riders on guy-wired parachutes until it stopped running, for good, in 1968 — a few years after the park around it had already closed. It was never demolished. It stands today as a lit monument to a ride that no living visitor has taken.

These are not a park. They are relics that outlived the thing they belonged to, which is exactly why people treat them as sacred.

## Nathan's, and the hot dog as ritual

The food outlasted the parks too. A German immigrant, Charles Feltman, was selling sausages in rolls on the Coney Island sand by 1867 — the dish that became the Coney Island hot dog. In 1916 one of his former employees, Nathan Handwerker, opened his own stand on Surf Avenue and undercut Feltman to a nickel apiece. Nathan's is still on that corner.

The July 4 hot-dog eating contest held there is a genuinely odd piece of Americana: a working-class kitsch ritual elevated to a nationally broadcast event, where competitors put away absurd quantities of the cheapest food on the boardwalk. Joey Chestnut's record is 76 hot dogs and buns in ten minutes, set in 2021. It is the rare Coney Island institution that has only grown.

## The decline and the demolitions

The fires were the first wave of loss; policy and economics were the second. After World War II, cars and the suburbs gave Americans somewhere else to go, and the aging amusement strip struggled. Robert Moses, the city's parks commissioner, openly disliked Coney Island's "catchpenny" amusements; he widened the beach with sand, pushed parking and bath houses over arcades, and in 1947 moved the Aquarium onto the old Dreamland site, partly to keep another amusement park from rising there. His repeated rezoning efforts in the 1950s shrank the amusement district toward a narrow strip.

The most-cited single act came later. Steeplechase, the toughest of the three parks, finally closed on September 20, 1964. The developer Fred Trump bought the land, sold off the rides, and in 1966 demolished the Pavilion of Fun — reportedly to forestall landmark designation under the city's new 1965 preservation law. By the late 1960s every one of the three parks that made Coney Island famous was gone. The decades that followed brought arson lots, disinvestment, and a reputation, in the worst years, as a place the city had written off. The films that fixed Coney in the national imagination — *The Warriors* in 1979, *Requiem for a Dream* in 2000 — used it as a landscape of decay and last stops, not glamour.

## The rebirth, and the next thing coming for it

The modern chapter swapped one destruction cycle for a slower one: storm and rezoning. A 2009 city plan carved out a protected amusement district across 19 blocks, from the Parachute Jump to the Aquarium. A Zamperla subsidiary reopened a new Luna Park in 2010 on part of the historic footprint, operating the landmarked Cyclone. The name came back; the thing itself did not, exactly.

Then, in October 2012, Hurricane Sandy drove a storm tide of roughly 14 feet across the peninsula, flooding the rides, Nathan's, the aquarium, and tens of thousands of homes — a preview of what a low-lying sandbar faces as the ocean rises. The current argument is about development: thousands of new apartments going up on a flood-prone frontier while preservationists and longtime residents fight over what Coney Island is even supposed to be now.

Through all of it, the one thing nobody ever managed to destroy was the sand. The beach is city-owned, has never been gated, and stayed open and free the entire time the parks were burning and closing and getting bulldozed. Stand on it today, in front of a 1927 coaster and a dead steel tower, and you are standing on a monument to American mass leisure and to how fast America discards its own monuments — both stacked on the same 4.3 kilometers of public beach.
