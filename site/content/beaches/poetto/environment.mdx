# Two Stories on One Shore: Poetto's Lost Sand and Its Flamingos

Poetto is the kind of beach that teaches you something, because the few hundred meters from its waterline to the salt pans behind it hold two opposite environmental stories at once. On the seaward side is a decades-long struggle to keep a beach from washing away, capped by a notorious botched repair. On the landward side is one of urban Europe's great conservation successes. To understand Poetto you have to walk from one to the other.

## The Sand It Was Famous For

Poetto is the eight-kilometer main beach of Cagliari, the Sardinian capital, running from the limestone headland of the Sella del Diavolo to the edge of Quartu Sant'Elena. For most of the twentieth century its reputation rested on one quality: a powdery, brilliant white sand that Cagliaritani regarded as among the finest anywhere. That sand is the thread of the whole modern story, because the beach has spent decades losing it.

## How the Erosion Started

The decline began with the loss of an institution. For years the beach was lined with **casotti**, colorful wooden huts that were part changing-room and part miniature beach house, a fixture of Cagliari summers. In **1986** they were removed entirely, for sanitation reasons. Their disappearance was not just a cultural loss: combined with overcrowding and a lack of serious erosion-prevention work, it set off a gradual dispersal of the beach's sand and a faster rate of erosion. The famous white strand began, quietly, to thin.

## The Renourishment That Backfired

In **2002**, the authorities tried to fix it, and produced one of the best-known beach-engineering failures in Italy. The plan, the *ripascimento*, was standard practice: dredge sand from deeper water and spread it along the Cagliari end of the beach to rebuild the lost width. The problem was the sand itself. The dredged material was **darker, coarser, and of a completely different consistency** than the fine white sand the city had grown up with, and it changed the appearance of Poetto immediately and unmistakably.

The reaction was severe and, unusually, legal: the administration that had approved the project was taken to court for damages. The renourishment had handed the beach back some width while stripping it of the color that made it famous, in a single operation. And it did not even resolve the underlying problem. Erosion has continued; the beach has been reported to lose on the order of **a meter of sand a year**, with storms delivering further blows, and Cagliari has kept returning to replenishment and prevention plans ever since. It is a textbook case of a fix that solved one problem by creating another, and of how hard, and how political, holding a beach in place actually is.

## The Pink Half of the Story

Now turn around and walk inland, across the road, and the story flips from cautionary tale to triumph. Directly behind Poetto lie the **Molentargius salt pans**, a wetland and former salt-works in the middle of a metropolitan area, now protected as a regional park. Since **1993**, Greater Flamingos have bred here, in the thousands of pairs. Together with the Camargue in France and Fuente de Piedra in Spain, Molentargius is one of the three most important Greater Flamingo breeding colonies in the western Mediterranean, and the birds fly over the city and the beach daily, between the pans and the sea. Cagliari has fairly earned its nickname as a city of flamingos.

That this exists at all, a major flamingo colony thriving inside a European city of around 150,000 people, is a real conservation achievement, the product of protecting the wetland rather than draining or developing it. It is the exact inverse of the beach's own story: where decades of mismanaged engineering have struggled to hold the sand together, simply safeguarding the lagoon has produced thousands of nesting flamingos.

## The Lesson on the Shore

Poetto's value is in holding both halves honestly. It is not the flawless white strand of the old postcards; it is a real, contested, eroding city beach that the city once sued itself over. And it is the seaward edge of one of Europe's great urban bird sanctuaries. The darker sand and the pink birds, the lawsuit and the conservation win, sit a few hundred meters apart on the same shore, and the most honest way to understand a modern Mediterranean city beach is to stand on Poetto and look both ways.
