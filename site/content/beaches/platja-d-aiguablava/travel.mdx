# Platja d'Aiguablava Travel Guide

Platja d'Aiguablava is one of the Costa Brava's most intimate beach experiences—a pine-backed cove that feels both accessible and genuinely Mediterranean. Nestled in the Empordà region of Catalonia, this beach has drawn visitors for decades, offering exceptional clarity, dramatic scenery, and the rare combination of comfort and authenticity that makes the Spanish coast distinctive.

## Getting There

### By Air

Most international visitors arrive at **Girona-Costa Brava Airport (GRO)**, located 37.7 kilometers north of Aiguablava. This regional hub receives flights from across Europe, with particularly good connections from UK and Central European hubs. The drive from GRO to Begur takes approximately 1 hour via the C-66 highway, which cuts inland through agricultural landscape before descending toward the coast.

An alternative option is **Barcelona-El Prat Airport (BCN)**, 140 kilometers south. While farther, BCN offers vastly more flight options and competitive prices, particularly for long-haul connections. The drive from Barcelona to Aiguablava is 2–2.5 hours via the AP-7 toll motorway northbound, then the C-66 toward Begur.

### By Car

**From Girona (GRO)**: Drive southeast on the C-66 toward the coast. The road climbs through cork oak and pine forest, then descends into Begur village. Follow signs to "Platja d'Aiguablava" from the village center; the beach is a 1.8-kilometer drive down a winding coastal road. Drive time: 50–65 minutes depending on traffic through Begur.

**From Barcelona (BCN)**: Take the AP-7 motorway northbound toward France (toll: approximately €20). Exit at junction 6 (Girona-Nord), then follow signs for Begur via the C-66. Total drive time: 2–2.5 hours.

**From France (Perpignan)**: Enter Spain via the AP-7 motorway southbound, exiting at Girona-Nord; follow the C-66 toward Begur. Allow 1.5–2 hours from Perpignan.

### Public Transit

Public transportation to Aiguablava is limited but workable. From Barcelona or Girona, regional bus operators (Sagales, Teisa) run services to Begur village. From Begur, local taxi or ride-share is necessary to reach the beach, as no direct bus service exists. A taxi from Begur to Aiguablava costs approximately €15–20 for a short 2-minute ride. Alternatively, rent a car in Girona or Barcelona; car rental is essential for exploring multiple Costa Brava coves and proves more economical than repeated taxi fares.

### By Car (Essential Context)

**Do not visit the Costa Brava without a car.** The beaches are distributed across 100+ kilometers of coastline, and public transit is minimal. A car allows you to explore multiple coves (Aiguablava, Fornells, Sa Riera, Tamariu) and provides the flexibility that coastal Spain requires.

## Best Time to Visit

### Seasonal Overview

**Summer (June–August)** brings peak heat and crowds. Daytime temperatures reach 28–32°C (82–90°F), with minimal rainfall. Water temperature peaks at 23–25°C (73–77°F), excellent for swimming without a wetsuit. Expect full parking lots, particularly on weekends. July and August are warmest but most congested.

**Spring (April–May)** offers ideal conditions: 20–24°C (68–75°F) air temperature, 16–18°C (61–64°F) water, minimal crowds, and plentiful daylight. Rain is infrequent. Excellent for snorkeling, walking, and photography.

**Fall (September–October)** mirrors spring with comfortable air temperature (18–24°C/64–75°F), water still warm from summer (19–22°C/66–72°F), and declining crowds. September retains summer warmth; October brings more cloud cover but remains pleasant.

**Winter (November–March)** is cool (10–15°C/50–59°F) and occasionally rainy. Water temperature drops to 12–14°C (54–57°F), requiring a wetsuit. However, this is the season of solitude and dramatic light. The pine forests surrounding the beach are lush from recent rain; the water, while cold, is exceptionally clear.

## Where to Stay

### Aiguablava Cove (On-Site)

**Parador Aiguablava** is the only accommodation directly at the beach—a luxury 4-star hotel built into the cliff face above the cove. It's expensive (€200–350/night depending on season) but offers direct beach access and professional service. Book well in advance, particularly for summer weekends. The Parador restaurant is excellent and surprisingly reasonably priced for its setting.

Budget alternative: Small vacation rental apartments exist nearby, though inventory is limited and prices competitive with the Parador outside peak season.

### Begur Village (1.8 km)

Begur itself is a charming medieval hilltop village with narrow stone streets, a castle ruin, and excellent restaurants. It's the logical base for exploring multiple beaches. Accommodation options include:

- **Small rural hotels**: €80–150/night; typically family-run, good local knowledge
- **Vacation rentals (apartments/villas)**: €100–200/night for 1–2 bedrooms; abundant supply
- **Casas rurales** (countryside guesthouses): €70–120/night; rustic charm, local food often included

Begur is 5 minutes by car from Aiguablava, 10 minutes from Sa Riera, and 15 minutes from Tamariu. It's the ideal hub if exploring multiple Costa Brava beaches.

### Sa Riera Beach (4 km west)

Sa Riera is a larger, family-oriented beach with more commercial development. Hotels and rentals are abundant. It's a viable base if you want more amenities (supermarket, restaurants, beach bars) and don't mind a slightly busier atmosphere. Accommodation is typically €80–160/night.

### Tamariu Beach (8 km south)

Tamariu is smaller than Sa Riera, more upscale, and has fewer but pricier accommodations. This is a quieter alternative if crowds concern you and budget allows.

### Palafugell (12 km inland)

For a broader selection of budget hotels and packages tours, Palafugell is a larger town with more conventional tourism infrastructure. It's 15–20 minutes by car from Aiguablava and offers greater choice at lower prices, but loses the charm of beachside locations.

## Costs

### General Price Level

**Beach access**: Free. There is no entrance fee or day-use charge for Platja d'Aiguablava.

**Parking**: Free informal parking exists near the beach entrance, though availability is limited during peak summer. Arrive early or use street parking in Begur and walk.

### Nearby Services

**Food and beverage**: 
- Parador Aiguablava restaurant: €15–25 for lunch, €25–40 for dinner (excellent quality, premium pricing)
- Local cafes and chiringuitos (beach bars) in Begur: €8–15 for casual meals
- Picnic supplies from Begur supermarket: €10–20 per person

**Rentals**: 
- Snorkeling/diving equipment rental: €15–30/day (typically offered through dive centers in Begur or nearby towns)
- Paddleboard rental: Not readily available at Aiguablava; source in Begur or Sa Riera (€30–50/day)

**Accommodation**: 
- Budget vacation rentals: €80–150/night
- Mid-range rural hotels: €120–200/night
- Parador Aiguablava: €200–350/night

**Overall daily budget**: €60–100 per person for a day visit (food + parking + rentals); €150–250+ if staying overnight and dining at quality restaurants.

## Safety

### Swimming Conditions

Platja d'Aiguablava is one of the Costa Brava's safest swimming beaches:

- **Water clarity**: Exceptional. Rocky substrate and minimal sediment create visibility of 10–20 meters on clear days.
- **Currents**: Minimal. The cove's enclosed geography protects against strong currents or dangerous undertows.
- **Tidal range**: Small (1–1.5 meters). Tides are noticeable but not dramatic, and rarely strand swimmers.
- **Water temperature**: 23–25°C (73–77°F) in summer; 12–14°C (54–57°F) in winter. Entirely swimmable without wetsuit in summer; thermal protection advised October–April.
- **Shark incidents**: None recorded. Mediterranean sharks avoid shallow coves and are not aggressive.
- **No lifeguards**: The beach is unguarded. Swim at your own risk, always use the buddy system.

### General Safety

The area is safe for tourists. Begur and surrounding villages have low crime rates. Standard precautions apply:

- Don't leave valuables unattended in cars or on the beach.
- Avoid walking alone at night on unlit coastal paths.
- Be cautious of rocky terrain when entering/exiting the water, particularly at low tide.

### Health Considerations

Water quality is typically excellent. The Spanish government monitors bathing water seasonally. Aiguablava has never been closed for water quality concerns. However, after heavy rainfall, runoff may temporarily affect clarity; check local conditions before visiting.

Sunscreen is essential—Mediterranean sun is intense. The water appears cool enough to be misleading about UV exposure; you will burn quickly.

## Local Tips

**Visit outside peak summer.** May, September, and October offer ideal conditions—warm water, pleasant air, and a fraction of summer crowds. Aiguablava in early June or late August is an excellent compromise: warm enough to swim comfortably, but less packed than peak weeks.

**Arrive by 10 AM on weekends.** Limited parking makes early arrival essential if visiting Saturday or Sunday. Weekday visits offer essentially unlimited parking and a notably different atmosphere.

**Bring water shoes or snorkel booties.** The rocky substrate between beach and deep water is sharp and unforgiving. Proper footwear prevents cuts and makes entry/exit safer.

**The Camí de Ronda is exceptional.** This historic coastal path connects Aiguablava to Fornells Bay (20 minutes) and Sa Riera (45 minutes). Walk the stretch between Aiguablava and Fornells for dramatic views without major effort. Coastal paths in Catalonia are well-maintained and marked.

**Snorkeling directly from Aiguablava's rocks is excellent.** You need not book a dive boat. Simply mask and fins from the beach and explore the rocky substrate 10–20 meters offshore. Visibility is typically excellent; marine life is abundant.

**Fornells Bay is worth a day trip.** Just 20 minutes north along the coastal path, Fornells offers deeper swimming, wreck diving (the remains of a 19th-century sailing ship), and excellent underwater topography. Bring a dive computer if you're certified.

**Medes Islands (Îles Mèdes) are nearby.** Accessible via boat from L'Estartit (20 kilometers north), these protected islands are a premier Mediterranean dive destination. Morning boat dives depart 8–9 AM; return by early afternoon.

**The Parador restaurant is worth the splurge.** Even if not staying overnight, book dinner at the hotel restaurant. The views—sunset over the cove and toward the Medes Islands—combined with excellent Catalan cuisine justify the €40–50 per person cost.

**Winter solitude is a hidden treasure.** If you can tolerate 12–14°C (54–57°F) water and occasional rain, visiting November–February offers an entirely different experience: empty beaches, dramatic lighting, lush vegetation, and a genuine sense of discovery.
