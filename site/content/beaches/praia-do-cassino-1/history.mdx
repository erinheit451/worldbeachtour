# The Longest Beach in the World, and the Asterisk

Praia do Cassino is famous for a single claim, and the claim is more complicated, and more interesting, than the postcard version. This is the story of how a windswept strip of cold-water sand in the far south of Brazil came to be called the longest beach in the world, what that title actually means, and what is really there.

## The Claim

The sand at Cassino runs unbroken from the Molhes, the stone jetties at the entrance of the Rio Grande seaport, south to the mouth of the Chuí Stream on the border with Uruguay. Depending on the source, that distance is given as anywhere from 212 to 254 kilometers. In 1994, Guinness World Records recognized Praia do Cassino as the world's longest beach, and the title has defined the place ever since.

## The Asterisk

The honest version requires an asterisk, and it is the most genuinely interesting thing about the beach. "Longest beach" runs straight into the **coastline paradox**: a shoreline has no single true length, because the more finely you measure its curves and irregularities, the longer the measured line becomes. A beach measured at coarse resolution is shorter than the same beach measured at fine resolution. So "the longest beach in the world" is partly a statement about how, and at what scale, you chose to measure, and about where you decided one beach ends and the next begins.

That is why Cassino's own published length swings by some 40 kilometers between sources, and why other candidates, notably the 80-Mile Beach in Western Australia, have credible competing claims to the title. The defensible statement is not "the longest beach in the world" but "one of the longest uninterrupted sandy beaches on Earth, long enough that the ordinary idea of a beach stops working." You do not stroll Cassino from end to end. People drive it, and once a year runners race 230 kilometers down it in the Cassino Ultra Race.

## A Working, Cold-Water Coast

Cassino is not a tropical beach and has never pretended to be. This is the cool south of Brazil, gaúcho country, and the **Falklands (Malvinas) Current** brings cold water north along the coast, part of the energetic Brazil–Falkland Confluence where cold and warm currents meet. The water is grey and bracing; the wind is near-constant.

It is also a working surface as much as a leisure one. The firm sand functions as a road: people legally drive cars and motorbikes onto it and along it. That is less a charming novelty than a real hazard, and the standing local advice, four-wheel drive, check the tide before you set out, carry recovery gear, exists because vehicles get bogged in soft patches and caught by the incoming tide on a beach with no high ground to retreat to.

## The Molhes and the Sailing Cart

The northern end of the beach belongs to the **Molhes da Barra**, breakwaters of piled stone built out into the sea in the early twentieth century to protect the entrance of the Rio Grande port. They are the beach's signature structure, and they come with one of the strangest sights on any beach in the world: a **wind-powered sail-cart** that runs about 4.3 kilometers out along the western jetty to the lighthouse, taking roughly 20 minutes, a sailing vehicle on rails over the open sea.

## The Wreck

Sixteen kilometers south of the town center sits the rusting hull of the **Altair**, a ship driven ashore by a storm in June 1976 and stranded ever since. Nearly fifty years later it is still there, corroding in place, an accidental monument and one of the only fixed landmarks on a beach that otherwise gives the eye almost nothing to hold. On a coastline defined by emptiness and length, the wreck has become a destination in its own right.

## An Old Resort at the Edge of a Wilderness

None of this is new. **Rio Grande**, the oldest city in the state, was founded in 1737, and Cassino opened as a tourist complex on **26 January 1890**, developed by the Suburban Mangueira Company, which makes it one of the oldest beach resorts in Brazil. The developed northern end at Cassino town, busy through the December-to-March southern summer with around 150,000 visitors a year, is a genuine and venerable seaside resort. It simply happens to be the doorway to a few hundred kilometers of near-empty, wind-scoured wilderness that runs south until it quietly becomes Uruguay.

That is the real Cassino: not the prettiest beach, and not unambiguously the longest, but one of the most honest superlatives in the world, a place whose contested record matters less than the plain, elemental fact of its scale.
