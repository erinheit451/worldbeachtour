# Long Beach Travel Guide

Long Beach is one of North America's most dramatic and powerful coastal destinations—a 16-kilometer expanse of sand and sea that marks the intersection of old-growth rainforest and the Pacific Ocean. Located on Vancouver Island's west coast between Tofino and Ucluelet, Long Beach has become a pilgrimage site for surfers seeking world-class waves, travelers pursuing authentic wilderness experience, and anyone drawn to the raw beauty of the Pacific coast. This guide covers the practical logistics of reaching this remote and remarkable beach.

## Getting There

### From Vancouver (YVR)

Long Beach is approximately 200 kilometers from Vancouver by direct distance but requires over 4 hours of driving and ferry transit to reach. Most visitors follow this route:

**Option 1: BC Ferries + Highway 4 (Most Common)**

Fly into **Vancouver International Airport (YVR)** and rent a car. Drive north to **Tsawwassen Ferry Terminal** (approximately 1 hour south of downtown Vancouver). Board the **BC Ferries** Tsawwassen to Nanaimo crossing (approximately 95 minutes—ferries operate multiple times daily).

From Nanaimo, drive west on **Highway 4 (Pacific Rim Highway)** toward Tofino—a scenic but demanding drive of approximately 135 kilometers taking 2–2.5 hours. Highway 4 is a single-lane winding road through temperate rainforest with limited passing opportunities. Drive time varies significantly with weather and traffic; allow extra time during winter or when following recreational vehicles.

Highway 4 features several pullouts and scenic overlooks worth the stop: Cathedral Grove (old-growth Douglas fir forest) and Kennedy Lake are compelling diversions that add 30–60 minutes but provide crucial context for the region's ecology.

**Option 2: Direct Flight to Tofino**

**Tofino Airport (YAZ)** receives direct service from Vancouver on Pacific Coastal Airlines and other regional carriers—approximately 1 hour flight time. This eliminates the ferry entirely and reduces total travel time from Vancouver to under 3 hours (including airport procedures). Flight prices are higher ($150–300 return) but justify themselves for those with limited time or strong aversion to driving Highway 4.

From Tofino Airport, Long Beach is 30 minutes south via Highway 4.

### From Seattle (SEA)

Seattle-Tacoma International Airport (SEA) requires either flying through Vancouver or driving north through Washington, crossing into British Columbia at Blaine, and continuing to Vancouver before the ferry/highway sequence outlined above. Total time from SEA is 6–8 hours depending on border conditions.

### Highway 4 Conditions

Highway 4 (Pacific Rim Highway) is the critical link once ferry travel concludes. This is a narrow, winding mountain road through rainforest with:

- Limited passing opportunities (single lane in both directions for most of the route)
- Variable weather—fog, rain, and occasional snow in winter
- No services between Parksville and Tofino (approximately 140 kilometers)
- Seasonal closures possible during severe winter weather

Winter travel (November–February) should be approached cautiously. Bring chains or winter tires, allow extra driving time, and check road conditions in advance. Summer travel (June–August) is straightforward but expect slower speeds due to recreational traffic.

## Best Time to Visit

### Seasonal Overview

**Summer (June–August)** is peak season in terms of access and weather. Daytime temperatures average 18–22°C (64–72°F), with long daylight hours (sunset after 9:30 PM in June/July). Rain is minimal. Pacific water temperatures reach their yearly maximum around 12–14°C (54–57°F)—still cold but more tolerable for surfers in light wetsuits. Expect moderate crowds, particularly on weekends. Accommodation books 2–3 months in advance.

**Fall (September–October)** brings declining crowds while maintaining reasonable weather. September remains warm (15–18°C) with occasional summer-like conditions. October transitions to fall patterns: temperatures drop to 10–14°C, rain increases, but storm watching begins. This is arguably the ideal window for those seeking both reasonable weather and fewer visitors.

**Winter (November–February)** is the definitive storm season and storm-watching peak. Temperatures plunge to 5–10°C (41–50°F) with frequent rain and dramatic cloud patterns. However, this is when Long Beach reveals its true power. Atmospheric rivers generate enormous Pacific swells, wind speeds exceed 60 km/h regularly, and the beach takes on a primal, almost apocalyptic character. Accommodation is abundant and affordable. Surfers and storm enthusiasts treat winter as the ultimate season.

**Spring (March–May)** is transitional and unpredictable, with temperatures rising from 8–15°C and rainfall remaining elevated. Spring has fewer visitors than summer but less drama than winter. Useful primarily as a shoulder season for those seeking solitude and moderate conditions.

## Where to Stay

Long Beach itself has no accommodation directly on the sand. The nearest villages—Tofino (10 kilometers north) and Ucluelet (15 kilometers south)—provide the full range of lodging options.

### Tofino

Tofino is the larger, more developed village and the default base for Long Beach visitors. As a former logging town pivoted toward tourism, Tofino offers:

- **Hotels and motels**: Range from budget (C$60–100/night) to mid-range (C$120–200/night) to upscale resorts (C$200–400+/night)
- **Vacation rentals**: Abundant through platforms like Airbnb and Vrbo; typically C$100–250/night for 1–2 bedrooms
- **Hostels and backpacker lodges**: C$30–50/night for dormitory beds
- **Bed & breakfasts**: C$80–150/night with variable amenities

Tofino has restaurants, grocery stores, and services. It functions as the regional hub. The drive to Long Beach's main access points is 15–30 minutes.

### Ucluelet

Ucluelet ("Safe Harbour" in Nuu-chah-nulth) is smaller and quieter than Tofino, with fewer accommodation options but maintained charm. Similar pricing as Tofino but less crowding. Ucluelet is home to the Wild Pacific Trail (8 kilometers of spectacular coastal walking) and is marginally closer to Long Beach's southern sections.

### Pacific Rim National Park Green Point Campground

**Green Point Campground**, located directly adjacent to Long Beach within Pacific Rim National Park Reserve, offers the most immersive accommodation option:

- **Campsites**: C$24–32/night for regular sites; some waterfront sites available
- **Facilities**: Flush toilets, tap water, fire pits
- **Capacity**: Approximately 95 sites; books solid during summer via Parks Canada website

Camping at Green Point places you seconds from the beach and connected directly to park facilities and the Rainforest Trail (a spectacular old-growth walk). Winter camping is possible but demands experience with coastal weather and well-maintained equipment.

### Accommodation Booking Strategy

- **Summer (June–August)**: Book 8–12 weeks in advance for preferred properties; prices peak and availability tightens significantly
- **Fall/Spring (September–May)**: Book 4–6 weeks in advance; far more availability and lower prices
- **Winter (November–February)**: Book 2–4 weeks in advance; prices are lowest (C$60–120/night for many properties) and availability is highest

## Costs

### Access & Park Fees

Long Beach is part of **Pacific Rim National Park Reserve**. Day-use access requires a **Parks Canada day pass** (C$7.80 per adult as of 2024; subject to change). Annual passes are available for C$65.

Parking at Long Beach parking areas is free (included with day-pass purchase). Highway 4 is free to drive. BC Ferries Tsawwassen-Nanaimo costs approximately C$19 per adult foot passenger or C$60–70 per car plus driver.

### Accommodation (Per Night)

- Budget (hostels, basic motels): C$40–80
- Mid-range (vacation rentals, small hotels): C$100–200
- Upscale (resort hotels): C$250–400+
- Camping (Green Point): C$24–32

### Food & Services

- Groceries (from Tofino/Ucluelet): C$12–25 per meal per person for picnic provisions
- Restaurant meals: C$15–25 for casual lunch, C$35–60+ for dinner at upscale establishments
- Rental equipment (surfboard, wetsuit): C$20–40/day or C$60–100/day for high-end boards

## Safety Considerations

Long Beach is a wild Pacific coast. Conditions demand respect:

### Cold Water

Pacific water temperatures never exceed 14°C, even in summer. Hypothermia is a genuine risk for those immersed longer than 10–15 minutes without adequate protection. **A 4–5mm wetsuit is strongly recommended year-round; a 6mm or hooded suit is essential in winter.** Many surfers wear suits even in August.

### Rip Currents and Strong Tides

Long Beach experiences tidal ranges exceeding 4 meters. Strong tidal currents flow near headlands and can exceed 2 knots. Swimmers should avoid entering at times of tidal transition and should always be aware of current direction and strength.

### Logs and Debris

The intertidal zone is littered with massive driftwood logs—some partially buried in sand, others resting against rocks or other logs. These logs are unpredictable hazards that can shift with waves and tides. The original explorer Juan Pérez noted that logs were a navigational hazard for early trading vessels; they remain a hazard for anyone not watching carefully.

### Weather Changes

Coastal weather changes with remarkable speed. Clear conditions can transition to fog, rain, or high winds within minutes. Hypothermia and disorientation are genuine risks for those inadequately prepared. Check weather and tide forecasts obsessively.

### Wildlife

Roosevelt elk occasionally appear on the beach, particularly at dusk and dawn. Approach distances should be greater than 100 meters. Sea lions and harbor seals are common and generally unproblematic, but do not approach or attempt to hand-feed. Black bears are present in adjacent forests but sightings on the beach itself are exceptionally rare.

## Tips & Highlights

### Rainforest Trail

The **Rainforest Trail** is a 0.8-kilometer easy loop through old-growth temperate rainforest immediately adjacent to Long Beach. This walk provides crucial ecological context—massive Sitka spruce and western red cedar trees, nurse logs supporting younger generation growth, and a sense of the forest ecosystem that frames the beach. Budget 45 minutes; the trail is boardwalked and accessible year-round.

### Wild Pacific Trail (Ucluelet)

If based in Ucluelet, the **Wild Pacific Trail** is a 8-kilometer network of coastal walks featuring spectacular rock formations, tide pools, and coastal views. This complementary experience to Long Beach's sandy expanses is walkable in sections and provides intimate access to the region's geology.

### Hot Springs Cove

**Hot Springs Cove** is accessible by floatplane or boat charter from Tofino (approximately C$300–400 per person for guided tours). The cove features natural thermal springs discharged into an ocean-fed pool, creating a unique experience of soaking in hot water while surrounded by cold ocean and temperate rainforest.

### Surfing

Long Beach is world-renowned for consistent, powerful Pacific swells. The beach breaks and reef breaks along the 16-kilometer expanse offer options for surfers of all levels, though winter swells (6–10 meters) demand advanced skills. Summer swells are smaller (2–4 meters) and suit intermediate surfers. Equipment rental and lessons are available in Tofino.

### Tide Pools

At low tide, extensive tide pools are exposed, revealing sea stars, anemones, chitons, and small fish. Exploration requires caution—rocks are slippery, and falling into deeper pools is hazardous. Respect marine organisms and minimize disturbance.

### Photography

Long Beach offers exceptional photography opportunities: dramatic headlands, old-growth forest, powerful wave action, and moody coastal weather. Storm season (November–February) offers the most dramatic lighting and conditions but demands weather resistance and safety consciousness.
