Almyrida is a family-oriented beach in the municipality of Apokoronas, on the eastern side of Souda Bay, about 22 kilometers east of Chania on Crete's north coast. Its shoreline is two low sandy beaches separated by a short rocky spit topped with a small white chapel, opening onto the Cretan Sea. The beach sits at the western edge of Almyrida village, a former fishing settlement that has grown into one of the developed holiday corners of Apokoronas.

What distinguishes Almyrida is not scale but geometry: the bay stays shallow and warm well offshore, which makes it a genuine option for families with young children, cautious swimmers, and paddleboarders, and a beginner windsurfing spot rather than a surf beach. The seabed is predominantly sand with patches of Posidonia seagrass, a protected marine habitat. The hinterland of Apokoronas is olive-growing country of groves and stone villages, though the immediate village is now ringed with villas and rental apartments.

To the south, the White Mountains (Lefka Ori), which rise to about 2,453 meters at Pachnes, are visible from the beach and are snow-capped from late autumn into spring, a hard contrast above the warm bay. The beach faces roughly north, so it is exposed to winter northerlies; the biggest seas here come in February, and the calmest month is June.

The water temperature varies dramatically by season: 16–17°C in winter (November–March), rising to 24–25°C in summer (July–August). The bay's calm conditions make it ideal for beginner swimmers from May through October. Tidal range is negligible (Mediterranean tides average 30–40 centimeters), meaning the waterline remains consistent throughout the day.

Almyrida's beachfront is organized rather than wild: public facilities (restrooms, showers), rows of rented umbrellas and beds, a strip of waterfront tavernas known regionally for fresh fish, and a windsurf and watersports rental operation on the sand. There are no high-rise hotels; the accommodation is villas and rental apartments spread through the village and the hills behind it. The rhythm is seasonal: quiet and largely local in winter, busy with families and day-trippers from Chania through the summer.

**Most Relevant Lenses:**

**Travel & Recreation** — Almyrida functions as a day-trip destination for Chania visitors and a base for those avoiding the busier beaches of Elafonissi or Balos. The drive from Chania is about 22 kilometers (roughly 30 to 40 minutes) via Kalyves, straightforward but enough to keep casual tourism manageable.

**History** — At the village's eastern edge lies the open-air mosaic floor of a three-aisled Christian basilica of the second half of the 6th century AD, excavated 1974-1989, the surviving trace of an early Byzantine town destroyed in 7th-century raids. Offshore, the islet of Karga carries ancient wall ruins and German bunkers from the 1941 Battle of Crete.

**Family** — The shallow bay, sand bottom, minimal currents, and calm conditions from May–September create a genuinely safe environment for children learning to swim. The absence of aggressive water sports keeps the water space uncrowded. Nearby tavernas cater explicitly to families rather than nightlife-seeking backpackers.

**Environment** — Patches of protected Posidonia seagrass grow over the sandy bottom, a habitat that supports fish populations. The islet of Karga, directly offshore, is a nesting and resting site for seabirds and a target for small-boat excursions; visitors are asked not to tear up or anchor in the seagrass beds.

**Accessibility** — Unlike cliffside beaches (Navagio, Cala Goloritze), Almyrida is reachable via standard vehicle, has dedicated parking, and requires zero scrambling. This makes it a legitimate option for elderly visitors and those with mobility constraints.

Almyrida Beach works because it doesn't attempt to be Mykonos or claim to be untouched—it's a functional Mediterranean beach where families actually swim, locals actually eat, and the landscape provides enough grandeur to remind you where you are.
