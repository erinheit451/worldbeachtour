# Almyrida Beach Travel Guide

Almyrida is a shallow, sheltered bay on the eastern side of Souda Bay, about 22 kilometers east of Chania on Crete's north coast, in the municipality of Apokoronas. Its shoreline is two low sandy beaches divided by a short rocky spit topped with a small white chapel. The water stays shallow and warm well offshore, which is why the village works better as a family and beginner-windsurf base than as a surf beach. It suits both a day trip from Chania and a quiet week's stay.

## Getting There

### By Air

Most visitors fly into **Chania International Airport (CHQ)** on the Akrotiri peninsula. It is close in a straight line, about 10 kilometers, but the road wraps around Souda Bay, so allow roughly 25 to 35 minutes by car. CHQ is a compact airport that handles seasonal European flights alongside domestic routes.

**Heraklion International Airport (HER)**, Crete's largest, is about 120 kilometers east, roughly 2 to 2.5 hours by road on the north-coast national road. HER carries more international routes and can be cheaper, at the cost of a much longer ground transfer.

From either airport a rental car is the most practical way in. From CHQ you drive around Souda Bay and take the national road (VOAK) east to the Kalyves exit, then a short local road to Almyrida. From HER the route runs west via Rethymno toward Chania, leaving the highway at Kalyves.

### By Car

From Chania town the drive is about 22 kilometers: east on the VOAK national road, off at the Kalyves exit, then through Kalyves to Almyrida. Allow 30 to 40 minutes in normal conditions; July and August weekend traffic can add 10 to 15 minutes.

- **From Rethymno** (about 45 km): national road west toward Chania, exit at Kalyves; roughly 40 minutes.
- **From Heraklion** (about 120 km): national road west via Rethymno, exit at Kalyves; about 2 to 2.5 hours.
- **From southern Crete** (Chora Sfakion, Plakias): drive north to the national road, then as above; 2 to 3 hours depending on start point.

Parking at Almyrida is free, in informal areas along the beachfront and in the village. Spaces fill on summer weekends, so arriving before mid-morning helps. The village is small enough to cross on foot once you have parked.

### By Public Transport

The KTEL Chania regional network runs a coastal line through Apokoronas that serves Kalyves and Almyrida. On weekdays there are several departures spread through the day, fewer at weekends and in winter; the ride from Chania takes roughly an hour. The Almyrida stop is on the main road by the bakery, and tickets are bought on the bus. Schedules shift by season, so confirm current times with KTEL Chania (e-ktel.com) before relying on the bus. Reaching Almyrida from Heraklion or Rethymno means changing at Chania and adds a few hours, so the bus is workable for flexible travelers but not for tight day trips.

### By Boat

There is no ferry to Almyrida. Small private boats can anchor off the beach in calm to moderate conditions, common from May to September, but the bay is open to the north and a strong northerly builds swell quickly. Boats should avoid anchoring on the protected Posidonia seagrass beds visible over the sandy bottom.

## Best Time to Visit

**Summer (June to August)** is the warm, busy season. Sea temperature reaches about 24 to 25°C, warm enough for long swims without a wetsuit, and rain is rare. Crowds build in July and August, especially on weekends and when day-trippers come east from Chania. Early June and late August are the quieter edges of the warm window.

**Spring (April to May)** is mild, with light crowds and abundant wildflowers in the Apokoronas hills, but the sea is still cool, around 18 to 21°C, so swimming is bracing. It is a good stretch for walking the villages and groves inland.

**Fall (September to October)** mirrors spring on land but keeps warmer water: swimming stays comfortable through September before the sea cools and the first autumn rains arrive in October.

**Winter (November to March)** is the local season. The village is quiet, many seasonal tavernas close, and the sea drops to about 16 to 17°C. The bay faces roughly north, so the biggest seas of the year come in winter, peaking around February, while the White Mountains to the south carry snow.

## Where to Stay

Almyrida has no high-rise hotels and no large resort complexes; the accommodation is villas, self-catering apartments, and small family-run rooms and studios spread through the village and the slopes behind it. That is a deliberate character of the place rather than an oversight, and it shapes how you stay here.

- **In Almyrida village.** Staying in the village puts you within a short walk of the beach and the seafront tavernas. Expect family-run studios and apartments and privately let holiday houses; many are booked directly by owners or through the usual rental platforms. Book several weeks ahead for July and August, when the village is at its fullest.
- **Kalyves**, about 4 kilometers west, is the nearest larger settlement, with a wider spread of rooms, apartments, and small family hotels, a beach of its own, and more shops and dining. It is a short drive from Almyrida and a reasonable base if the village is full.
- **Chania**, about 22 kilometers west, offers the full range from budget rooms to higher-end hotels, plus the Venetian old town, nightlife, and varied dining. From here Almyrida is a 30 to 40 minute drive, which works for a day-trip base but means driving back after dinner.

Because the beach is small and the water sports are low-key, most people who want beach time choose the village or Kalyves and treat Chania as the option for a busier stay.

## Costs

The beach is free to enter. Sunbeds and umbrellas on the organized stretches are rented from the tavernas and operators, and parking is free.

- **Food and drink.** Seafront tavernas serve grilled fish, Cretan mains, and mezes; a main course typically runs in the low-to-mid teens of euros, a Greek salad and water a good deal less, and a coffee a couple of euros. The village has a small market for picnic supplies.
- **Windsurf and watersports rental.** Beach operators rent windsurf gear by the hour or half-day, with beginner-friendly kit and life vests; expect roughly 25 euros for an hour of windsurf rental, more for longer sessions or lessons.
- **Car rental.** A small economy car from Chania or Heraklion is broadly in the range of 35 to 60 euros a day, higher in peak summer.
- **Bus.** The Chania–Almyrida fare is a few euros one way, paid on the bus.

As a rough guide, a self-catering day with one taverna meal runs somewhere around 50 to 100 euros per person before lodging, depending on how you eat and drink.

## Safety

Almyrida's two beaches are organized and hold a Blue Flag, the award that requires water quality, safety provision, and beach management to meet a set standard. Seasonal lifeguards are posted during the summer months; outside that season there is no supervision, so swim within your limits.

- **Swimming.** The bay is shallow and sandy, slopes gently, and sits well inside Souda Bay, so it is protected and usually calm. That is the whole reason it draws families and beginner swimmers. A strong north wind is the exception: it pushes waves and chop into the bay, and on those days the water can turn rougher than the shallow setting suggests.
- **Currents.** No significant rip currents are documented in this enclosed, shallow bay. The main hazard is wind-driven chop on northerly days rather than current.
- **Water quality.** The beach rates in the top, "excellent" category under EU bathing-water monitoring, consistent with its Blue Flag status. Post-rainfall issues are uncommon given the small settlement and low runoff.
- **Sun.** Mediterranean summer sun here is strong, with UV index often in the 8 to 10 range; the fenced basilica enclosure inland has no shade, so cover up if you walk over to it in the middle of the day.
- **Medical.** The nearest hospital is in Chania, about 22 kilometers and 40 minutes by car; Kalyves has a small clinic. Serious cases are transferred to Chania.

## Nearby

- **The Almyrida basilica.** At the village's eastern edge, a fenced, unroofed enclosure holds the mosaic floor of a three-aisled Christian basilica of the second half of the 6th century AD, excavated 1974 to 1989. It is a short walk from the sand and largely unsignposted; low morning or late-afternoon light makes the crosses, peacocks, and fish in the floor easiest to read.
- **Karga islet.** The small islet directly offshore carries ancient wall ruins of disputed date and concrete bunkers from the German coastal defenses of 1941. It is a seabird habitat, reachable only by small boat, and is unmanaged.
- **Kalyves** (about 4 km west) has its own beach, a river mouth, and more shops and tavernas.
- **Apokoronas villages.** Inland, Vamos, Gavalochori, and the surrounding stone villages sit in olive-growing country and are worth a slow drive for tavernas and walking.
- **Georgioupoli** (about 15 km east) is a longer sandy beach at the head of Almyros Bay, the site generally identified with the ancient port of Amphimalla.
- **Chania old town** (about 22 km west) is the region's main draw for its Venetian harbor, market, and dining.

## Local Tips

- **Arrive early on summer weekends** for parking and a quieter beach; it fills through the late morning in July and August.
- **Bring cash.** Some tavernas and rentals take cards, but not all, and there is no ATM in the village; the nearest are in Kalyves and Chania.
- **Respect the Posidonia seagrass.** The dark patches over the sandy bottom are protected marine habitat; don't tear it up or anchor boats in it.
- **The windsurfing is for beginners.** When the summer Meltemi is not blowing, an afternoon thermal westerly builds across Souda Bay, enough for learners and rentals over flat water or light chop; experienced sailors go to other Cretan spots.
- **Look south for the White Mountains** (Lefka Ori), roughly 30 kilometers away and snow-capped from late autumn into spring.
