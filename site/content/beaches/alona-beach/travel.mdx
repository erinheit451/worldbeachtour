# Alona Beach Travel Guide

Alona Beach on Panglao Island is the Philippines' premier dive hub, a working beach where serious water sports meet tropical accessibility. Unlike many Philippine resort beaches, Alona's identity is rooted in what lies beneath the surface—the Balicasag Island Marine Sanctuary and the broader coral ecosystems that make the Central Visayas a world-class diving destination. Whether you're a certified diver seeking underwater walls, a snorkeler exploring shallow reefs, or a traveler using Panglao as a base for exploring Bohol's Chocolate Hills and spinner dolphin populations, Alona delivers the infrastructure, expertise, and access to make it work.

## Getting There

### By Air

The opening of **Bohol-Panglao International Airport (TAG)** in late 2023 transformed access to the area. The airport sits just 2.8 kilometers from Alona Beach—roughly a 10-minute drive by taxi or tricycle. Direct flights now connect to Manila (1.5 hours), Cebu (40 minutes), Davao, and select international routes from East Asia and Australia.

If flying from North America or Europe, your typical routing is: international gateway (usually Manila or Cebu) → regional hub (Manila or Cebu) → TAG. Total travel time is 18–24 hours depending on connections.

### By Ferry

For travelers already in the Visayas, ferry routes from Cebu City to Tagbilaran (Bohol's capital) remain popular. The 2.5-hour ferry crossing operates multiple times daily; cost is approximately 600–800 PHP (US$11–15). Tagbilaran sits 20 kilometers northeast of Alona Beach—a 30–40 minute drive by tricycle, taxi, or tour van. Many dive operators offer airport/port pickup as part of package bookings.

### From Airport/Ferry to Alona

From TAG Airport: Taxis, Grab (app-based ride-sharing), or hotel shuttle services will run 150–400 PHP (US$3–8). Negotiated tricycle rates are often cheaper (100–200 PHP) if you're comfortable with open-air three-wheelers. The drive is straightforward—the airport is on Panglao Island's northern shore; Alona is on the southern shore, roughly 15 minutes across the island.

From Tagbilaran Port: Similar options apply. Most dive operators and mid-range hotels offer airport/port transfers; factor this into package costs upfront rather than arranging independently.

## Best Time to Visit

The Philippines operates on a tropical maritime climate. Alona's diving conditions and weather patterns reflect this.

### Seasonal Overview

**March–May (Dry Season)** is the golden window. Daytime temperatures hold at 30–32°C (86–90°F), trade winds are light to moderate, and water visibility peaks at 20–40 meters. Seas are calm; boat access to Balicasag and other sites is nearly guaranteed. Rain is rare. This is peak diving season—expect higher accommodation costs and busy dive shops. Water temperature is warm (27–28°C / 81–82°F).

**June–October (Rainy/Monsoon Season)** brings afternoon squalls and increasing swell. Morning visibility can still exceed 15 meters, but afternoon conditions deteriorate. Humidity climbs. Some marginal dive days occur when morning boats launch successfully but afternoon reschedules happen. Typhoon season peaks July–November; major storms are rare but possible, with September–October carrying elevated risk. Accommodation is cheaper; dive shops less crowded. Water remains warm (26–27°C / 79–81°F).

**November–February (Cool Dry Season)** offers stable weather and good visibility (20–30 meters). Daytime temperatures drop to 26–29°C (79–84°F), and water temperature dips slightly (25–27°C / 77–81°F). Seas remain generally calm. This is a secondary peak for divers seeking fewer crowds than March–May. Comfortable for non-divers too.

## Where to Stay

Alona Beach offers a spectrum of accommodations, from dive-resort packages to casual beachfront hotels and backpacker operations. The beach itself is compact—roughly 800 meters—so most lodging is within walking distance of the main strip.

### Alona Beach (On the Beach)

The central beachfront strip is packed with hotels, dive resorts, and casual inns. This is where the energy is: dive shops, restaurants, water sports operators, and the main swimming/snorkeling area. Most visitors stay here.

- **Dive Resorts** (upscale): 3,500–8,000 PHP per night (US$65–150). All-inclusive packages bundling accommodation, meals, and daily dives run 4,500–10,000 PHP per person per night. These are the choice for serious diving trips; you can schedule dives before breakfast and between meals. Examples lean toward higher-end operators with modern facilities.

- **Mid-Range Beachfront Hotels**: 1,500–3,500 PHP per night (US$28–65). Comfortable, often with air conditioning, private bathrooms, and views. Walking distance to dive shops and restaurants.

- **Budget Beachfront Inns**: 600–1,500 PHP per night (US$11–28). Fan-cooled or basic A/C, shared or private bathrooms, minimal amenities. Atmosphere-rich, popular with backpackers and budget divers.

### Panglao Island (Broader)

Beyond Alona, Panglao Island has quieter beaches and secondary dive areas. Dauin (southern Panglao) and Dumaluan Beach (eastern Panglao) offer different experiences:

- **Dauin**: Muck diving specialization, fewer crowds, more local feel. 1,200–3,000 PHP per night for decent accommodation.
- **Dumaluan**: White sand beach, quieter, family-oriented. 1,500–4,000 PHP per night.

These are 15–20 minutes from Alona by tricycle; useful if you want diving but also solitude.

### Tagbilaran City

Bohol's capital, 20 kilometers northeast, offers more conventional hotels and city amenities. 1,800–4,500 PHP per night for mid-range options. Useful if you're combining Alona diving with Chocolate Hills tours and want a quieter base camp, but it adds 30–40 minutes each way to daily diving logistics.

### Accommodation Booking Strategy

For divers: Book a dive-resort package through your dive shop or online aggregators (Booking.com, Agoda, Divvydivers) 4–6 weeks ahead, especially for March–May. Packages are often cheaper than paying separately and guarantee dive spot coordination.

For mixed activities (diving + Chocolate Hills + casual beach time): Mid-range beachfront hotels on Alona offer the best balance—central location, walkable beach, access to dive shops, restaurants, and tour operators.

Book ahead during March–May and December–January; off-season (June–October) offers walk-in availability and better rates.

## Costs

### General Price Level

The Philippines is one of Southeast Asia's most affordable beach destinations. Alona reflects this: accommodation, food, and activities are substantially cheaper than comparable offerings in Thailand or Indonesia.

### Accommodation

- **Budget beachfront**: 600–1,500 PHP/night (US$11–28)
- **Mid-range beachfront**: 1,500–3,500 PHP/night (US$28–65)
- **Upscale dive resort**: 3,500–8,000 PHP/night (US$65–150)
- **All-inclusive dive packages**: 4,500–10,000 PHP per person/night (US$85–190)

### Food

- **Street food / casual warung**: 50–150 PHP (US$1–3) per meal
- **Local restaurant lunch**: 200–400 PHP (US$4–8) per meal
- **Beachfront/tourist restaurant dinner**: 500–1,500 PHP (US$9–28) per entree
- **Groceries for self-catering**: Modest budget, widely available at small markets

### Diving

- **Dive shop certification (PADI Open Water)**: 15,000–18,000 PHP (US$280–340)
- **Guided dive (boat, 1 dive)**: 2,500–4,000 PHP (US$47–75)
- **5–10 dive packages**: 10,000–15,000 PHP total (US$190–280)
- **Snorkel trips to Balicasag**: 600–1,200 PHP (US$11–23) per person; includes boat, guide, lunch

### Activities & Transportation

- **Chocolate Hills day tour**: 1,500–2,500 PHP (US$28–47) per person (includes van, guide, park entry)
- **Dolphin-watching sunrise tour**: 1,500–2,000 PHP (US$28–38) per person
- **Tricycle rental (hourly)**: 300–600 PHP (US$5.50–11)
- **Grab/taxi to airport**: 150–400 PHP (US$3–8)

### Overall Budget Scenarios

- **Budget traveler (backpacker, dive-focused)**: 2,000–3,500 PHP/day (US$38–65) — accommodation + food + 1 dive + transport
- **Mid-range (comfort + diving)**: 4,500–7,000 PHP/day (US$85–130) — mid-range hotel + restaurant meals + 1 dive + activities
- **Upscale (resort package + all meals)**: 9,000–15,000 PHP/day (US$170–280) — all-inclusive dive resort

The Philippines' affordability and the concentration of infrastructure at Alona make it one of the world's best-value diving destinations.

## Safety

### Water Safety

**Diving & Snorkeling**: Balicasag's reefs are well-established and properly managed. The marine sanctuary has banned destructive fishing, and conditions are monitored. For divers:

- Always dive with certified operators (PADI is universal standard; check operator credentials)
- Respect depth limits; Balicasag's walls drop beyond recreational limits quickly
- Avoid touching coral; it's fragile and legally protected
- Watch for sea urchins in shallow areas and around coral rubble

For snorkelers:

- Shallow reef areas (Alona's house reef and Balicasag's shallows) are beginner-friendly
- Water clarity varies; mornings are typically better than afternoons
- Reef shoes are advised; sharp coral and sea urchins are present

**Hazards**: Barracuda sightings are rare; sharks are not aggressive. Sea snakes exist in deeper waters but avoid snorkeling/diving areas. Saltwater crocodiles are not found on Panglao or Bohol.

### Beach Safety

**General Security**: Alona Beach and the surrounding Panglao Island are considered safe for tourists. Petty theft (from unattended bags on the beach) occurs occasionally; don't leave valuables unattended. Violent crime targeting tourists is rare.

**Environmental Hazards**:
- **Sun exposure**: Tropical UV is intense. Reef-safe sunscreen is required (mineral-based, no oxybenzone/octinoxate, as these damage coral).
- **Dehydration**: Drink more water than you think necessary; the heat is deceptive when you're in water.
- **Typhoons**: July–November carries elevated tropical storm risk. The Philippines has excellent weather forecasting; monitor updates. If a typhoon warning is issued, boats won't launch and the beach may be unsafe due to rip currents.

### Health Considerations

**Water Quality**: Alona's marine sanctuary maintains ecological standards. Saltwater contact is safe for minor cuts; rinse with fresh water afterward.

**Food Safety**: Restaurants catering to tourists maintain reasonable hygiene. Eat where other tourists are eating. Street food is generally safe if busy (high turnover = fresh food). Avoid tap water; drink bottled water (widely available, cheap).

**Dive-Specific Health**:
- Ensure you're certified for the depths you're diving. Balicasag's deep walls require Advanced certification and experience.
- Decompression sickness (the bends) is rare with responsible operators, but dive insurance (DAN—Divers Alert Network) is strongly advised.
- If you have pre-existing medical conditions (heart, lung, ear), get physician clearance before diving.

### Local Customs & Etiquette

- Filipinos are exceptionally hospitable; basic courtesy and respect for local traditions goes a long way.
- Dress modestly away from the beach; swimwear is beach-only attire.
- Ask permission before photographing local people.
- Tipping is not obligatory but appreciated for good service (5–10% is standard at restaurants).

## Local Tips

**Book dives in advance during peak season.** March–April sees dive shops fill up by mid-morning, especially for Balicasag. Reserve the night before or as part of your accommodation package.

**Dive early.** Boat launches happen at 6–7 AM for morning dives; visibility is best before afternoon plankton blooms. Night dives are also offered; they're magical but require experience and a confident operator.

**Do Balicasag Island snorkeling or diving early.** The marine sanctuary is stunning but can get crowded by mid-morning with tour boats. An early departure (6–6:30 AM boat) means fewer people and better light.

**Virgin Island sandbar at low tide.** A small island off Panglao's east coast disappears and reappears with tides. Timing your visit for low tide (check tide charts online) means a bigger sandbar and better photos. Tours include this as a secondary stop; request it specifically.

**Chocolate Hills are best at sunrise.** Most tours depart 6–7 AM and reach the viewpoint by 7–8 AM. The light is superior, it's cooler, and the scene is less crowded. Book the night before through your hotel or a local operator.

**Dolphin watching at dawn.** Spinner dolphins hunt in Bohol Strait early morning (5–8 AM). Tours depart Alona at 5 AM and return by 8–9 AM, leaving your day open for diving or other activities. Success rate (actually seeing dolphins) is high but not guaranteed; go with realistic expectations.

**Coral-safe sunscreen is mandatory.** Reef-safe, mineral-based sunscreen only. Sunscreen chemicals damage coral. Bring yours from home or buy at the dive shop (more expensive). Reef shirts or wetsuits provide additional protection and reduce sunburn.

**Cash is king in rural Panglao.** ATMs exist on Alona and in Tagbilaran, but not everywhere. Withdraw PHP before arriving if possible. Credit cards work at resorts and larger restaurants but not small operators or for transportation.

**Learn basic Filipino phrases.** "Salamat" (thank you), "Magandang umaga" (good morning), and "Magkano?" (how much?) are appreciated and open doors. English is widely spoken in tourism areas, but effort matters.

**Hire a local guide for activities beyond Alona.** Chocolate Hills, hidden waterfalls, and local villages are best explored with local knowledge. Hotels can arrange guides for 1,500–3,000 PHP/day; it's worth the investment.

**The dry season breeze is genuine.** March–May have consistent trade winds; you'll feel cooler water and larger seas some days. This improves visibility but occasionally makes snorkeling on the house reef less appealing. Balicasag's deeper walls are sheltered and less affected.

**Night walks on Alona are safe and pleasant.** The beachfront strip is lit and populated until 10–11 PM. The sound of waves and tropical night ambiance is memorable; many travelers spend evenings walking the beach.
