# Diving and Snorkeling Seven Mile Beach: The Reef, the Kittiwake, and the Cayman Wall

Most of what makes Seven Mile Beach famous happens on the sand. The diving happens in the water in front of it, and it is a different proposition entirely. This is the leeward, west-facing coast, which is the whole reason it works: the trade-wind swell that hammers Grand Cayman's north and east shores breaks somewhere else, leaving this side flat enough that you can swim a reef line off the beach on most days without a boat. From a single base on the resort strip you can shore-snorkel a protected patch reef, dive a 251-foot Navy wreck, drop over a wall that falls thousands of feet, and run a boat north to the most over-touched sandbar in the Caribbean. None of it is wilderness. All of it is honest diving if you read the conditions and your own limits first.

## Cemetery Reef: the shore snorkel at the north end

The one piece of genuine reef you can reach from the sand without a boat sits off Cemetery Beach, near the northern end of the strip where the resorts thin out and a small public access point lets you in past the old seaside graveyard that gives the spot its name. Park, walk down, and swim out. The reef proper sits roughly a hundred yards offshore in about 30 feet of water, with shallower coral heads closer in around 20 feet — call it a six-minute swim for an unhurried snorkeler. Because fishing the reef is prohibited, the fish are dense and unbothered: parrotfish grazing the coral, blue tang in loose schools, French angelfish, the odd barracuda hanging in the blue, and on a good day a green turtle or a southern stingray working the sand between heads.

The thing to respect here is the current. It runs parallel to the shore and it can run hard, harder than the flat surface suggests, and it is the single most common reason people get into trouble on this stretch. Stand on the beach and watch the water move before you commit. Swim out against any current so the tired leg of the swim is the one that pushes you home, go early before the wind builds, and do not snorkel it alone. It is a forgiving site on a calm morning and a genuinely tiring one on a breezy afternoon.

## The USS Kittiwake: a Navy wreck off the strip

The Kittiwake is the marquee dive on this coast and one of the better-known wreck dives in the Caribbean. She was a Chanticleer-class submarine rescue vessel, 251 feet long, in U.S. Navy service from 1946 to 1994 — a working ship with real history, including the recovery of the flight-data recorder from the Space Shuttle Challenger in 1986. After years of planning she was scuttled deliberately as an artificial reef off the northern end of Seven Mile Beach, towed to the site on Christmas Day 2010 and sunk on January 5, 2011.

She sits on a flat sand bottom in about 64 feet of water, just inshore of the wall, with multiple decks penetrable by trained divers and wide hatches cut into the hull to keep the swim-throughs safe and well-lit. For years she stood almost perfectly upright with her superstructure within roughly 15 feet of the surface, which made her unusual: a wreck shallow enough that snorkelers and freedivers could work the top while scuba divers explored the holds below. That changed in October 2017, when the ground swell from Tropical Storm Nate snapped her mooring chains and rolled her onto her port side. She was inspected, declared stable, and reopened, but the geometry is different now — the deck plan you read in older guides no longer matches the wreck you descend onto.

The Kittiwake is a managed site, not a free-for-all. It is run as a marine park with a per-person fee (a few dollars for scuba, less for snorkeling), and access is through licensed operators. After more than a decade underwater she has become reef in her own right: sponges and corals on the rails, silversides pouring through the companionways, and resident fish that treat the hull as home.

## The walls: Grand Cayman's drop-offs and the Cayman Trench

The reason serious divers come to Grand Cayman is the wall. The island is the eroded cap of a submarine mountain rising off the edge of the Cayman Trench, the deepest point in the Caribbean Sea, and the coral shelf around it does not slope away gently — it ends. The fringing reef runs out to a lip somewhere around 50 to 60 feet on most of the island, and beyond that lip the bottom simply falls, in places past 2,000 feet and, off the North Wall, into the thousands. You hang in clear blue water with a vertical coral face on one side and nothing on the other.

From a Seven Mile Beach base, the closest walls are the West Wall, a short boat ride straight offshore, with a reef edge that begins shallow enough for a comfortable multi-level profile. For the dramatic stuff, boats run around to the North Wall, a longer trip but the trade-off most divers make: steeper, more sheer, hung with sponges and sea fans in better condition than the more-trafficked west side, with sites like Babylon and Eagle Ray Pass and regular fly-bys from spotted eagle rays and turtles. Wall diving rewards discipline. The bottom is not there to catch you, narcosis arrives quietly, and watching your depth and gas matters more here than on the patch reefs. This is not beginner terrain past the lip.

## Stingray City and the North Sound

Northeast of the beach, inside the barrier reef that shelters the North Sound, is Stingray City — two sites that share a name. The Sandbar sits in two to four feet of water, shallow enough to stand on, where dozens of southern stingrays gather because boat crews feed them; the Cayman Islands Department of Environment puts the regular population at roughly a hundred animals, broadly stable over the past decade. Nearby, the original Stingray City proper is a 12-foot dive, often called the best 12-foot dive in the world, where the same rays cruise a sand patch among scattered coral heads.

It is worth being clear-eyed about what this is. The rays are wild animals conditioned by decades of feeding — the habit traces to the 1980s, when fishermen cleaned their catch in the calm shallows and the rays learned to follow the scraps. The encounter is genuine and unforgettable and also entirely managed. Handle the animals gently if at all, never lift one clear of the water, and pick an operator that limits boat numbers. It is snorkeling and shallow diving more than a technical dive, and on a busy cruise day the sandbar can be crowded with hulls.

## Conditions, seasons, and the state of the reef

The leeward position is the standing advantage. The wave record off this coast averages around 1.18 meters, dropping to about 0.85 meters in the calm of May and building toward 1.5-plus in December, with a typical period near six seconds — short, local wind chop rather than long ocean swell. Visibility is routinely excellent. The catch is the winter "Nor'westers," cold-front swells that wrap onto the west side a few times each winter and can churn the inshore water, blow out Cemetery Reef, and force boats to the more sheltered side of the island. Diving runs year-round; June through November is hurricane season, and a passing storm is exactly what rolled the Kittiwake.

The reef itself is under real pressure, and the dive operators do not hide it. Lionfish, an Indo-Pacific invader first recorded in Cayman waters around 2008, spread fast and Cayman responded early by certifying divers to cull them; recent reporting suggests numbers are finally easing, helped by sustained culling and by native predators starting to take them. The larger threat is heat. The 2023 marine heatwave drove the worst bleaching on record across the Caribbean, Cayman's Department of Environment warned in 2025 that the reefs are dying, and stony coral tissue loss disease has been moving through the region. You will still see vibrant coral here, especially deep on the North Wall — but you are diving a reef in a hard decade, not a postcard. Anchoring is banned, mooring buoys are mandatory, and reef-safe sunscreen and hands-off discipline are the minimum the place asks of you.
