Myrtos Beach stands as one of Greece's most visually striking coastal destinations, famous for its dramatic limestone cliffs that plunge directly into crystalline waters. Located on the island of Kefalonia in the Ionian Islands, this pebble beach has earned its reputation as a photographer's paradise and a must-see for anyone exploring this less-crowded corner of Greece.

## Geography & Location

Situated approximately 14 kilometers from the port town of Sami, Myrtos is easily accessible yet feels remote enough to escape the worst of Mediterranean tourism crowds. The beach lies along Kefalonia's northern coast, where the island's distinctive geology creates some of its most spectacular scenery. Kefallinia Airport is about 25 kilometers away, making this an easy addition to a broader island exploration.

The beach's defining feature is its setting: towering white and cream-colored limestone cliffs rise 300 meters above the shoreline, creating a natural amphitheater that frames the turquoise water below. This dramatic topography isn't merely scenic—it shapes the entire beach experience, providing afternoon shade and shelter from winds that can affect other Ionian beaches.

## What to Expect

Myrtos is a pebble beach, not sand, which defines both its character and appeal. The smooth, rounded pebbles are comfortable underfoot and signal the beach's geological authenticity. The water is exceptionally clear, with excellent water quality ratings confirming what your eyes will tell you. Swimming conditions are generally safe, with no recorded shark incidents and good visibility for snorkeling near the rocky outcrops.

Facilities are modest but functional—restrooms and shower facilities are available, which is essential given the beach's remote location and the lack of commercial development along the shoreline. This isn't a party beach or a place for nightlife; it's a destination for contemplation, swimming, and photography.

## Best Lenses for Exploring Myrtos

**Photography** emerges as the primary lens here. The dramatic cliffscape, color contrasts between limestone and water, and quality of light make this one of Greece's most photographed beaches. Sunrise and golden hour photography are particularly rewarding.

**Travel & Adventure** matters significantly. Getting here involves winding roads with steep approaches, but the journey itself becomes part of the experience—a reminder that Kefalonia remains less commercialized than other Greek islands.

**Environment** is worth attention. The beach supports 20 recorded bird species, including Barn Swallows and European Red-rumped Swallows, making it interesting for birdwatchers and nature observers. The geology itself—how these cliffs formed and continue to shape the coastline—tells a compelling environmental story.

**Family** travel works here, particularly for families seeking a quieter, safer alternative to crowded resort beaches, though the pebbly substrate and lack of extensive amenities mean you'll need to come prepared.

Myrtos rewards visitors who approach it on its own terms: as a natural wonder rather than a resort destination, best experienced with a camera, a good book, and time to simply exist in one of Greece's most beautiful coastal settings.
