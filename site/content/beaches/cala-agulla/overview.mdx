Cala Agulla is a cove of roughly 500 meters of pale, quartz-dominant sand on the northeast tip of Mallorca, in the municipality of Capdepera, about a kilometer up the coast from the resort town of Cala Rajada. The name is Catalan for "needle." Behind the sand runs a dune system and a wood of Aleppo pine (Pinus halepensis); in front is Mediterranean water carrying the European Union's top bathing-water grade.

The defining fact of the beach is the edge of that pine forest. On 30 January 1991 the Parliament of the Balearic Islands passed Law 1/1991, the Llei d'Espais Naturals, declaring Cala Agulla together with the adjoining coves of Cala Moltó and Cala Mesquida an Àrea Natural d'Especial Interès (ANEI), a protected corridor of about 538 hectares where most building is barred. Cala Rajada beside it had been filling with hotels since the mid-20th-century tourism boom, and a holiday village had spread at Cala Mesquida from the 1970s. The 1991 law stopped the next wave before it reached these dunes: the built town ends and the pine wood begins.

The protected ground reads as a sequence most of Mallorca's east coast has lost. The dunes are held by marram grass and sea daffodil (Pancratium maritimum); above them the Aleppo pine mixes with savins and an understorey of mastic, strawberry tree, and the Mediterranean fan palm (Chamaerops humilis), the only palm native to continental Europe. Offshore, meadows of Posidonia oceanica seagrass keep the water clear and earn it the EU's "excellent" bathing rating.

Protection from building is not protection from people, and this is the honest half of the story. Cala Agulla is a fully serviced summer beach with seasonal lifeguards, rented sunbeds and parasols, a beach bar, and water-sports operators, and it fills heavily in July and August. The friction between a protected natural area and a beach that draws resort-scale crowds has recently centered on cars: the beachfront parking lot was dismantled in February 2025 because it lay inside the protected zone, Capdepera police cited 947 vehicles for illegal access between May and October 2025, and in May 2026 the council installed a barrier to block cars altogether.

**Most Relevant Lenses:**

**Nature & Conservation** — Cala Agulla's dune system, pine wood, and offshore Posidonia meadows are the reason the 1991 ANEI exists, a preserved sand-to-dune-to-forest sequence beside a heavily built coast.

**Coastal Policy** — The beach is the clearest place on this coast to see what a conservation law does and does not do: it kept the hotels off the land behind the sand, but the cove itself is still a crowded summer beach the town is now working to manage.

**Swimming & Clarity** — Water rated "excellent" under the EU bathing directive, waves usually under a meter and calmest in August, and clear water off the rocky headlands make Cala Agulla straightforward for swimming and snorkelling.

Cala Agulla is not a wild or empty beach. It is a crowded, serviced cove whose surroundings survived intact because a 1991 statute said they had to, on a coast where nearly everything comparable was built over.
