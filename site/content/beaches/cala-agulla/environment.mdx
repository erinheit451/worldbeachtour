# The Line a Law Drew: Cala Agulla's Protected Dune Coast

Cala Agulla is roughly 500 meters of pale sand on the northeast tip of Mallorca, in the municipality of Capdepera, about a kilometer up the coast from the resort town of Cala Rajada. What makes it worth explaining is not the sand but the edge of the pine forest behind it. On one side of that edge is one of the most-developed holiday coasts in the Mediterranean; on the other is a dune system and Aleppo pine wood that was never built on. The line between them was drawn by a specific piece of legislation on 30 January 1991.

## The 1991 law that froze the coast

Law 1/1991 of the Parliament of the Balearic Islands, the Llei d'Espais Naturals, classified dozens of coastal and inland sites as Àrees Naturals d'Especial Interès (ANEI), a designation that blocks most urban development. The 1991 act named 47 ANEIs on Mallorca alone. Cala Agulla was declared an ANEI that year together with its neighbours Cala Moltó and Cala Mesquida, a protected corridor of about 538 hectares.

The timing is the whole point. Cala Rajada, immediately to the south, had been filling with hotels and apartments since the mid-20th-century tourism boom, and a holiday village had grown at Cala Mesquida from the 1970s. The 1991 law arrived after that first wave and stopped the next one before it reached the Cala Agulla dunes. The result is a beach where the built town ends and a pine forest begins, with the boundary held in place by statute rather than by chance.

## What the protection covers

Behind the sand sits a dune system stabilised by marram grass and sea daffodil (Pancratium maritimum), the plants whose roots hold loose coastal sand in place. Above the dunes stands a wood of Aleppo pine (Pinus halepensis) mixed with savins, and an understorey that includes mastic, strawberry tree, and the Mediterranean fan palm (Chamaerops humilis), the only palm native to continental Europe. This is the vegetation sequence, sand to dune to pine, that most of Mallorca's east coast lost to construction and that the ANEI line preserves here.

Offshore, the water carries a European Union bathing-water classification of "excellent," the top grade under the EU directive. The clarity owes partly to meadows of Posidonia oceanica, the slow-growing Mediterranean seagrass that binds sediment and oxygenates the water; damaging it is precisely what coastal protection regimes try to prevent. The sand itself is quartz-dominant, modelled at about 71 percent quartz, which is why it reads pale rather than the darker tone of sediment-fed shores.

## The uncomfortable second half

Protection from building is not protection from people, and Cala Agulla shows the difference plainly. The cove is a fully serviced summer beach with lifeguards, rented sunbeds and parasols, a beach bar, and water-sports rentals, and it draws heavy crowds in July and August. The tension between a protected natural area and a beach the size of a small resort's clientele has come to a head recently over one mundane thing: cars.

In February 2025 the beachfront parking lot at Cala Agulla was dismantled because it sat inside the protected zone. Drivers kept coming anyway. Between May and October 2025, Capdepera's local police cited 947 vehicles for entering the natural area illegally. On 23 May 2026 the town council installed a pivoting barrier to physically stop cars, directing visitors to a lot at Clot de sa Grava and to the walk or bus from Cala Rajada. Thirty-five years after the law drew the line, the municipality is still enforcing it against the beach's own popularity.

## Fire and weather at the edge

The pine wood that the ANEI protects is also the coast's fire fuel. In August 2013 a wildfire burned roughly 480 hectares in the neighbouring Artà–Capdepera hills, around Cala Torta and the public land of Sa Duaia, mostly scrub with a smaller share of pine; it did not reach the Cala Agulla wood itself, but it is a standing reminder of what the protected forest sits inside. The sea is gentler than the land here: wave heights average about 0.77 meters over the year, biggest in February, with the calmest water in August, so the cove is usually swimmable through the season it is busiest.

Cala Agulla is not wild in the sense of empty. It is a working, crowded beach whose surroundings survived because a 1991 statute said they had to. The dunes and the pines are the visible argument for what that law was for, and the barrier at the road is the current, unfinished chapter of enforcing it.
