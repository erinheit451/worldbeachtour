# Cala Agulla Travel Guide

Cala Agulla sits on the northeast tip of Mallorca, in the municipality of Capdepera, about a kilometer up the coast from the resort town of Cala Rajada (also written Cala Ratjada). It is a protected natural area — an ANEI since the 1991 Balearic natural-areas law — that is also a fully serviced summer beach, so plan for July and August crowds rather than solitude. The single most important logistical change is recent: the beachfront car park was dismantled in February 2025 because it lay inside the protected zone, and in May 2026 the Capdepera council installed a barrier blocking cars from the natural area. Getting to the sand now means parking outside and walking, or arriving by bus.

## Getting There

### By Air

**Palma de Mallorca Airport (PMI)** is the island's only commercial airport and the gateway for nearly all visitors. It is about 64 kilometers from Cala Agulla in a straight line and roughly 80 kilometers by road — figure one hour to 75 minutes of driving, longer in peak-season traffic. PMI handles scheduled and charter flights from across Europe and is busiest May through October.

### By Car

Renting at the airport is the most flexible option; in high season book the car in advance, as availability tightens. The route is straightforward:

1. Leave PMI following signs for the **Ma-15** motorway toward Manacor and the northeast.
2. Stay on the Ma-15 through Manacor, then continue toward Capdepera.
3. Before Capdepera village, follow signs toward Cala Rajada; Cala Agulla is signed from there.

Mallorca's roads are toll-free, and petrol stations are common along the Ma-15 and in both Capdepera and Cala Rajada. The catch is the end of the journey. The beachfront lot was removed in February 2025, and after drivers kept entering the protected area anyway — Capdepera police cited 947 vehicles between May and October 2025 — the council installed a barrier in May 2026. Park at the **Clot de sa Grava** lot or in Cala Rajada and cover the last stretch on foot or by local bus.

### By Public Transit

Bus travel is realistic but not fast. **TIB (Transport de les Illes Balears)** runs coach service between Palma and Cala Rajada by way of Manacor and Artà (line 411 and its express variant); allow roughly 1.5 to 2 hours depending on the service and stops. For airport arrivals, TIB's Aerotib network links PMI with the Llevant coast via Manacor (line A42), and a seasonal Cala Rajada–Manacor–Palma express runs in the warmer months. Once in Cala Rajada, the beach is about a kilometer away and walkable; a local taxi or Bolt ride covers it for a few euros if you would rather not walk.

### By Boat

Private boats anchor off the cove in summer, but the seabed here is carpeted with protected Posidonia oceanica seagrass — anchor on sand, never on the meadows. An afternoon onshore breeze can build swell. Car ferries reach Palma from the Spanish mainland (Barcelona, Valencia), but there is no scheduled boat service to Cala Agulla itself.

## Best Time to Visit

**Summer (June–August)** brings warm, dry weather with daytime highs around 28–30°C and sea temperatures peaking near 25–26°C — comfortable swimming without a wetsuit. It is also when the beach is fullest and when the seasonal lifeguard service and beach facilities are in full operation. June and early September strike the best balance of warm water and thinner crowds.

**Late spring (April–May)** is mild (around 20–25°C) with a cooler sea (roughly 18–20°C) and far fewer people — a good window for walking the dunes and pine wood. **Autumn (September–October)** mirrors it, with the sea still holding summer warmth into September (23–24°C) before crowds and temperatures ease. **Winter (November–March)** is cool (12–16°C air, 13–15°C water), quieter, and outside the lifeguard season; the cove is genuinely calm and often empty, but swimming is a cold-water affair.

## Where to Stay

There are no hotels on Cala Agulla itself — that is the point of the ANEI protection, and the reason the cove goes dark at night. Lodging clusters in the towns within a few kilometers, and honest description matters more than invented names here.

**Cala Rajada (about 1–2 km)** is the natural base: Mallorca's main northeast resort town, a working fishing port with its own beaches, restaurants, bars, and the widest range of hotels, from simple three-star properties to smaller upscale places. It is the busiest and most tourist-oriented of the options, and the closest to the beach on foot.

**Capdepera village (about 2.5 km inland)** is quieter and more residential, sitting below its medieval hilltop castle. It has fewer hotels and more of a local feel, with vacation rentals, a supermarket, and small restaurants; accommodation tends to run cheaper than Cala Rajada.

**Cala Mesquida (about 3–4 km)** is the next protected cove up the coast, with a small holiday development dating from the 1970s and a limited set of rentals and hotels — a middle ground between the resort and seclusion.

Practically, most stays fall into vacation rentals (studios to apartments, the most common option), modest 2–3 star hotels, and a smaller supply of higher-end rural hotels. Book four to six weeks ahead for July and August; the shoulder seasons open up quickly at lower rates.

## Costs

Beach access is free — no entrance fee and, historically, no charge for the sand itself. The costs are the extras.

- **Sunbeds and parasols**: rented seasonally on the main beach; expect the usual Mallorca rates per item per day.
- **Food and drink**: limited on the sand to a seasonal beach bar (chiringuito) serving sandwiches, snacks, and ice cream. Real meals happen in Cala Rajada and Capdepera, where lunch menus run roughly €10–15 and dinners €25–40+. A supermarket picnic runs €10–18 per person.
- **Water sports**: kayak and paddleboard rentals operate in summer; budget €15–40 per hour or more for a day.
- **Parking**: the free beachfront lot is gone; park at Clot de sa Grava or in Cala Rajada, where paid parking rates vary by season.
- **Fuel**: petrol runs roughly €1.50–1.70/liter.

A day visit lands around €30–60 per person (picnic, parking, fuel); an overnight stay with restaurant meals pushes to €80–150+.

## Safety

Cala Agulla is a serviced beach, and its safety picture is better than a remote cove's — with the important caveat that the protection is seasonal.

- **Lifeguards**: Cala Agulla has a **seasonal lifeguard service** through the summer, with lifeguards on duty, flag signaling of sea conditions, and a **first-aid post in high season**. Outside the summer season there is no lifeguard cover, so take extra care and avoid swimming alone in spring, autumn, and winter.
- **Currents and swell**: the cove is usually gentle, with waves averaging under a meter over the year and calmest in August. The main variable is wind — an onshore breeze commonly builds through the afternoon, so mornings are the flattest, calmest time to swim. Heed the beach flags when they are flying.
- **Sea temperature**: from about 13–15°C in winter to 25–26°C in high summer. A wetsuit is sensible outside the warm months.
- **Underfoot**: sand gives way to rock and uneven ground near the headlands; water shoes prevent cuts. Keep off the Posidonia meadows offshore — they are protected and fragile.
- **Sun**: the Mediterranean sun is strong from late spring onward; natural shade is limited to the pine fringe at the back of the beach, so bring cover and SPF 30+.

For anything beyond first aid, medical centres operate in Capdepera and Cala Rajada, and the nearest full hospital is **Hospital de Manacor**, about 30 kilometers away by road — roughly a 30-minute drive via the Ma-15.

General safety is unremarkable: the area is calm, and the main petty risk is theft from parked cars, so leave nothing visible in a vehicle.

## Nearby

- **Cala Moltó** — a small rocky cove about 50 meters north, part of the same 1991 ANEI, quieter and rockier than Cala Agulla and commonly used as a clothing-optional spot. No services.
- **Cala Mesquida** — the next protected cove up the coast, with a longer beach, its own dunes, and a small resort development. A short drive or a walk through the natural area.
- **Castell de Capdepera** — the walled medieval castle above Capdepera village, tied to the 1231 Treaty of Capdepera, about 2.5 km inland and worth an hour for the history and the coastal views.
- **Far de Capdepera** — the lighthouse marking Mallorca's easternmost cape, a short distance south near Cala Rajada.

## Local Tips

**Come early in summer.** With the beachfront car park gone, the simplest high-season approach is to arrive on foot or by bus from Cala Rajada, early, before the walk-in crowd builds.

**Swim in the morning.** The sea is flattest before the afternoon onshore breeze picks up, particularly June through August.

**Bring your own food.** Beach food is limited to one seasonal bar; stock up in Capdepera or Cala Rajada.

**Watch the flags.** Lifeguard cover is summer-only — outside the season, judge the water yourself and don't swim alone.

**The cove faces east-northeast**, so the sea catches the sunrise, not the sunset; by evening the light is on the pines and dunes behind the sand.

**September is the quiet sweet spot** — warm water near 24°C, reliable weather, and noticeably thinner crowds than July and August.
