# Cala Agulla Travel Guide

Cala Agulla lies on Mallorca's northeast coast, near the small town of Capdepera, roughly 80 kilometers from the island's main airport. This remote-feeling beach—despite its proximity to developed tourist infrastructure—has become a destination for visitors seeking Mediterranean swimming and coastal solitude without the crowds of Cala Milfor or Peguera. Whether you're spending a day or making it a base for exploring the quieter northeast, Cala Agulla offers genuine water clarity and an intact ecological setting.

## Getting There

### By Air

**Palma de Mallorca Airport (PMI)** is the island's primary international gateway, located approximately 80 kilometers southwest of Cala Agulla (roughly 90 minutes to 2 hours drive depending on traffic and time of day). The airport handles flights from across Europe and beyond.

From PMI, rent a car at the airport terminal or use a shuttle service to reach your accommodation. Most visitors book vehicles in advance during peak season (May–September) to avoid rental shortages.

### By Car

The most straightforward route from Palma is via the **MA-15 motorway** heading northeast. From the airport:

1. Exit PMI and follow signs for MA-15 toward Manacor and the northeast coast
2. Continue on MA-15 through Manacor, then follow signs toward Capdepera
3. Before reaching Capdepera village, turn toward Cala Ratjada; Cala Agulla is signposted from there
4. The total drive is approximately 1.5 to 2 hours in normal traffic, though summer midday congestion can extend this to 2.5 hours

Petrol (gasolina) stations are plentiful along MA-15 and in both Capdepera and Cala Ratjada. Tolls are minimal on Mallorca's roads.

Parking at Cala Agulla is free and generally adequate; a small unpaved lot sits set back from the beach beyond the dune access. On peak summer days (July–August Sundays), arrive before 10 AM to secure a spot.

### By Public Transit

Bus service exists but is infrequent. **TIB (Transport Illes Balears)** operates routes from Palma to both Cala Ratjada and Capdepera; journey time is 2–2.5 hours depending on the route. From either town, a local taxi or ride-share (bolt.eu, available in Mallorca) covers the final 2–3 kilometers to Cala Agulla (€8–12).

For those without a rental car, this remains slower than driving but is feasible if you're staying in Capdepera or Cala Ratjada for multiple days.

### By Boat

Private vessel access is straightforward; the beach has good holding ground and reasonable protection from north to east winds. Be aware of the summer north wind pattern (Tramuntana) which can kick up afternoon swell. Ferry connections exist from the mainland (Barcelona, Valencia) to Palma, but no direct boat service reaches Cala Agulla itself.

## Best Time to Visit

### Seasonal Overview

**Summer (June–August)** offers warm, dry conditions with daytime highs of 28–30°C. Water temperature peaks around 25–26°C, making swimming genuinely enjoyable without a wetsuit. However, July–August sees peak tourist traffic; June and early September offer better balance between weather and crowds. Expect full parking and busier beaches during this window, but also the most reliable sunshine and longest daylight hours.

**Late Spring (May)** brings pleasant weather (22–25°C), lower water temperature (~20°C, still swimable with brief immersion), and markedly fewer visitors. Rainfall decreases toward month's end. Excellent window for photography and hiking.

**Autumn (September–October)** mirrors late spring with warming water through September (still 23–24°C) and declining crowds. October brings occasional rain but remains warm (19–23°C) and surprisingly quiet. September is particularly underrated.

**Winter (November–March)** is cool (12–16°C), with variable rainfall and frequent cloud cover. Water temperature dips to 12–14°C, requiring a wetsuit for extended swimming. However, the beach becomes genuinely solitary, and visibility can be spectacular on clear days. This season appeals primarily to locals and those seeking authentic escape.

## Where to Stay

Cala Agulla itself has no hotels directly on the beach; the nearest lodging is concentrated in three small towns within 2–4 kilometers.

### Capdepera (2.7 km)

The closest town to Cala Agulla, Capdepera is a quiet, largely residential settlement with a handful of modest hotels and vacation rental properties. It offers the shortest commute to the beach (5–10 minute drive) and has a small number of local restaurants and a supermarket. Accommodation here tends to be simpler and more affordable than nearby Cala Ratjada. This is an excellent choice if you want proximity to Cala Agulla without tourist resort infrastructure.

### Cala Ratjada (4 km)

Slightly larger and more developed, Cala Ratjada is Mallorca's primary northeast tourist hub. It has its own attractive beach and a concentration of restaurants, bars, and shops. Hotels range from simple three-star properties to small upscale inns. The town feels more "touristy" than Capdepera but still maintains local character. A 10–15 minute drive separates Cala Ratjada from Cala Agulla, making it a convenient base if you want flexibility to explore other nearby beaches.

### Cala Mesquida (3 km)

Another protected cove with its own small development, Cala Mesquida sits between Capdepera and Cala Agulla. It has fewer lodging options than Cala Ratjada but remains quieter. A handful of vacation rentals and small hotels serve visitors. Good middle ground between absolute seclusion and tourist convenience.

### Accommodation Types

- **Vacation rentals (Airbnb, Vrbo, local sites)**: €80–180/night for studios to 2-bedroom apartments; most common option in the area
- **Small 2–3 star hotels**: €70–130/night; modest amenities, family-run feel
- **Hostels/budget pensions**: €25–50/night for dorm beds; limited options in this region
- **Upscale rural hotels**: €150–280/night; limited supply, book ahead

Book accommodation 4–6 weeks ahead for peak season (July–August); off-season offers abundant availability at lower rates.

## Costs

### General Price Level

Cala Agulla beach access is completely free. No entrance fee, parking fee, or beach club charge. This represents genuine value on Mallorca's often-expensive eastern coast.

### Nearby Services

**Food and beverage**: Few options directly at the beach; small chiringuitos (beach bars) operate seasonally at Cala Agulla and nearby coves, typically charging €5–12 for sandwiches and drinks. Capdepera and Cala Ratjada have restaurants ranging from casual €8–15 lunch menus to upscale €30–50+ dinners. A picnic assembled from a supermarket costs €10–18 per person.

**Rentals**: Paddleboard and kayak rentals operate from Cala Ratjada during summer (June–September); expect €20–40/hour or €50–85/day.

**Parking**: Free.

**Fuel**: Petrol costs €1.50–1.70/liter; a typical rental car uses €30–40 of fuel for a week of Mallorca touring.

**Overall daily budget**: €35–60 per person for a day visit (picnic + parking + fuel), or €80–150+ if staying overnight and dining out at restaurants.

## Safety

### Swimming Conditions

Water at Cala Agulla is generally safe for swimming during daylight hours. Key points:

- **Water temperature** ranges from 12–14°C in winter to 25–26°C in summer. A wetsuit is essential November–April and advisable for sensitive swimmers year-round.
- **Currents and undertow** are minimal; the beach slopes gradually, making it safe for varied swimming abilities.
- **No lifeguards** are on duty; swim at your own risk and avoid swimming alone.
- **Shark incidents**: Zero recorded incidents at Cala Agulla; sharks are not a concern in Balearic waters.
- **Visibility**: Often excellent (5–15 meters), particularly in calm conditions away from the sandy shore.
- **Posidonia seagrass**: The underwater meadows are protected and fragile; avoid stepping on seagrass beds and never anchor boats directly on them.

Water quality is monitored by Spanish authorities and is consistently rated "excellent" (EU Bathing Water Directive). Post-rainfall contamination does not occur here as frequently as on developed beaches.

### General Safety

The Capdepera area is safe for visitors. Petty theft from parked cars is possible; don't leave valuables visible in vehicles. The beach itself has minimal hazards: uneven terrain and occasional sharp rocks near the waterline merit foot protection (water shoes).

### Health Considerations

Sunscreen (SPF 30+) is essential; Mediterranean sun is intense year-round. Medical facilities are available in both Capdepera and Cala Ratjada. The nearest hospital is in Arta, approximately 10 kilometers away.

## Local Tips

**Arrive early in summer.** If visiting July–August, be at the beach by 9 AM to secure parking and avoid crowds building after 10 AM.

**Bring a picnic.** The lack of beachfront food services makes self-catering the most practical and economical choice. Stock up in Capdepera or Cala Ratjada.

**Water shoes are practical.** The sand transitions to rocks and uneven substrate near the water; protective footwear prevents cuts.

**Watch the wind pattern.** The Tramuntana (north wind) typically builds in afternoon, particularly June–August. Morning swimming is usually calmer; afternoon often develops chop.

**The eastern end near the tree line is quieter.** If crowds accumulate, walk toward the pine forest backdrop—the beach extends further than most realize.

**Bring layers despite warm weather.** Morning and evening air remains cool even in summer. A light fleece or shirt prevents chill.

**Explore neighboring Cala Mesquida.** A short walk (or 2-minute drive) separates it from Cala Agulla; it offers slightly different character and fewer crowds on busy days.

**Visit the Capdepera castle.** The fortified castle overlooking the coast provides hiking and historical context; it's 20 minutes from Cala Agulla and adds depth to a multi-day visit.

**Snorkeling is exceptional here.** The Posidonia meadows and rocky points host diverse fish; bring a snorkel and explore beyond the sandy shallows.

**Sunset is golden hour.** The beach faces roughly east-northeast, so sunrises are spectacular; sunsets reflect off the water warmly.

**September is your secret weapon.** The combination of warm water (~24°C), good weather, and dramatically lower crowds makes September the best-kept timing secret on northeast Mallorca.
