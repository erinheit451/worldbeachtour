# Juno Beach: How Canada Took the Beach on 6 June 1944

Juno was the Canadian sector of the D-Day assault, an 8-kilometer front held by two battalions of the German 716th Infantry Division and attacked at first light by the 3rd Canadian Infantry Division. This is the morning told in order: the plan that set the price, the run-in to the seawall, the fight for the three towns, the cost, and how the ground is held in memory now.

## The plan

Juno was assigned to the 3rd Canadian Infantry Division under Major-General Rod Keller, reinforced by the 2nd Canadian Armoured Brigade and the specialized armour of the British 79th Armoured Division. Planners split the beach into two sectors: Mike in the west, at the mouth of the Seulles at Courseulles-sur-Mer, and Nan in the east, running through Bernières-sur-Mer to Saint-Aubin-sur-Mer, with Nan further cut into Green, White, and Red. The 7th Canadian Infantry Brigade landed in Mike and Nan Green at Courseulles; the 8th Canadian Infantry Brigade landed in Nan White and Nan Red at Bernières and Saint-Aubin.

The division had three objectives for the day, given the codenames of trees. The deepest, "Oak," was the Caen-Bayeux railway and road, roughly 18 km inland; nearer lay "Elm," an intermediate line; and the assault was to seize Carpiquet airfield west of Caen and link the British beaches, Gold to the west and Sword to the east, into a continuous front. An offshore reef forced H-Hour back to roughly 07:45, about ten minutes after the British beaches, so the rising tide would cover the rock. The same rising tide also climbed over the rows of mined obstacles the Germans had staked below the high-water mark, so that the leading craft ran in among them rather than ahead of them.

## The assault

The naval bombardment did not do its work. The first wave of the 7th Brigade landed shortly before 08:00 near Courseulles, with follow-on touchdowns logged around 08:10 and 08:12; many of the German strongpoints set into the seafront, blockhouses and gun casemates at the harbour mouth and behind the seawalls, were still firing when the ramps dropped. The armour that was supposed to silence them arrived unevenly. The 1st Hussars (6th Armoured Regiment), supporting the 7th Brigade, launched their Duplex Drive swimming Shermans at sea: 29 went into the water and seven were lost on the run-in to rough seas and fire before reaching the sand. Off Bernières the Fort Garry Horse made the opposite call and did not launch at all, bringing their DD tanks straight onto the beach aboard landing craft.

The infantry took the worst of the gap between bombardment and tanks. At Bernières, B Company of the Queen's Own Rifles of Canada came ashore about 250 yards east of its aiming point, directly in front of an intact German pillbox, and lost roughly 65 men in the first minutes before the survivors crossed the seawall into the town. To their east at Saint-Aubin, the North Shore (New Brunswick) Regiment and British No. 48 (Royal Marine) Commando fought against Widerstandsnest 27, whose 50 mm gun casemate still stands on the seafront. At Courseulles the Royal Winnipeg Rifles and Regina Rifles, with the Canadian Scottish, cleared a strongpoint mounting an 88 mm gun, a 75 mm, and two 50 mm guns.

## The towns

Juno was not a beach to cross so much as three seafronts to break into, because the towns sat directly above the sand. Once over the seawall the fight became a matter of streets and houses, and the reserve battalions passed through to carry it inland: Le Régiment de la Chaudière behind the 8th Brigade at Bernières, the Canadian Scottish behind the 7th at Courseulles. The villa at Bernières that became known as La Maison des Canadiens, "Canada House," was taken at first light by the Queen's Own Rifles; it is commonly described as among the first French houses liberated on D-Day, a claim shared with other buildings along the invasion coast and best stated as a tradition rather than a settled fact.

Then the division did the thing the casualty count hides. Despite the slaughter in the leading companies, Canadian units pushed inland farther than any other Allied force on 6 June, reaching a line roughly 7 miles (about 11 km) deep by mid-afternoon; the Queen's Own Rifles got as far as Anisy by last light. The full objectives were not met. Carpiquet airfield and the Caen-Bayeux road held out, and the assault halted on the intermediate "Elm" line to regroup rather than press to "Oak."

## The cost and the counterattack

The figures must be given carefully, because the numbers in circulation count different things. The 3rd Canadian Infantry Division at Juno suffered 340 killed, 574 wounded, and 47 taken prisoner on 6 June, roughly 961 casualties in the sector. The Juno Beach Centre's own often-quoted total of 381 killed folds in airmen and the airborne dead who fell elsewhere that day; the honest version separates the beach count from the wider total, so about 340 Canadians were killed on and just behind Juno itself. The Queen's Own Rifles ended the day with 143 casualties, the heaviest of any battalion in the sector. About 14,000 Canadians landed here, part of some 21,000 troops counting the British and supporting arms, and proportionally Canadian losses were among the heaviest of any Allied beach.

The beachhead was not secure at nightfall. The Canadians failed to make contact with the British 3rd Division coming off Sword to the east, leaving a gap of roughly 3 km, and around 16:00 elements of the German 21st Panzer Division pushed armour into it, the most serious German counterstroke of the day. The thrust reached the coast near Lion-sur-Mer but pulled back at dusk when a mass of Allied gliders passed overhead toward the airborne bridgehead; the German tanks, fearing encirclement, withdrew. The gap stayed open, and both flanks of the Canadian advance remained exposed into the following days.

## The Juno Beach Centre and memory

The memory along this seafront is concrete and specific. At Courseulles, on the sand the Regina and Royal Winnipeg Rifles crossed, stands the Juno Beach Centre, Canada's D-Day museum, inaugurated on 6 June 2003 with Prime Minister Jean Chrétien and about a thousand veterans present. It was driven by the veteran Garth Webb and his association, who raised the money when no official body would; a German casemate and a Sherman DD tank recovered from the sea stand on the same ground. Just west, at the Graye-sur-Mer boundary, a steel Cross of Lorraine marks where General Charles de Gaulle came ashore from the Free French destroyer La Combattante on 14 June 1944, eight days after the landing, before going on to Bayeux to give his first speech on liberated French soil and install the new Republic's officials. The Canadian dead lie a few kilometers inland at the Bény-sur-Mer Canadian War Cemetery. Treat the bunkers, Canada House, and the casemate at Saint-Aubin as the war graves' near neighbours they are, even on an August afternoon when the supervised beach below them is full of swimmers.
