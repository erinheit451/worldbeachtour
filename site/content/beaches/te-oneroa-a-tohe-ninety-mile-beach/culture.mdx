# Te Oneroa-a-Tōhē: The Spirit Road and the Iwi Who Hold It

Most beaches are governed as recreation. Te Oneroa-a-Tōhē is governed as an ancestor, a road, and a route for the dead at once. The English name, Ninety Mile Beach, records a colonial measurement that is wrong by a third — the beach is about 88 kilometres, roughly 55 miles. The Māori name records something the measurement cannot: Te Oneroa-a-Tōhē, "the long beach of Tōhē," for the Te Aupōuri ancestor said to have travelled its length. This is a deep dive into the cultural and legal life of that name.

## The Name and the Ancestor

Te Oneroa-a-Tōhē translates as "the long beach of Tōhē," carrying the name of the Te Aupōuri ancestor Tōhē. The beach lies on the western coast of the Aupōuri Peninsula at the far north of the North Island, in Northland, and runs north toward Cape Reinga. The 2015 Te Hiku Treaty settlement confirmed the dual name Te Oneroa-a-Tōhē / Ninety Mile Beach in law, so that the Māori name now stands first and official rather than as a folkloric footnote to the English one.

The English name is the older error. "Ninety Mile Beach" overstates an 88-kilometre beach as ninety miles, a discrepancy usually attributed to early travellers — missionaries or stock drovers — estimating distance by how long it took bullock teams or horses to walk it across soft sand, where slow going made a shorter beach feel longer. The name fixed itself in maps and memory before anyone reconciled it with the survey.

## Te Houtaewa: The Beach as a Running Ground

The beach's most-told story is one of speed. Te Houtaewa, a descendant of the Te Aupōuri chief Te Ikanui, was famed as an athlete. In the traditional account he raided a Te Rarawa village at Ahipara, at the southern end of the beach, stole baskets of kūmara, and — despite the load — outran the Te Rarawa warriors who chased him, returning the full length of Te Oneroa-a-Tōhē to Te Kao in the north.

The story is not only kept as oral tradition; it is run. Te Houtaewa's descendants hold an annual beach race along Te Oneroa-a-Tōhē in his memory, a marathon-distance event run entirely on beach terrain that has drawn competitors from across New Zealand and overseas. The legend casts the beach as a place of endurance and movement — a meaning that sits oddly and aptly alongside its modern legal life as a road.

## Te Ara Wairua: The Pathway of Spirits

The deepest cultural fact about Te Oneroa-a-Tōhē runs the other way, toward death. In Māori belief the beach is the final stretch of Te Ara Wairua, the spirits' pathway. After death, the spirits of the dead travel north along the coast to Te Rerenga Wairua at Cape Reinga, the headland at the very top of the country where the Tasman Sea meets the Pacific.

At Te Rerenga Wairua an ancient, wind-bent pōhutukawa — estimated at around 800 years old — clings to the cliff. Tradition holds that the spirits descend by its exposed roots into the sea below, beginning the journey back to the ancestral homeland of Hawaiki, surfacing briefly at the Three Kings Islands to bid a last farewell. Cape Reinga is regarded as the most spiritually significant place in the country for Māori. The beach is its approach. To drive north along Te Oneroa-a-Tōhē is to travel, in this understanding, the same direction the dead travel.

## Co-Governance: The 2015 Te Hiku Settlement

For most of the twentieth century the beach was administered as Crown land and a recreational route. The 2015 Te Hiku Claims Settlement legislation changed that. Passing its third and final reading in 2015, it established the Te Oneroa-a-Tōhē Beach Board, a statutory co-governance body charged with the management of the beach.

The Board brings together the four Te Hiku iwi — Te Aupōuri, Te Rarawa, NgāiTakoto and Ngāti Kuri — with the Far North District Council and the Northland Regional Council. Its stated purpose is to protect and enhance the environmental, economic, social, cultural and spiritual wellbeing of the beach for present and future generations. This is shared authority by statute, not consultation: the iwi who descend from Tōhē and Te Houtaewa hold a governing seat over the beach that carries their ancestors' names and stories.

## Toheroa: A Protected Taonga

Cultural authority over the beach extends to what may be taken from it. The toheroa — a large surf clam long prized as a delicacy and a customary food — was abundant enough that from 1928 to 1969 Northland factories canned about 20 tonnes a year, with a record 77 tonnes canned in 1940. The harvest collapsed the population, and in 1982 the toheroa fishery was closed nationwide.

The ban still holds on Te Oneroa-a-Tōhē despite a partial recovery, making toheroa one of New Zealand's most protected shellfish. Taking them can bring fines up to NZ$20,000. The more common, glossier-shelled tuatua are not under the same restriction. Customary gathering by iwi is provided for under permit — a recognition that the shellfish are not only a regulated resource but a taonga tied to the people who hold the beach.

## Two Roads on One Beach

What makes Te Oneroa-a-Tōhē culturally singular is the overlay. The same 88 kilometres of sand is, in New Zealand law, a gazetted public highway open to road-licensed vehicles at low tide — and, in Māori tradition, Te Ara Wairua, the road of departing spirits to Cape Reinga. One reading is administrative and printed on maps; the other is sacred and printed nowhere. The 2015 settlement did not choose between them. It confirmed the Māori name, seated the iwi in governance, and left the beach to carry both meanings — a road for the living heading north at low tide, and a path for the dead heading the same way, forever.
