# La Push (First Beach) Travel Guide

La Push First Beach is one of the Pacific Northwest's most pristine and culturally significant coastal destinations, located on the Quileute Indian Reservation on Washington's Olympic Peninsula. Unlike developed urban beaches, La Push demands genuine planning and respect—the reward is access to a wild coastline where dramatic sea stacks emerge from the surf and the Quileute Nation's presence is felt throughout the landscape.

## Getting There

### By Air

Most visitors arrive via **Seattle-Tacoma International Airport (SEA)**, located approximately 220 kilometers east of La Push. From SEA, you have two main route options to reach La Push:

**Option 1: West via Bainbridge Ferry (most scenic)**
- Drive from SEA north to Bainbridge Island ferry terminal (approximately 1 hour)
- Take the ferry to Bainbridge Island (35 minutes)
- Drive west across Bainbridge, take ferry from Kingston to Edmonds (30 minutes)
- From Edmonds, drive north-northwest on US-2 toward Bellingham and Concrete, then south on WA-20 or continue on US-101 (total drive 2-2.5 hours)
- This route adds 30-45 minutes but provides genuine Pacific Northwest scenery and avoids greater Seattle traffic

**Option 2: Drive direct via I-5 (faster)**
- From SEA, take I-5 north toward Tacoma/Seattle
- Continue north past Seattle approximately 30-40 miles
- Exit toward Olympia and take US-101 north
- Follow US-101 through the Olympic Peninsula toward Forks (approximately 4 hours total from SEA)

Both routes eventually converge on **US-101**, the primary north-south highway through the Olympic Peninsula.

### By Car from US-101

From the south (Portland, Oregon area):
- Take I-5 north into Washington
- Transition to US-101 near Salem or continue on I-5 north of Washington, then US-101 west
- Plan 8-10 hours from Portland

From the east (Spokane, Eastern Washington):
- Take US-2 west across the Cascade Mountains toward Bellingham
- Transition to US-101 southbound toward Forks
- Plan 7-8 hours from Spokane

From the north (Vancouver, BC or Bellingham):
- Take US-101 south toward Forks and the Olympic Peninsula
- Plan 2-3 hours from Bellingham

### Final Approach: Forks to La Push (US-101 to WA-110)

Once on US-101, Forks becomes your landmark. La Push is located **25 kilometers west of Forks** via Washington State Route 110 (WA-110).

- From US-101 near Forks, turn west on WA-110 toward La Push
- WA-110 is a well-maintained secondary road
- Follow signs to "La Push" and "Quileute Oceanside Resort"
- The drive from Forks to the La Push trailhead is approximately 30-40 minutes
- Road conditions are generally good year-round, though winter may bring rain and occasional closures due to weather or landslides

### Parking and Beach Access

Parking at La Push is free and available at the First Beach trailhead near Quileute Oceanside Resort. The lot accommodates roughly 50-75 vehicles on typical days and can fill quickly on sunny weekends or during summer peak season.

From the parking area, First Beach is accessible via a short trail (approximately 10 minutes on foot). Access is unrestricted to the general public, though visitors should be aware they are on Quileute tribal lands and should respect closure signs and tribal regulations.

## Best Time to Visit

### Seasonal Overview

**Summer (June–August)** is peak season but brings consistent crowds, especially on weekends. Daytime temperatures range from 60–68°F (15–20°C). Water temperatures peak at 50–55°F (10–13°C), still cold but tolerable with a wetsuit. Rainfall is minimal. The extended daylight allows for longer exploration windows. Expect the parking area to fill by mid-morning on sunny days.

**Spring (April–May)** offers fewer crowds, gradually increasing daylight, and moderate temperatures (50–60°F / 10–15°C). Rainfall is more frequent but not constant. Wildflowers appear in meadows near the coast. This is an excellent window for photography and hiking without summer press.

**Fall (September–October)** provides excellent conditions: temperatures remain mild (55–65°F / 13–18°C), crowds drop significantly after Labor Day, and the light becomes dramatic as days shorten. September is particularly good; October brings increasing cloud cover and wind but remains walkable. Storm activity increases later in October.

**Winter (November–March)** is rarely visited but offers genuine solitude. Temperatures hover around 40–50°F (4–10°C) with frequent rain and occasional snow at higher elevations. Daylight hours are short (roughly 8 AM–4 PM). However, this is when storm watching reaches its peak—massive waves driven by Pacific storms create an entirely different beach experience. Sections of beach can become inaccessible due to king tides. Winter is for committed visitors only.

### Tide Considerations (Critical)

La Push beaches are governed by significant tidal ranges (12-14 feet between low and high tide). Some sections of the beach, particularly the route between First and Second Beach, become impassable at high tide. Always check tide tables before visiting, particularly if planning to hike beyond First Beach.

- **Low tide** (optimal): Provides maximum beach access, reveals tide pools, and allows safe passage between beaches
- **High tide** (restrict activity): Can cut off escape routes and trap visitors against cliffs

Tide tables are available at NOAA (tides.noaa.gov) or local ranger stations.

## Where to Stay

La Push itself has no hotel accommodations directly on the beach. The **Quileute Oceanside Resort**, located at the village of La Push, is the only option within the immediate area and is operated by the Quileute Nation. This is the appropriate place to support when visiting and respecting tribal sovereignty.

### Quileute Oceanside Resort (La Push)

- **Location**: Directly above First Beach at the end of WA-110
- **Type**: Tribal resort with basic lodge rooms and vacation cabins
- **Amenities**: Rooms with views, some with kitchenettes, restrooms, modest facilities
- **Price**: Approximately $80–150/night depending on room type and season
- **Booking**: Phone or online through the resort's website
- **Character**: This is the authentic La Push lodging experience—modest, locally-owned, with proceeds benefiting the Quileute Nation
- **Note**: Accommodations fill quickly during summer weekends; book 2-4 weeks in advance

### Alternative: Forks (25 kilometers east)

Forks is a small logging/tourism town serving as the closest alternative if Quileute Oceanside Resort is booked.

- **Distance**: 25 km east of La Push via WA-110 to US-101
- **Options**: 3-4 small motels, budget hotels, vacation rentals
- **Price range**: $70–120/night
- **Character**: Forks is famous as the setting for the Twilight books; some accommodation markets this theme
- **Dining**: Limited but functional; expect casual restaurants focused on logging culture and basic tourist services
- **Drive**: 30-40 minutes from La Push—manageable but not ideal if planning multiple beach visits

### Alternative: Port Angeles (100 kilometers northeast)

Port Angeles is a larger town serving as a ferry gateway to Victoria, BC. It offers more accommodation variety but is further away.

- **Distance**: Approximately 1.5 hours drive from La Push via US-101
- **Options**: Full range of hotels, chains, vacation rentals, bed & breakfasts
- **Advantages**: More dining and shopping; better weather information and services
- **Disadvantage**: Adds significant drive time for beach visits
- **Consider only if**: Staying multiple days and wanting to explore other Olympic Peninsula attractions (Hurricane Ridge, Port Angeles waterfront, etc.)

## Costs

### General Price Level

La Push First Beach itself is **free to access**. There is no entrance fee, day-use charge, or parking fee. Vehicles can park at the trailhead parking area at no cost.

### Accommodation

- **Quileute Oceanside Resort**: $80–150/night (cheapest true option near the beach)
- **Forks motels**: $70–120/night
- **Port Angeles hotels**: $100–180/night

### Food and Supplies

- **Groceries in Forks**: Modest selection; plan to shop in larger towns if seeking specific items
- **Restaurants in Forks**: Casual meals ($10–20 per entree); no fine dining
- **Packed picnic food**: $15–25 per person (recommended—bring supplies from larger towns)
- **Gas**: Fill up in Forks before heading to La Push; no services at the coast

### Activities and Rentals

- **Hiking**: Free; no permits required for day hikes
- **Tide pool exploration**: Free
- **Photography**: Free; no commercial photography permits mentioned for casual visitor use
- **Equipment rental**: No kayaks, paddleboards, or hiking gear rental services at La Push; rent in Seattle before traveling

## Safety Considerations

### Water Safety (Critical)

**Do not swim in the Pacific at La Push.** The conditions that make this coast spectacular also make it dangerous:

- **Cold water**: Pacific temperatures (50–55°F / 10–13°C) cause cold-water immersion injuries within 15–20 minutes for most people
- **Sneaker waves**: Large, unexpected waves can sweep across the beach without warning, particularly near rocks and sea stacks. Never turn your back on the ocean.
- **Rip currents**: Strong currents flow away from shore in specific locations; if caught in one, swim parallel to shore rather than fighting the current
- **Undertow**: The combination of wave action and sandy beach creates powerful undertow conditions
- **Visibility**: Water clarity is poor; underwater hazards (rocks, logs) are invisible

Paddleboarding and kayaking are similarly hazardous without extensive experience and proper equipment. If water sports interest you, seek calm-water alternatives (protected bays, lakes).

### Tide and Beach Hazards

- **Sudden tide changes**: Tide can rise 12-14 feet; sections of beach become trapped if you venture beyond point-of-no-return
- **Log debris**: Dead timber washes ashore and creates obstacles; watch your footing
- **Slippery rocks**: Algae-covered rocks near sea stacks are extremely slippery
- **Unstable cliffs**: Cliff erosion is ongoing; don't approach the base of cliffs or assume they're stable

### Weather and Navigation

- **Fog**: Pacific fog can roll in quickly, reducing visibility to zero and making navigation difficult
- **Wind**: Coastal winds are constant and can gust violently during storms
- **Hypothermia risk**: Air temperatures are cool and humidity is high; dress in layers and bring a windproof shell
- **No cell service**: Cell reception is poor or nonexistent; don't rely on emergency communication

### Respect for Quileute Territory

La Push is Quileute Indian Reservation land. Visitors should:

- Respect closure signs and tribal regulations
- Avoid photographing individuals or ceremonies without permission
- Recognize that some areas may be sacred to the Nation and off-limits to visitors
- Support local Quileute businesses (Quileute Oceanside Resort, tribal services)
- Understand that this is not a public recreation area controlled by government agencies but a tribal community space

## Travel Tips

### Before You Go

1. **Check tide tables** (NOAA): Plan your beach time around low tide; high tide can cut off sections of beach
2. **Check weather**: Pacific Northwest coastal weather can change rapidly; be prepared for rain and wind
3. **Pack the car**: Bring a picnic, water, layers, and a windproof shell; services are limited
4. **Bring headlamp/flashlight**: Short daylight hours mean you'll need light for evening beach walks
5. **Book accommodation in advance**: Quileute Oceanside Resort fills quickly during summer
6. **Fill gas tank in Forks**: There are no fuel services between Forks and the coast

### What to Bring

- **Waterproof jacket and rain pants**: Essential year-round
- **Layers**: Fleece or wool; air temperature is cool and wind strips heat rapidly
- **Sturdy hiking shoes**: Rocky beach, mud, stream crossings; waterproof trail boots are ideal
- **Hat and gloves**: Particularly September–April
- **Sunscreen and sun hat**: Water and sand reflect UV light; overcast skies don't eliminate burn risk
- **Binoculars**: For spotting eagles, seals, and sea stacks detail
- **Camera**: The sea stacks and light create outstanding photography opportunities
- **Headlamp**: For early morning or late afternoon beach time
- **Map and tide table**: Even if you have GPS, carry paper backups

### Second and Third Beaches

The progression of First → Second → Third Beaches offers escalating coastal wildness:

- **Second Beach**: 0.7 kilometers from First Beach via tide-dependent beach walk or trail; features prominent sea stacks and tide pools; requires low tide to reach safely
- **Third Beach**: 1.4 kilometers from Second Beach; involves hiking inland through temperate rainforest before descending to the beach; most exposed and least developed of the three; requires careful tide planning and map reading

These hikes are for people with coastal navigation experience and serious preparation. Do not attempt Second or Third Beach without tide tables, weather knowledge, and realistic physical fitness.

### Nearby Attractions

- **Hoh Rainforest**: Approximately 50 kilometers southeast; UNESCO World Heritage site with old-growth forest and maintained trails
- **Olympic National Park**: Most of the surrounding Olympic Peninsula; extensive hiking trail system and wilderness camping
- **Forks and Twilight sites**: If literature interests you, the nearby town has become a pilgrimage destination for Twilight fans
- **Lake Quinault**: Larger lake with lodge accommodation and more developed infrastructure; 80 kilometers south

### Photography

La Push's sea stacks, offshore islands, and temperate rainforest create exceptional photography opportunities:

- **Golden hour (sunrise/sunset)**: Early morning and late afternoon light transforms the sea stacks and rocks
- **Storm conditions**: Dramatic skies and powerful wave action during winter storms
- **Tide pools**: Detailed intertidal life at low tide
- **Wildlife**: Eagles perching on sea stacks, seals hauling out on rocks
- **Composition**: The combination of foreground rocks, middle-ground sea stacks, and background coastline creates natural compositional depth

Bring extra batteries; cold temperatures drain them faster than you'd expect.

## Final Thoughts

La Push First Beach represents a different category of Pacific Northwest coastal destination—a place where geological drama, cultural significance, and ecological integrity remain intact. It demands respect, preparation, and realistic assessment of your own abilities. The reward for thoughtful planning is access to a coastline that remains wild, beautiful, and connected to its indigenous heritage.
