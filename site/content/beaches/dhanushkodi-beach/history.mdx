# Dhanushkodi: The Night a Town Drowned at India's Land's End

Dhanushkodi stands at the southeastern tip of Pamban Island in Tamil Nadu, on a sandspit that runs out toward Sri Lanka about 24 kilometers west of Talaimannar. Until December 1964 it was an inhabited town. On the night of 22–23 December 1964 a cyclone destroyed it in a few hours, and it was never rebuilt. What follows is what is documented, what is disputed, and what the older record holds.

## The Town Before the Storm

Dhanushkodi was not a fishing outpost that happened to wash away. It was a functioning town because it sat at a crossing. The metre-gauge Pamban–Dhanushkodi railway, carried onto the island by the Pamban Bridge that opened in 1914, ran out across the spit to a terminus and ferry pier, where boats took pilgrims and traders the short sea distance to Talaimannar in Ceylon. To serve that traffic the town had a port, a railway station, a post office, a customs and immigration post, hotels, a church, a higher secondary school, and a small railway hospital.

People lived their lives here, on a thin bar of sand standing at sea level between the Bay of Bengal to the north and the Palk Strait and Indian Ocean to the south.

## The Cyclone of December 1964

The storm formed in the Bay of Bengal on 18 December 1964 and tracked west toward the Palk Strait, reaching the Dhanushkodi spit and the Ceylon coast late on 22 December. Peak sustained winds reached around 240 km/h, with gusts near 280 km/h.

The killing force was the water, not the wind. The storm surge over Dhanushkodi has been estimated at about 7.6 meters — roughly 25 feet. A bar that stands at sea level has nothing above the waterline to break a sea of that height, and the surge ran across the whole spit. At least 1,800 people died across the disaster, with at least 800 in Dhanushkodi alone. In the wider region around 5,000 houses and 700 fishing boats were destroyed, with heavy losses in the Jaffna district of Ceylon.

## The Train That Was Lost

The image that fixed the disaster in memory is the train. A Pamban–Dhanushkodi passenger train was running across the spit in darkness when the surge caught it, and it was lost with everyone aboard. The exact toll is reported differently in different sources: some accounts give about 110–115 passengers and railway staff on the train that reached or neared the Dhanushkodi station, while others put the figure at roughly 200. The accounts agree that there were no survivors among those on the train.

The Pamban rail bridge that linked the island to the mainland was also wrecked. It was rebuilt with notable speed — in 45 days, under the engineer E. Sreedharan — restoring the line as far as Rameswaram. The stretch beyond, out to Dhanushkodi, was never relaid.

## Declared Unfit, Never Rebuilt

After the cyclone the Madras government declared Dhanushkodi unfit for living. The decision reflected the geography: the same thin, flat spit that made the town a useful crossing point made it indefensible against a surge that could simply run over it. The railway line and the Dhanushkodi station were closed permanently. The town was not reconstructed, and the survivors did not return to a rebuilt settlement, because none was built.

What the storm left is what stands now: the roofless brick shell of the church, the broken railway station, and fragments of the post office and other buildings, scattered across pale, shell-coloured sand. The Kothandaramaswamy Temple on the Dhanushkodi road is reported to have been the one structure in the area to survive the cyclone intact. A fishing hamlet remained at the edge of the ruins, its families living on without reliable electricity or piped water.

## The Older Meaning: Bow's End and the Bridge to Lanka

The spit carried a name and a story long before 1964. "Dhanushkodi" is Sanskrit for "bow's end." In the Ramayana, this is where Rama set the tip of his bow to mark the start of the causeway of stones his army built to cross to Lanka. The shoal chain that arcs from Arichal Munai — the point at the far tip of the spit — toward the Sri Lankan coast is the same feature mapped as Adam's Bridge, and is identified in that tradition as the Ram Setu. Arichal Munai is also where the Bay of Bengal meets the Palk Strait and the Indian Ocean.

So the place holds two meanings at once: the mythic beginning of a bridge to another country, and the literal end of a town the sea erased.

## Reaching the Ruins Today

For decades after the cyclone, getting to Dhanushkodi meant crossing the sand on foot or by jeep from Rameswaram. A metalled road was completed from Mukunthurayar Chathiram after 2016, with a further stretch of about 4.5 kilometers running out to Arichal Munai, and local vans now run the final sandy section to land's end. The hard pilgrimage has become a day trip, and the ruins take a steady traffic of visitors.

The ground deserves to be read for what it is. The church and the station are not picturesque ruins. They are the buildings a town of people drowned in on a December night, and the hamlet beside them is inhabited still.
