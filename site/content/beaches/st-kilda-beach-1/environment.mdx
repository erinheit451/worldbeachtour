# The Olympic Breakwater That Became a Rookery: St Kilda's Little Penguins

St Kilda Beach has a heritage amusement park, a festival, and a nightlife strip, but the thing that makes it singular is biological. On a bluestone breakwater at the western end of the beach, poured to shelter a harbour for the 1956 Melbourne Olympic yachting, a wild colony of about 1,400 little penguins now nests, 6 kilometres from the centre of a city of roughly five million people. Nobody put them there.

## The World's Smallest Penguin, in the World's Least Likely Place

The birds are little penguins, *Eudyptula minor*, the smallest of the world's penguin species. An adult stands about 33 centimetres tall and weighs roughly a kilogram, with slate-blue plumage that gives the species its other names, fairy penguin and little blue penguin. They are night-adapted seabirds: they spend the day fishing in Port Phillip Bay, sometimes ranging tens of kilometres, and come ashore only after dark to their nests. That nocturnal habit is the single most important fact for anyone visiting them, and the reason the site is run the way it is.

## How a Sailing Harbour Turned Into Habitat

The breakwater was never meant to house wildlife. It was built in the mid-1950s to create a sheltered harbour for the sailing events of the 1956 Melbourne Olympic Games. When the Games ended, the rock stayed, and the deep, dry crevices between the bluestone blocks turned out to be close to ideal little-penguin nesting sites: dark, above the waterline, protected from swell, and sitting on the edge of a fish-rich bay. Penguins began nesting there in the 1970s, entirely on their own.

The scale of what followed is best read in the count. In 1986, a small group of volunteers led by Monash University's Professor Mike Cullen carried out the first census and recorded 57 penguins. Four decades of continuous monitoring later, the colony is estimated at around 1,400 birds. That is one of the longest-running datasets on any little-penguin colony anywhere, kept largely by volunteers now organised as Earthcare St Kilda, who band and microchip birds and run night counts.

## A Wild Colony, Not an Exhibit

This is what separates St Kilda from a ticketed wildlife attraction. The penguins here are free-ranging wild animals, not a fenced display, and viewing is free from the public pier at dusk. That openness only works because of enforceable rules. In 1992, after years of community pressure, the breakwater was declared a Wildlife Management Cooperative Area, which bans domestic animals, climbing on the rocks, white light, and careless rubbish disposal.

The prohibition on white light is not a courtesy. Little penguins come ashore in near-darkness and are disoriented by bright light and camera flash, which can keep them from landing at all. Viewing is done under dim red light, quietly, from a distance. The rules are the price of a wild colony that chose to live beside a city instead of away from one.

## The Pressures of Living Next to a City

A remote island rookery does not face what St Kilda's does. Off-leash dogs on the foreshore are a recurring cause of adult penguin deaths. Foxes and cats hunt the shoreline, boat strike and discarded fishing line take birds in the water, and pollution or oil in the bay is a standing risk. Heat is another: little penguins tolerate extreme temperatures poorly, and marine heatwaves cut the small fish they depend on. Even the breakwater's other residents are a threat. The rakali (*Hydromys chrysogaster*), the native Australian water-rat, lives in the same rocks and preys on penguin eggs and chicks.

None of this is hypothetical, and none of it is fully solved. The roughly 1,400 birds are a managed equilibrium held in place by monitoring and enforcement, not a wilderness left alone.

## Engineering Around the Birds

The infrastructure the colony depends on keeps being rebuilt beneath it. A major refurbishment of the breakwater in 1998 relocated about 22,000 tonnes of rock, timed and staged to avoid destroying nests. The pier's kiosk end burned in 2003 and was rebuilt, and the entire pier was replaced in a redevelopment that reopened in December 2024. Each project had to be engineered so that the seabird colony a few metres away survived the work meant to protect the structure it lives in.

That is the whole story of St Kilda's environment in one image: a city built a wall for two weeks of Olympic sailing, walked away, and the sea moved in and stayed. The colony is not a relic of untouched nature. It is a wild population that adapted to human infrastructure and now persists on the enforcement, science, and restraint of the people who come to watch it.
