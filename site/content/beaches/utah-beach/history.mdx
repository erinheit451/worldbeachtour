# Utah Beach: The Westernmost D-Day Landing, and the One That Went Right

Utah sits apart from the other D-Day beaches. The four British, Canadian, and American beaches of 6 June 1944 run in a line along the Calvados coast; Utah lies around the corner, on the east-facing shore of the Cotentin Peninsula, a low and almost featureless strip of sand backed not by cliffs but by marsh. The Americans who came ashore here belonged to the U.S. 4th Infantry Division, and what happened to them on the morning of the landing is why this beach is remembered differently from its neighbors. It is the D-Day beach where, against every reasonable expectation, the plan more or less worked — and the discomfort of the place is bound up in exactly that.

## A beach where the ground was the plan

At Omaha, the killing came from high bluffs above the sand. Utah had no bluffs. Its danger was inland and underfoot: the German command had deliberately flooded the low-lying farmland behind the dunes, turning the country into a shallow lake crossed by only a handful of raised roads. These were the causeways, and the whole battle hinged on them. A division could put men on the open beach easily enough. What it could not do was get off the beach and into Normandy except across those few narrow exits. Hold the inland ends of the causeways, and the beach becomes a trap with the sea at its back.

This reshaped the assault. Utah, alone among the seaborne landings, was built around a large airborne operation the night before — not as a flourish but as a precondition. Paratroopers would come down behind the flood to seize the causeway exits from the landward side before the seaborne troops ever reached them. The beach could not be made to mean anything without the men dropping inland in the dark.

## The wrong beach, and the right call

At 06:30 on 6 June the first wave of the 4th Infantry Division touched down — and not where it was meant to. Strong tidal currents, drifting smoke, and the loss of control craft had carried the assault craft roughly 2,000 yards south of the intended sector, away from the heavily defended ground near Les Dunes de Varreville and onto a stretch that was, by accident, more lightly held. (Sources usually give the displacement as about 2,000 yards or 1,800 meters; the figures are close enough to treat as the same error.)

The senior officer on the sand was Brigadier General Theodore Roosevelt Jr. — eldest son of the former president, fifty-six years old, walking with a cane and carrying a heart condition he did not advertise, and the only general to land in the first wave on any of the five beaches that day. He had asked repeatedly to go in with the leading troops, and his division commander had reluctantly agreed. Roosevelt recognized that the landing had drifted, that correcting it would throw the timetable into chaos, and that the ground under his feet was in fact better than the ground in the plan. He chose to bring the follow-on waves in where they were.

The line attributed to him — most often rendered "We'll start the war from right here" — is the sentence the beach is remembered by, though it survives in several wordings and should be treated as remembered rather than transcribed. He was awarded the Medal of Honor for his conduct that day but did not live to receive it: he died of a heart attack on 12 July 1944, in his sleep, on the day he was to take command of the 90th Infantry Division. He is buried in the Normandy American Cemetery above Omaha Beach.

## The men who came down in the dark

While the beach was being taken comparatively cheaply, the airborne divisions that had made it possible were paying a far steeper price inland. The 101st Airborne began dropping from around 01:30, the 82nd from around 02:30, across the flooded Cotentin to seize the causeway exits, road junctions, and villages behind the beach. The drops went badly wrong almost everywhere. Cloud, flak, and inexperienced navigation scattered the paratroopers far from their drop zones; men came down alone or in small bands, miles from where they meant to be, and some drowned in the flooded fields, dragged under by their own equipment.

The most enduring image of that night belongs to Sainte-Mère-Église, about 12 km northwest, which became one of the first French towns liberated, in the small hours of 6 June. A paratrooper of the 82nd, John Steele, caught his canopy on the church tower and hung there, feigning death, for roughly two hours before being taken prisoner; he later escaped. A figure of a paratrooper still hangs from that steeple today. Keep the proportion straight: the airborne losses inland were heavier than the losses on the beach the name commemorates. They are part of Utah's true cost even though they fell off the sand.

## Across the causeways, and out

Because the paratroopers had taken — or were fighting for — the inland ends of the causeways, the 4th Division could do the thing that should have been hardest: move off the beach. Through the day, troops pushed inland along the raised roads, linked up with scattered airborne units, and joined the two halves of the operation into a single bridgehead. By nightfall roughly 21,000 men of the 4th Division and somewhere between 13,000 and 14,000 airborne troops were ashore on the Cotentin. The division's casualties for the whole of 6 June are most often cited at around 197 — a figure that already folds in some 60 men lost at sea when part of a field-artillery battery went down before reaching shore.

Set that against the roughly 2,400 American casualties usually cited for Omaha on the same morning and the contrast is stark. But "comparatively low" is the load-bearing phrase, and the honest reading does not stop at the beach. From Utah the VII Corps turned north toward Cherbourg — the real strategic prize, a deep-water port the Allies needed — and the fighting in the bocage hedgerows beyond the beach was neither quick nor cheap.

## The rehearsal that cost more than the battle

The most uncomfortable fact about Utah is that more Americans died practicing for it than died taking it. In late April 1944, Force U rehearsed the landing at Slapton Sands in Devon — a beach chosen because its gravel shore, strip of land, and lake behind it resembled the Cotentin ground. The exercise was codenamed Tiger. In the early hours of 28 April, a flotilla of German E-boats slipped undetected into a convoy of slow, heavily laden landing ships in Lyme Bay and attacked the unprotected rear, sinking two and crippling a third. At least 749 American servicemen died — many drowned or died of cold, weighed down by packs and untrained in the use of their life vests, in water they could not survive long enough to be pulled from.

The disaster was kept secret at the time, for fear a leak would warn the enemy, and stayed largely unknown for decades. Any clean, triumphal telling of Utah that leaves out Slapton Sands is dishonest by omission. The lessons paid for there — life-vest training, better convoy escort, common radio frequencies — were folded back into the real landing. The cost of the rehearsal bought down the cost of the event.

## What stands on the dune now

At La Madeleine, on the dune at the center of the landing beach, the Utah Beach Landing Museum is built around a real German blockhouse — strongpoint WN-5 — that defended this sector on 6 June. It opened in 1962 and reopened at about 3,000 square meters after a 2011 extension; its centerpiece is a Martin B-26 Marauder, one of only a handful of the medium bomber surviving anywhere. Nearby, a red granite obelisk dedicated in 1984 commemorates the VII Corps that came ashore here and drove north to Cherbourg, on ground given to the United States in perpetuity by the commune of Sainte-Marie-du-Mont.

Behind it all spreads the marsh — the same flooded ground the Germans weaponized, now the Marais du Cotentin et du Bessin regional nature park, grazed and walked and watched for waders and wildfowl. In summer the beach fills with families swimming in calm, shallow water. That ordinariness is the hardest thing to hold here honestly: Utah is a quiet seaside beach laid over the floor of an assault, and the only fair way to stand on it is to remember that its lightness was bought by the men who fell in the air, in the marsh, and on a beach in Devon.
