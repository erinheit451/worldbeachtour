# Sword Beach: the easternmost D-Day landing and the race to Pegasus Bridge

Sword was the left edge of the whole invasion. On 6 June 1944 the five Allied landing sectors ran west to east along the Calvados coast — Utah, Omaha, Gold, Juno, and Sword — and Sword was the last of them, the easternmost beach, the hinge on which the entire eastern flank turned. Its job was not simply to put men on the sand. It was to join the two halves of that flank: the airborne troops who had come down in the dark hours before dawn, holding the bridges over the Orne, and the seaborne troops landing in daylight. Then it was to drive 14 kilometers inland and take Caen before nightfall.

The beach did the joining. It did not take Caen. That distance — between what Sword achieved by lunchtime and what it had been ordered to achieve by dark — is the thing worth understanding before you walk it. This page follows the day in order: the plan, the dawn assault, the link-up with the airborne, the stalled drive on Caen, and what is left on the coast now to hold the memory.

## The plan

Sword fronts a string of small communes at the mouth of the Orne and the Caen canal: Ouistreham and its Riva-Bella seafront at the east end, then Colleville-Montgomery, Hermanville-sur-Mer with its La Brèche access, Lion-sur-Mer, and Luc-sur-Mer. The sector ran roughly 8 kilometers, though the assault went in on a narrow front.

The British 3rd Infantry Division, under Major General Tom Rennie, drew the task. Caen was the prize — a road and rail hub 14 kilometers inland whose early capture would have opened the country south of the beachhead — and the first-day objective was explicit: reach it on 6 June. To the division's east, beyond the Orne, the ground had already been claimed in the night by the 6th Airborne Division, whose lightly armed paratroopers and glider troops could hold the bridges only so long against armour. Someone had to come up from the sea and reach them. That requirement set the geometry of the whole left flank, and explains why the eastern end of the landing mattered out of all proportion to its width.

## The dawn assault

The landing began at 7:25 a.m. — H-Hour on Sword. The swimming DD tanks of the 13th/18th Royal Hussars went in with the leading infantry of the 8th Infantry Brigade, the South Lancashires and East Yorkshires, on the Queen White and Queen Red sectors. The principal German strongpoint, codenamed "Cod," stood at La Brèche d'Hermanville and had to be reduced in the first hour, and that hour cost the most. By the standards set up the coast at Omaha this was not a slaughter; the surf and the strongpoints were deadly in the first wave, but the beach was largely cleared through the morning and the follow-on brigades came ashore behind the lead.

At the eastern end, the fighting had particular weight. The Free French troop under Philippe Kieffer — 177 men, attached to Lord Lovat's brigade and the only French troops to land on the D-Day beaches — fought through Ouistreham and Riva-Bella and reduced the fortified casino strongpoint there shortly after 9 a.m. Frenchmen liberating French soil on the first morning of the invasion, a fact the Ouistreham memorials keep at the front.

The day-one casualty count for the 3rd Division on Sword is usually given as roughly 630, but the figure is an estimate and sources differ. A unit-by-unit tally — the brigades, the machine-gun battalion, the commando losses — comes nearer 683; neither number is exact. About 28,845 men of I Corps had come ashore here by nightfall.

## Pegasus Bridge and the airborne link-up

The eastern flank's story actually opens before the beach landing, just after midnight. At about 00:16 on 6 June, six Horsa gliders — towed across the Channel by Halifax bombers and then cast off to come down silently in the dark — landed almost on top of the two bridges over the Caen canal at Bénouville and the Orne at Ranville. This was Operation Deadstick, a coup de main led by Major John Howard with a reinforced company of the 2nd Oxfordshire and Buckinghamshire Light Infantry, roughly 180 men. The lead glider stopped about 47 yards from the canal bridge — among the most precise pieces of flying in the entire invasion — and both bridges were taken intact in a short, sharp fight. The signal sent back, "Ham and Jam," meant both bridges seized and both bridges whole.

Holding them intact was the whole point. These were the only crossings of the Orne and its canal on the eastern flank: kept in Allied hands they denied the Germans an easy route to roll up the invasion from the east, and gave the Allies their own route to reinforce the airborne bridgehead beyond the river. The canal bridge was later renamed Pegasus Bridge for the winged-horse badge of the 6th Airborne Division, and the Café Gondrée beside it bills itself as the first house in France liberated on D-Day.

But Howard's men could not hold indefinitely against tanks, and the relief had to come from the sea. It came from Sword. Lord Lovat's 1st Special Service Brigade pushed off the beach and inland toward the bridges, with Lovat's personal piper, Bill Millin, playing the bagpipes as they went. The War Office had banned pipers from leading men into action after the losses of the First World War; when Millin pointed out the regulation, Lovat — a Highlander — told him it was an English War Office order and did not apply to two Scotsmen. Millin played "Hielan' Laddie" and "The Road to the Isles" on the beach and on the march. German prisoners said afterward that they had not shot him because they assumed he was mad. The brigade reached the bridges around midday, near 13:00, joining the seaborne and airborne forces on the eastern flank. Lovat is remembered for apologizing to Howard for arriving a few minutes after the time he had promised. The sea and the air had met.

## The drive on Caen

And then the larger objective slipped away. The 3rd Division's advance south got partway inland — roughly five miles by evening — and stalled. The cause was the only major German armoured counter-attack mounted on the coast that day. The 21st Panzer Division, joined by the 192nd Panzergrenadier Regiment, drove north into the gap between Sword and Juno where the British and Canadians had not yet linked. Some of its forward elements reached the sea late in the afternoon; sources place the contact near Lion-sur-Mer or Luc-sur-Mer, at the western edge of the Sword sector. The thrust was checked: tanks were lost on the Périers ridge, and just before 9 p.m. a great fly-over of glider reinforcements — hundreds of tugs and gliders passing over the heads of the panzers toward the airborne bridgehead — helped convince the Germans that they risked being cut off, and they pulled back.

The counter-attack failed to split the beachhead. But it did the one thing that mattered most to Sword: it stopped the rush on Caen. The city would not fall until mid-July, after weeks of fighting — Operations Charnwood and Goodwood among them — that destroyed much of it. So the ledger of Sword reads split down the middle: a clean link-up at the bridges and an early French liberation of Ouistreham on one side, a first-day strategic objective missed by a month on the other.

## The bunker, the museum, and memory

The fighting did not end with the landing. At Ouistreham a 17-meter German fire-control tower — built to direct the coastal batteries covering the mouth of the Orne — held out after the beach was cleared; the commandos had been driven off it on 6 June by machine-gun fire and grenades from the top. On 9 June, Lieutenant Bob Orrell of the Royal Engineers, with three men, set explosive charges against its armoured door. It took about four hours to break open, and the garrison of two officers and some fifty men surrendered. That tower is now the Grand Bunker, restored to its 1944 state and run as the Atlantic Wall Museum.

What sits on the sand now is a working seafront, less visited and less filmed than Omaha. Ouistreham is a live cross-Channel ferry port with sailings to Portsmouth; Riva-Bella has a casino on the ground where the strongpoint stood; in summer the promenades at Colleville-Montgomery, Hermanville, and Lion-sur-Mer fill as ordinary French holiday beaches. The memory is held in fixed points rather than across a consecrated field: the Grand Bunker, the bronze statue of piper Bill Millin on the dunes at Colleville-Montgomery, a preserved Churchill AVRE tank at Lion-sur-Mer, the divisional and commando stones along the front, and the bridges up the Orne that the men from this beach raced to reach. The honest way to stand here is to take in the ferry terminal, the holiday seafront, and the unmet city of Caen beyond the bridges as one fact, not three.
