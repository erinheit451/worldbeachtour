# Accra's Front Yard: Music, Horses, and the Plastic Tide

Labadi Beach is the busiest beach in Ghana, and it is best understood not as a place to swim but as the capital's shared public stage. To understand it you have to take both halves seriously at once: the extraordinary cultural life that fills the sand, and the polluted Atlantic that the same city pours into and the tide hands back. Labadi is both, every weekend.

## A Ga Town's Beach

Labadi, formally La Pleasure Beach, belongs to the town of La, a historic Ga community in eastern Accra, near Teshie and barely five kilometers from the international airport. That matters: this is not a resort strip carved out for tourists but the seafront of an old, living African town. The Ga people of La celebrate **Homowo**, the harvest festival famous for the ceremonial sprinkling of *kpokpoi*, and the beach sits inside that culture rather than apart from it. When Accra needs a communal space, somewhere to gather, perform, exercise, and celebrate, Labadi is where the city goes.

## The Scene

On a weekend or holiday, Labadi is a fairground of sound. Live **reggae** and **hiplife**, the Ghanaian fusion of highlife and hip-hop, play from beach stages; there is playback, and traditional Ga drumming and dancing. The single most famous fixture is the **Wednesday reggae night**, which runs from 6pm and pulls a dedicated crowd to local reggae stars, a standing appointment in Accra's week.

And there are the horses. Horse and pony rides along the surf, for only a few cedis (around three US dollars), are one of Labadi's signature images, an unexpected and beloved sight on a West African pleasure beach. Early on weekend mornings, before the music and the vendors, the beach belongs to people working out on the cool sand. Across a single day Labadi is a gym, a concert, a dance floor, a meeting place, and a beach.

## The Beach You Pay to Enter

One thing sets Labadi apart from a free public shore: you pay to get in. The beach is maintained by the hotels that line it, and visitors not staying at those hotels are charged an entrance fee, typically a modest 10 to 20 cedis, a dollar or two, more on event days. The arrangement funds the upkeep and security that a crowd this size needs, and it is also a recurring, pointed debate in Accra: who should control the city's most-loved beach, and, given the litter that still washes up, what exactly the fees are delivering. It is a fair question, and an unresolved one.

## The Plastic Tide

That litter is the honest shadow over the whole exuberant scene. Labadi, like the entire Accra coast and much of the Gulf of Guinea, is increasingly fouled by plastic and waste. The crucial and often-missed point is the source. The garbage is not, for the most part, dropped by the people on the beach. It is generated inland, across a fast-growing city, and dumped into drains, gutters, and streams that carry it to the ocean, from which the tide returns it and lays it along the sand. The beach the city loves is handed the city's own waste with every tide.

The effect is a place that is genuinely beautiful in its human energy and genuinely fouled in its physical reality, frequently at the same moment. A swim at Labadi is not the clean experience the music and the horses imply, and pretending otherwise would be a disservice.

## The Pushback

What keeps this from being a counsel of despair is that the problem is named and fought rather than hidden. Weekly volunteer clean-ups work the tide line, including Sunday morning efforts on neighboring stretches of the La coast, and organized projects such as the **Ghana Clean-up Project** have partnered with venues on the beach itself, like the Polo Beach Club, to handle collected debris and separate recyclable plastic. It is, honestly, a fight against a problem produced faster upstream than any beach crew can clear it. But it is a serious, organized one, and it has made Labadi a visible front in Ghana's national reckoning with plastic.

## Holding Both

The temptation with a beach like Labadi is to tell only one story: either the joyous African pleasure beach of the brochures, or the cautionary tale of the polluted coast. Both of those are real, and each on its own is a lie by omission. The truthful Labadi is the one that holds them together, a whole city's music, movement, and weekend life poured onto one strip of sand, on a coast its own city is polluting faster than anyone can clean. Come for the reggae and the horses and the crowd. Look at the tide line while you do.
