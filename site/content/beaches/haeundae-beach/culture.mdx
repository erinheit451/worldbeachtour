# The Beach Korea Films, Floods, and Photographs of Itself

Haeundae is the stretch of sand a whole country reaches for when it wants to picture summer, and it has spent a century earning the cliche. A ninth-century poet left his pen name on the rock at its western end; a 2009 blockbuster drowned its summer crowd on screen for more than eleven million paying viewers. This page is about everything that has been filmed, festivaled, and inscribed onto these 1.46 kilometers — the cultural weight that no reef or wave put there.

## The Beach That Korea Watched Get Destroyed

In July 2009 director Yoon Je-kyoon released "Haeundae," titled "Tidal Wave" in English, and billed as South Korea's first true disaster blockbuster. The plot is specifically, deliberately local: a megatsunami forms in the sea off Japan and races toward Busan, and the climax breaks the wave over this exact beach at the height of its summer crowd. Made for around US$16 million, it opened on 22 July 2009 and drew more than 11.3 million admissions, earning roughly US$74.4 million at the box office. Yoon became the fifth director in Korean history to pass ten million viewers on a single film.

The setting was the whole point. You cannot build a national disaster film on an anonymous coast — you build it on the one beach every citizen has either stood on or seen photographed at its most crowded, so the wave lands as collective fear rather than spectacle. The horror works because the crowd in the frame is the crowd from everyone's own holidays. The result is a strange double image that no other beach carries: the place the country pictures as the peak of summer is also the place the country paid to watch annihilated.

Screen fame runs deeper than one film. Haeundae district is the spiritual home of the Busan International Film Festival, founded in 1996 as the first international film festival in Korea and now widely regarded as Asia's most prestigious. Its first edition ran 13–21 September 1996, rooted in the Haeundae and Nampo-dong districts before the festival moved to its purpose-built home, the Busan Cinema Center in nearby Centum City, for its sixteenth edition in 2011. The Cinema Center is a short metro ride from the sand. For two weeks each autumn the beach district is the center of Asian cinema, which is a different kind of famous than the parasol photographs but feeds the same legend.

## A Festival Calendar That Runs All Year

Most beaches have a season. Haeundae has a calendar, and it is full in winter as well as summer. The strangest entry comes in January: the Haeundae Polar Bear Festival, first held in 1988 to mark the Seoul Olympics of that year. It began with around a hundred swimmers and now draws on the order of fifty thousand domestic and overseas visitors, several thousand of whom actually plunge into the near-freezing winter sea. The BBC once listed it among the ten most unusual winter sports events in the world, which is the kind of distinction a beach cannot manufacture on purpose.

Late spring brings the Haeundae Sand Festival, described as Korea's only sand-related eco-festival. International sculptors — in the 2026 edition, eleven artists from Korea, Canada, China, France, and Taiwan — build large-scale sand sculptures and reliefs on the beach, themed each year (recent themes have traced Busan's own history in sand), with sandboarding, workshops, night illuminations, and fireworks around them. The festival concentrates at the Haeundae Beach Event Plaza and along Gunam-ro, the road that runs down to the sand, and the sculptures are left standing for weeks after the event itself ends.

Then comes the summer headliner. The Busan Sea Festival, running since 1996 as Korea's first general ocean festival, opens on 1 August and spreads across Haeundae, Gwangalli, and the city's other beaches, with Haeundae as its main stage. It bundles a rock music festival, beach concerts and K-pop shows, water sports, and fireworks into the hottest, most crowded stretch of the year — a festival layered on top of a beach that is already running at capacity, which is a very Haeundae idea of a good time.

## Nurimaru, the Headland Where the World Met

At the western end of the beach is Dongbaekseom, the Camellia Island headland — once a true island, now joined to the mainland by silt and reclamation. Its coastal loop walk is the one place where the towers fall behind you and the rock, the camellia trees, and the offshore Oryukdo islets become the whole view. The walk is the antidote to everything dense and engineered about the main beach.

On the headland sits Nurimaru APEC House, built to host the leaders' meeting of the 2005 APEC summit. The name itself is a small piece of culture: it combines the Korean words *nuri*, meaning "world," and *maru*, meaning "summit" or pavilion — "a house where the world's leaders gather." Architect Yang Jin-hwang, working with a tiny plot of land hard against the sea and heavy security constraints, answered with a contemporary version of a *jeongja*, a traditional Korean pavilion, its three-story roofline drawn from the slope of Dongbaekseom itself. Visiting heads of state and global media praised it as among the most beautiful summit venues ever used, and it now stands open to the public as a free landmark and viewpoint. For a few days in November 2005 this small wooded headland off a holiday beach was a stage for the leaders of the Pacific rim.

## The Crowd as a National Self-Portrait

The single most reproduced image Korea exports of itself may be an aerial shot of this beach with no sand visible at all — solid parasol-to-parasol cover, tens of thousands of people in a meter of warm water. That image is not staged exaggeration; it is the ordinary peak-summer condition of a beach only 1.46 kilometers long sitting at the end of a subway line in a city of over three million, in a country where summer leave compresses into a few intense weeks. On the hottest weekends the sand runs at or near its stated capacity of roughly 120,000 people, and across a season Haeundae takes in something close to ten million visitors.

The crowd has been formalized into a record. On 21 July 2008, local authorities planted 7,937 beach umbrellas on the sand and claimed a Guinness World Record for the most beach umbrellas in one place — a record that is really a census of the crowd it was built to shade. This is the honest, slightly uncomfortable core of Haeundae's culture: the thing that makes it a national symbol is the saturation, and the saturation is the hardest part of actually being there. If your idea of a great beach is space and quiet, peak-season Haeundae is the deliberate opposite. The crush is not a failure of the place. The crush is the place, and the country chose it as its picture of summer with full knowledge of the parasols.

## After Dark: Towers, Cafes, and the City Behind the Sand

When the sun drops, Haeundae stops being a beach and becomes a lit-up city waterfront. The Marine City cluster on reclaimed land and the LCT The Sharp towers glow over the water; from the western end the lights of the Gwangan Bridge are visible across the bay. The sand quiets while the streets immediately behind it — the hot-spring district, the seafood alleys, the bars and cafes off Gunam-ro and around Haeundae Market — run late. The real eating and drinking of Haeundae happens a block inland from the sand, not on it: Busan's raw fish (*hoe*), the cloudy pork-and-rice soup *dwaeji gukbap*, the seed-stuffed *ssiat hotteok* of the local market.

The nightlife is genuinely commercialized, and worth being honest about. Summer evenings bring open-air clubs, EDM beach parties, and nighttime swimming in lit zones; after about 10 PM in season the beach tilts firmly toward party rather than postcard. The cafe culture is real but it is a coastal-city cafe culture of view-windows and chain roasters facing a wall of high-rises, not a quiet fishing-village scene. That wall of towers is the postcard and the grievance at once — it is why Haeundae photographs like a global metropolis, and it is exactly what residents and critics point to when they argue the coast has been overbuilt into a row of mostly-investment apartments that block the light, the breeze, and the access to the very sea they advertise. The night skyline is beautiful and it is contested, and both of those are true at the same time.

## A Poet's Pen Name, Carved in Stone

Everything above — the film, the festivals, the towers, the crowd — is younger than the name. "Haeundae" comes from Choe Chiwon, a celebrated scholar-official and poet of the late Unified Silla period, born in 857, who studied sixteen years in Tang China and passed its imperial examination before returning home. One of his literary names was Haeun, meaning "sea and clouds." By tradition he passed through this coast on his way toward Gayasan mountain, was captivated by the view from the headland, and carved the three characters *Hae-un-dae* into the southern rock face of Dongbaekseom. The inscription site was designated Busan Metropolitan Monument No. 45 in 1999.

There is an honest wrinkle worth keeping. Choe is in fact better known by another of his pen names, Go-un — "Lonely Cloud" — said to reflect the difficulty of his later life; Haeun is the secondary name, and it is the secondary one that stuck to the most crowded beach in the country. A solitary ninth-century poet's quiet name for himself now sits over ten million summer visitors, a parasol world record, a film festival, and a wall of 400-meter towers. The beach Korea uses to picture its loudest, most collective summer is named for a man whose lasting reputation is loneliness.
