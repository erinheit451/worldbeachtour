# Rockaway Beach Travel Guide

Rockaway Beach is New York City's only legal surfing beach and the only true ocean beach on a barrier island in the five boroughs. Located on the Rockaway Peninsula in Queens, this 5.5-mile stretch of sand is where New Yorkers escape for swell, boardwalk walks, and the raw Atlantic experience. Whether you're catching waves, strolling the Boardwalk, or simply watching storms roll across open water, Rockaway delivers a gritty authenticity rare in an urban setting.

## Getting There

### By Air

**John F. Kennedy International Airport (JFK)** is just 9.7 kilometers away—the closest major airport to any NYC beach. From JFK, Rockaway is approximately 30–45 minutes away by car or subway depending on traffic and connections.

**LaGuardia Airport (LGA)** and **Newark Airport (EWR)** are also options but further away (40–60 minutes).

### By Subway & Public Transit

This is the best way to reach Rockaway from Manhattan or Brooklyn.

**Via NYC Subway (most direct):**
- Take the **A train from Manhattan** (any station) heading downtown toward Far Rockaway or Rockaway Park.
- The A train terminates at either **Far Rockaway–Mott Avenue** or **Rockaway Park–Beach 116 Street** (the branches serve different sections of the beach).
- From downtown Manhattan, the journey takes approximately 45–60 minutes depending on which terminal you choose.
- Subway fare: **$2.90** per trip.

**Beach Sections by Terminal:**
- **Far Rockaway–Mott Avenue station**: Serves Beaches 67–71 (central surf zone, boardwalk access, most facilities)
- **Rockaway Park–Beach 116 Street**: Serves Beaches 87–92 (quieter western section, fewer crowds)

Once at the terminal, follow signs to the beach—it's a short walk.

### By Ferry

**NYC Ferry** operates a seasonal route from **Wall Street/Pier 11** (Lower Manhattan) to **Rockaway Beach** during summer months (typically May–September). The ferry ride is approximately 1 hour and offers scenic views across Jamaica Bay and Lower Manhattan. This is an excellent alternative to the A train if you want a memorable arrival. Check NYC Ferry's website for current schedules and pricing.

### By Car

If driving:
- **From Manhattan**: Take the **FDR Drive** south to the **West Side Highway**, then follow signs toward the **Williamsburg Bridge** or **Manhattan Bridge**. Once in Brooklyn, take **Atlantic Avenue** east or use **I-278/Brooklyn–Queens Expressway** heading toward Queens. Follow signs to **Rockaway Parkway** and then to the **Rockaway Peninsula**. Total distance: approximately 25 miles; plan 45–90 minutes depending on traffic. Avoid rush hours (7–10 AM and 3–7 PM weekdays).

- **From JFK Airport**: Take **Jamaica Expressway (I-440)** north toward the **Belt Parkway**. Follow Belt Parkway west toward **Rockaway Parkway**. Total distance: approximately 20 miles; plan 30–45 minutes in normal traffic.

**Parking:** Free street parking is widely available near Rockaway Beach, though popular sections near the Boardwalk fill up on sunny weekends and summer days. Arrive early or use less crowded sections of the beach (Beaches 87–92) for guaranteed parking.

## Best Time to Visit

### Seasonal Overview

**Surf Season (September–May)** is when Rockaway reveals its identity. Atlantic swells are reliable, water temperatures range from 45–60°F (7–16°C), and wetsuits are essential. September and May offer the best combination of swell and water warmth. Winter months (November–March) produce the biggest and most consistent waves, attracting serious surfers.

**Summer (June–August)** is the social season. The water is warmest (65–72°F/18–22°C), lifeguards are on duty, and the Boardwalk bustles with activity. However, swell is minimal—the ocean goes flat. This is when families, swimmers, and casual beach-goers dominate.

**Spring (April–May)** and **Fall (September–October)** offer balanced conditions: moderate swell, cooler water (still wettable), and smaller crowds than summer. September is particularly strong for surf.

**Winter (November–March)** is serious surfer territory. Swell is biggest, but water is cold (45–52°F/7–11°C) and storms can produce dangerous conditions. Lifeguards are not on duty. This is a local season.

### Lifeguard Coverage

Lifeguards are on duty **Memorial Day through Labor Day** (late May to early September) at designated sections, primarily Beaches 67–71. During off-season, you swim at your own risk.

### Water Quality

Water quality at Rockaway can degrade rapidly after heavy rainfall due to stormwater runoff and combined sewer overflows. The NYC Department of Environmental Protection (DEP) monitors water quality. **Check DEP's beach water quality advisories before swimming**, particularly after storms.

## Where to Stay

Rockaway Beach itself has limited lodging options—it's primarily a day-trip destination for New Yorkers, though some visitors stay overnight.

### On Rockaway Peninsula

Several small motels and vacation rentals operate on the peninsula. Options are limited but functional. Expect **$80–150/night** for basic rooms. These are motel-style accommodations, not resorts.

### Nearby Queens Neighborhoods

**Forest Hills** (approximately 5 miles inland) and **Jackson Heights** (approximately 4 miles) offer more diverse lodging options and better restaurant access. A 15–20 minute drive or bus ride gets you to Rockaway. This is a middle-ground option for those wanting more urban amenities.

### Brooklyn or Manhattan Day Trip

Most visitors stay in **Brooklyn** (Williamsburg, Park Slope, Sunset Park) or **Manhattan** and day-trip to Rockaway. From Manhattan, the A train gets you to the beach in under an hour. This is the most common approach for tourists. Hotel availability and dining options are vastly superior in Brooklyn/Manhattan.

### Accommodation Types

- **Vacation rentals**: Variable quality, $100–200/night
- **Budget motels**: $70–120/night; functional, modest amenities
- **Airbnb-style rooms**: $80–150/night

## Costs

### General Price Level

**Rockaway Beach itself is free to access.** There is no entrance fee, parking fee, or day-use charge.

### Nearby Services

**Food and beverage**: Boardwalk cafes and restaurants are modest and casual ($8–20 for lunch). A picnic from a local deli or supermarket costs $10–20 per person.

**Rentals**: Surfboard and paddleboard rentals operate year-round from local shops; expect **$20–35/hour** or **$40–70/day** for basic boards. Premium rental shops may charge more.

**Parking**: Free throughout most of Rockaway.

**Transportation**: NYC subway is $2.90 one-way. Ferry from Manhattan is approximately $5–7 one-way (when operating). Ride-share from Manhattan is $15–25.

**Overall daily budget**: Budget $40–70 per person for a day visit (food + transit), or $100–180 if staying overnight and renting equipment.

## Safety

### Surfing & Water Safety

- **Strong Atlantic rips and currents** are common at Rockaway. Do not underestimate the ocean. Never surf or swim alone.
- **Lifeguards** are on duty **Memorial Day to Labor Day only** at designated sections (primarily Beaches 67–71). Outside this period, there is no lifeguard coverage—you swim at your own risk.
- **Water temperature** ranges from 48°F (9°C) in winter to 72°F (22°C) in summer. Wetsuits are essential year-round except peak summer.
- **Shark incidents**: Zero recorded incidents at Rockaway Beach.
- **Rock and jetty hazards**: Some beach sections have jetties and submerged rocks, particularly at Beaches 67 and 71 (surf zones). Know the breaks before paddling out.

### General Beach Safety

- Rockaway is a generally safe, well-policed neighborhood. Petty theft from vehicles and unattended belongings does occur; do not leave valuables in your car or unattended on the beach.
- **Avoid the beach alone after dark.**
- **Check water quality advisories** before swimming, especially post-rainfall.

## Local Tips

**Bring a wetsuit year-round.** Even in summer, a thin wetsuit (3mm) makes a difference. Winter wetsuits (5mm+) are non-negotiable.

**Arrive early on weekends.** Rockaway's popularity has grown significantly. Weekend parking near the Boardwalk fills up by 10 AM on summer days.

**The Brooklyn deli surfing scene is real.** There's a distinct subculture of subway surfers from Brooklyn who commute to Rockaway—they're welcoming but have their own spots and breaks.

**Beaches 67, 69, and 71 are the main breaks.** These are where the swells organize and where you'll find the most active water sports scene. Beaches 87–92 are quieter but smaller swell.

**The Rockaway Boardwalk is the social heart.** Unlike most NYC beaches, Rockaway has a genuine boardwalk experience. Walk it, spend time, soak in the local vibe.

**Ramones history matters here.** The 1976 song "Rockaway Beach" captured something true about this place. Locals know it.

**Storm watching is excellent in winter.** When Atlantic storms approach, Rockaway's exposure creates dramatic conditions—big swells, wind, and moody skies. This is when the beach reveals its power.

**Ferry from Wall Street is worth the trip.** If available (summer), the NYC Ferry to Rockaway is a scenic, memorable way to arrive. The ferry passes through Jamaica Bay and Lower Manhattan.

**Water quality matters.** Always check DEP advisories. Post-rainfall, stay out of the water for 24–48 hours.
