Fig Tree Bay is a 500-metre crescent of pale gold sand at Protaras, on the eastern coast of Cyprus in the Paralimni municipality of the Ammochostos (Famagusta) district. It takes its name from the fig trees that grow along the coast behind it. The bay is sheltered and its water shallow and clear, with a small uninhabited islet just offshore, and it is best known abroad for a ranking: TripAdvisor's Travellers' Choice awards placed it third among European beaches in 2011.

The beach is physically small and intensely used. A curve of fine sand runs between low rocky margins, and the islet, covered in low scrub, sits close enough to shore that swimmers wade and paddle out to it through shallow water. The water stays shallow and gentle well out into the bay, which is the real reason families favour it. This is not an untouched cove: a continuous wall of Protaras hotels, apartments, and tavernas presses up directly behind the sand, and the beach itself is maintained. In 2018 the Paralimni municipality pumped sand onto the shore to counter erosion.

The name is older than the resort, but the single "ancient fig tree" of the travel brochures is not documented. Sources that assign the bay a lone 400-year-old or 800-year-old tree, planted by Venetians or other settlers, trace to tourism copy rather than records and contradict one another. What is documented is deeper time of a different kind: in May 2010, workers cutting the coastal footpath behind the beach uncovered an un-looted tomb about 2,400 years old, from around 300 BC, holding four sarcophagi with painted floral lids, human bones, glass, and pottery.

Protaras is a resort at all because of 1974. When Famagusta's Varosha resort strip was evacuated and sealed after the Turkish invasion that August, the tourism industry relocated down the surviving coast to Protaras and Ayia Napa. The crowd behind Fig Tree Bay is that displaced industry, rebuilt. The beach today has lifeguards from 9am to 6pm between April and October, a municipal car park, and Blue Flag certification for water quality and management, and water-sports operators run water-skiing, windsurfing, and parasailing off the bay in season.

**Most Relevant Lenses:**

**Family & Safety** — The shallow, sheltered, gently sloping bay with summer lifeguard coverage is the beach's core appeal for young children and nervous swimmers. Modelled wave heights average around 0.67 metres, biggest in January.

**History** — Four pasts are stacked on one small bay: the fig trees that named it, an un-looted Hellenistic tomb under the footpath, the 1974 war that moved a resort industry onto this coast, and the 2011 ranking that made the name famous.

**Geology & Landscape** — Quartz-dominant sand (modelled near 68% quartz) reading pale gold, a scrub-covered offshore islet, and a shoreline the municipality has had to replenish by pumping.

**Snorkelling** — The shallow, clear water and the rocks around the islet make for easy, low-risk snorkelling, though this is a busy resort bay rather than a rich reef.

**Photography** — Turquoise shallows and the low islet in front, the packed Protaras strip behind: the honest frame of Fig Tree Bay holds both the postcard and the development at once.

Fig Tree Bay works because it is a safe, well-run, genuinely pretty family beach. It is also small, heavily built up, and maintained by hand, and it is worth seeing as what it is rather than as the untouched cove its ranking implies.
