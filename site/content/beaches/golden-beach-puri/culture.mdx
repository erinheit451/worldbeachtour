# The Sacred Shore: Pilgrims, Chariots, and the Men in Conical Hats

Most beaches are places people go to get away from their lives. Puri's beach is the opposite: it is the sea-edge of a city people travel across India to reach for the most important religious act of their lives, and the beach is woven into that act. To understand Golden Beach you have to start with the temple behind it.

## One of the Four Char Dham

Puri is one of the Char Dham, the four cardinal pilgrimage sites of Hinduism, alongside Badrinath in the north, Dwarka in the west, and Rameswaram in the south. The city is built around the great Jagannath Temple, raised in the 12th century under the Eastern Ganga dynasty. Its inner sanctum is open only to Hindus, and for the pilgrims who come, the visit is not complete without a bathe in the Bay of Bengal. The beach is, in religious terms, part of the temple's grounds, the place where the pilgrimage meets the sea.

## The Festival That Gave English a Word

Once a year, in the monsoon month of Ashadha, the city stages the Rath Yatra, the Chariot Festival, and it is one of the largest religious gatherings on earth. The three deities, Jagannath, his brother Balabhadra, and their sister Subhadra, are brought out of the temple and pulled along the Bada Danda, the Grand Road, to the Gundicha Temple about three kilometers away, on enormous wooden chariots built new every single year. Jagannath's car stands roughly 14 meters high. Crowds of devotees haul the chariots with thick ropes, and the press of people around the moving cars is so total that the English word **juggernaut**, meaning an unstoppable, crushing force, comes directly from Jagannath and these chariots. For the days of the festival, the city and its beach are submerged under well over a million pilgrims.

## The Nulias

Against all of this stands the most distinctive institution on the beach: the **Nulia**. The Nulias are hereditary fishermen of Puri who have served as the shore's lifeguards since the British colonial period, more than a century of it, and they are unmistakable in their tall, conical white hats. Today over 450 of them work the coast under a lifeguard federation, covering everything from the hotel stretches to the certified Blue Flag zone.

They are essential, not decorative. The Bay of Bengal here runs strong rip currents, and the bathers are very often inland pilgrims who have never seen the sea and cannot swim, drawn into the water by the ritual rather than by any beach experience. The Nulias read the currents, whistle people back from the rip lines, and go in after those who get pulled out, rescuing dozens in a single busy week and far more across a season. The reason Puri's drowning toll is not catastrophic is this community, doing physically brutal, long-informal work for a city that runs on the pilgrims they protect.

## The Sand as a Stage

Puri's other cultural signature is made of the beach itself. **Sudarsan Pattnaik**, the internationally awarded sand sculptor honored with the Padma Shri in 2014, is based here, where he runs a sand-art institute and has turned the tideline into a gallery. His large sculptures, often carrying social or devotional messages, and the international sand-art festival he is associated with have made Puri one of the most famous places in the world for the form. Alongside the annual Puri Beach Festival, co-sponsored by the national tourism ministry and regional cultural bodies, the sand becomes, for a few weeks each year, a stage for music, dance, handicraft, and sculpture.

## Three Indias on One Strip of Sand

What makes Golden Beach culturally singular is the overlay. On the same sand, at the same time, you find the ancient pilgrimage, an inland family doing a ritual sea-bathe before the temple darshan; the modern, Blue-Flagged tourism product, with its certified zone and sand-art festival; and the working fishing community whose men, in their conical hats, quietly keep both groups alive in the water. The beach does not resolve these into one thing. It just holds them together, the way Puri has held them together for centuries.
