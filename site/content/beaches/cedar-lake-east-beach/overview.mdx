Cedar Lake East Beach, known locally as Hidden Beach or Cedar Lake Point, is Minneapolis's most distinctive freshwater swimming destination and a defining feature of the Chain of Lakes Regional Park. Situated on the east shore of Cedar Lake in the heart of the city's Kenwood neighborhood, this 0.8-mile crescent of sand offers something rare in urban freshwater recreation: genuine cultural significance as the traditional clothing-optional gathering space at the lake's north end, alongside a fully supervised public beach serving thousands of summer swimmers.

The lake itself—one of four linked lakes comprising Minneapolis's most visited park—occupies a remarkable geographic position. Just 15 kilometers northeast of Minneapolis-St Paul International Airport (MSP), Cedar Lake sits within a dense urban fabric yet feels genuinely removed from city noise. The surrounding landscape is entirely shaped by human design: mature oak and pine forest canopy, manicured picnic areas, and the famous Midtown Greenway bike trail running directly along the lake's perimeter.

What sets Cedar Lake East Beach apart is its dual identity. The southern sections are fully developed public beaches with parking, restrooms, shower facilities, and lifeguard coverage during summer months. The northern end—Hidden Beach—maintains a distinct cultural character as a long-established clothing-optional space, attracting a loyal community of naturist swimmers and sunbathers who've used this section for decades. Both communities coexist with minimal friction, and visitors are expected to respect the unwritten geography.

The natural history deserves attention too. Cedar Lake's shallow waters support surprising biodiversity for an urban lake: 20 observed species including Wood Ducks, Mallards, Great Blue Herons, and Wild Turkeys. The surrounding wetland plants—caltha palustris (marsh marigold), mertensia virginica (Virginia bluebells)—create a functioning riparian ecosystem even within a developed urban park.

The beach embodies Minneapolis's progressive outdoor culture: a place where public recreation, naturist tradition, ecological function, and urban design coexist. Swimming season is brief—June through August—when water temperatures reach the high 60s to low 70s Fahrenheit, creating a focused, intense summer community.

**Most Relevant Lenses:**

**Recreation & Community** — Cedar Lake East Beach is fundamentally a local gathering space, not a destination beach. The chain of lakes bike loop, Midtown Greenway access, and surrounding Uptown dining create an integrated leisure ecosystem that defines Minneapolis summer culture.

**Naturism** — Hidden Beach's clothing-optional tradition is central to its identity. This is one of the Upper Midwest's few established public clothing-optional beaches, with a multi-decade community history.

**Urban Ecology** — The lake's functioning wetland ecosystem, migratory bird populations, and native plant communities offer genuine environmental interest despite urban development. The beach demonstrates how freshwater systems serve ecological function in dense cities.

**Cycling & Active Transport** — The Midtown Greenway and Chain of Lakes Trail create one of North America's most complete freshwater urban cycling networks. Cedar Lake is inseparable from this transportation and recreation spine.

**Seasonality** — Minnesota's brief, intense summer (June–August) creates a concentrated cultural moment. The beach is essentially dormant outside this window, creating urgency and community consciousness during the swim season.

Cedar Lake East Beach succeeds because it unapologetically serves its communities—swimmers, naturists, cyclists, and wildlife—without pretending to be anything other than what it is: a working urban freshwater beach embedded in one of America's most progressive park systems.
