# Orchard Beach: How Robert Moses Built the Bronx a Coastline

The only public beach in the Bronx did not exist before the 1930s. Where the 1.1-mile crescent of sand now curves along the western edge of Long Island Sound, there was a marshy tidal lagoon separating the mainland at Rodman's Neck from two small offshore lands, Hunter Island and the Twin Islands. Orchard Beach is the result of a decision by one Parks Commissioner, Robert Moses, to fill that lagoon, tie the islands to the Bronx, and lay down sand that came from two other coastlines entirely.

## The Lagoon Before the Beach

Moses was appointed New York City Parks Commissioner in 1934, and the Orchard Beach project began almost immediately. The native shoreline of the western Long Island Sound is not a swimming beach: the sand is gray, pebbly glacial material, and the coast is broken into necks, inlets, and small islands. The site Moses chose was a tidal lagoon inside Pelham Bay Park, the largest park in New York City. To make a continuous beach, the lagoon was filled and Hunter Island and the Twin Islands were joined to the mainland by landfill. The islands survive in name and in their wooded character at the north end of the beach, but the water that once separated them from the Bronx is gone.

## Sand From the Rockaways and Sandy Hook

The detail that defines Orchard Beach is the sand. Moses disliked the Sound's native gray sand so much that he would not build a beach out of it. Instead, fine, pale sand was dredged from the Rockaway Peninsula in Queens and from Sandy Hook in New Jersey and carried north by barge — on the order of four million cubic yards of it — to form the swimming crescent. The beach you stand on was mined from two Atlantic coastlines and shipped in. That is also why it has to be replenished periodically: imported sand on an engineered estuary shore does not stay in place by itself.

The work was Depression-era public relief, funded and staffed through the federal Works Progress Administration. It was one of a wave of beaches and pools Moses opened across the city in the summer of 1936.

## The Aymar Embury II Pavilion

A built beach got a monumental building. Moses hired the society architect Aymar Embury II — his recurring designer across the 1930s parks program — to create the bathhouse and promenade pavilion, completed in 1936. It is a concrete, brick, and limestone structure with two long colonnades that radiate from a raised central terrace, finished with blue tile and terrazzo, fronting a promenade paved in hexagonal blocks. The grand colonnade is often compared to the Palais de Chaillot in Paris. In 2006 the New York City Landmarks Preservation Commission designated the bathhouse, promenade, and beach a city landmark.

The dedication ceremony was held on July 25, 1936, and drew more than 18,000 people, though construction of the full beach continued into 1938. The combination of calm Sound water and bright new sand earned the place a nickname that has lasted ninety years: the Bronx Riviera.

## Thirteen Sections and the Salsa Beach

Orchard Beach is divided into thirteen numbered sections, and over time those sections became social territory. From the 1950s on, the central sections became the gathering place of the borough's Puerto Rican and Dominican families. Mambo dancer and DJ Ernie Ensley began spinning records at the beach, and the open concrete deck behind Sections 4 and 5 grew into a home for salsa and Afro-Caribbean music. The tradition continued under DJ Carlos Santiago as Salsa Sundays, and the U.S. National Park Service has formally documented Orchard Beach as a cultural hub for salsa. An engineered beach became one of the most authentic cultural spaces in the Bronx.

## Decline, Water, and Restoration

The built environment aged unevenly. The Embury pavilion, the literal centerpiece of the 1936 design, spent roughly seventeen years closed to the public before a major restoration reopened it, and the beach's facilities have cycled through decline and reinvestment for decades.

The one part of Orchard Beach that no commissioner could command is the water. The beach faces the western Long Island Sound, downstream of a dense city's combined sewers, and after heavy rain bacteria levels can rise far enough that New York City advises against swimming and wading. In drier stretches the same beach has earned an 'A' grade for water quality. Robert Moses could barge in sand and hire an architect for the colonnade, but he could not make the Sound clean — and ninety years on, that remains the one entirely natural fact about an otherwise manufactured beach.
