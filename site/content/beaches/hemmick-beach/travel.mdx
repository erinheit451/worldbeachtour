# Hemmick Beach Travel Guide

Hemmick Beach is a secluded cove on Cornwall's Roseland Peninsula, accessible only by narrow single-track lanes and a small car park. Located between Dodman Point and Nare Head, this undeveloped beach offers raw coastal character, cold Atlantic swimming, and the solitude that comes from deliberate isolation. The SW Coast Path runs along the surrounding cliffs, and the nearest village services are several kilometers away. This is a beach that rewards preparation and self-reliance.

## Getting There

### By Air

Most visitors to Cornwall fly into **Cornwall Airport Newquay (NQY)**, located 26.7 kilometers from Hemmick Beach—approximately 45 minutes to 1 hour drive. Newquay handles flights from the UK, Europe, and occasional international connections.

An alternative is **Exeter International Airport (EXT)**, 150 kilometers away (approximately 2.5–3 hours drive). Exeter offers more frequent connections and often competitive fares; the longer drive is worthwhile if prices are significantly lower.

Both airports have car rental facilities. You will need a vehicle to reach Hemmick Beach reliably.

### By Car (Recommended)

**From Newquay (26.7 km, 45 minutes):** Take the B3276 south toward Gorran Haven. Continue through the village and follow signs toward Hemmick Beach. The final approach is a narrow single-track lane with passing places—drive slowly and be prepared to reverse if meeting oncoming traffic. The lane is steep and winding; large vehicles and caravans are not recommended.

**From Exeter (150 km, 2.5–3 hours):** Take the A30 west, then branch onto the A391 toward St Austell. From St Austell, follow the B3273 toward Mevagissey, then the B3276 to Gorran Haven. The final 3 kilometers follow the same narrow lane as above.

**From other Cornish towns:**
- **From Falmouth** (20 km, 35 minutes): East toward Truro, then south via Tregony and Mevagissey, then Gorran Haven lane.
- **From Truro** (25 km, 40 minutes): South on the A39 toward Mevagissey, then Gorran Haven lane.
- **From Penzance** (80 km, 1.5 hours): East on the A30, then as from Exeter route above.

The final 3-kilometer approach from Gorran Haven is the critical segment. This narrow lane is single-track with gravel sections and steep gradients. It floods in heavy rain and can be impassable in winter storms. In summer (July–August), expect congestion and long waits at passing places. The small car park holds approximately 20–30 vehicles; arrive early (before 10 AM on weekends) or be prepared to park in Gorran Haven village and walk the lane (1 kilometer extra).

### By Public Transit

Public bus service to Hemmick Beach is minimal. **Coastliner service 24A** connects Mevagissey to Gorran Haven, but the service is limited (2–3 daily in summer, less frequent in winter). From Gorran Haven, the beach is a 1-kilometer walk down the steep lane.

**Newquay to Mevagissey:** Take the **Newquay & District** service 556 or 557 (roughly 1.5 hours, 2–3 daily). Then connect to the Coastliner 24A service, adding another 20 minutes to Gorran Haven.

This journey is slow and impractical for day visitors. A hired car is strongly recommended.

### By Boat

No ferry or water taxi service serves Hemmick Beach. Private boats can anchor offshore in calm conditions, but there is no landing infrastructure or facilities. Tidal range is significant (approximately 7 meters), and the beach has rocky outcrops requiring careful navigation. This option is only viable for experienced mariners with appropriate vessels.

## Best Time to Visit

### Seasonal Overview

**Summer (July–August)** is the only period when water temperature approaches comfortable levels (approximately 15–16°C). Even then, immersion hypothermia is a genuine risk for prolonged swimming. Air temperatures reach 18–22°C. The narrow access lane becomes heavily congested on sunny weekends, and the car park fills by mid-morning. Visitors must arrive early and be prepared for significant waits at passing places.

**Spring (May–June)** offers mild air temperatures (14–18°C) and declining crowds compared to peak summer. Water remains cold (12–14°C). The landscape is actively green, wildflowers bloom on the cliff tops, and the SW Coast Path is at its most accessible. June is ideal for hiking.

**Autumn (September–October)** delivers comfortable walking conditions (12–16°C) and substantially reduced visitor numbers. September still carries some summer warmth. October brings increasing cloud and rain but remains walkable. Swell conditions improve as Atlantic storm systems develop.

**Winter (November–March)** is cool (6–10°C) and wet, with frequent rain and storm winds. The access lane becomes unreliable due to flooding and surface degradation. However, this is when dramatic Atlantic storm conditions occur—huge swell, moody light, and solitude. Only visit in winter with full storm-weather preparation and reliable transport.

### Tidal Considerations

Hemmick Beach has a tidal range of approximately 7 meters (spring tides). At low tide, the beach expands significantly, exposing sand and rock pools. At high tide, the beach becomes narrow and access to the back of the cove is limited. Always check tide tables before visiting; low tide provides the most usable beach and safer swimming conditions.

## Where to Stay

Hemmick Beach has no accommodation directly at the cove. Nearby villages offer limited services; advance booking is essential, particularly in summer.

### Gorran Haven (Closest, 1 km upslope)

Gorran Haven is a small fishing village with a handful of vacation rentals and one basic guesthouse. The village has a pub, general store, and post office. Accommodation is extremely limited; book months in advance for summer. Staying here minimizes driving and allows early beach access.

**Options:** Vacation rentals (1–2 bedrooms, typically £80–150/night), basic guesthouse (£60–100/night).

### Mevagissey (5.1 km, 10 minutes drive)

The nearest proper town with wider accommodation variety. Mevagissey is a working fishing port with a harbor, coastal path access, and sufficient restaurants and shops. This is where most Hemmick Beach visitors base themselves.

**Options:**
- **Budget guesthouses & B&Bs:** £55–90/night
- **Vacation rentals:** £90–180/night (1–2 bedrooms)
- **Small hotels:** £100–150/night

The town is scenic and charming; a day exploring Mevagissey's harbor and coastal walks is worthwhile even without beach time.

### Portloe (8 km, 15 minutes drive)

Another small fishing village further east along the coast, with a similar village character to Mevagissey. Slightly quieter and very scenic, with the Portloe Fishermen's Beach nearby. Limited accommodation; book ahead.

### St Mawes (12 km, 20 minutes drive)

A more established tourist village with better accommodation variety and superior restaurants. St Mawes has a castle, scenic harbor, and broader coastal infrastructure. More upscale and pricey than Mevagissey or Portloe but offers more amenities.

**Budget:** £120–180/night for decent rooms.

## Costs

### General Price Level

Hemmick Beach itself is **completely free to access**. There is no entrance fee, parking charge, or day-use payment.

### Parking

The small car park near Gorran Haven is free. However, during peak summer (July–August), all 20–30 spaces fill by mid-morning, forcing visitors to park in Gorran Haven village or nearby roads and walk (additional 1 km).

### Food & Supplies

There are **no facilities at the beach itself**. The nearest food is in Gorran Haven village (basic pub and general store) or Mevagissey (wider range of cafes, restaurants, and shops).

**Estimated meal costs:**
- **Pub meal in Gorran Haven or Mevagissey:** £12–20
- **Cafe coffee & pastry:** £4–7
- **Fish & chips takeaway:** £8–14
- **Supermarket picnic supplies:** £10–20 per person

Plan to bring a packed lunch and water unless you're willing to drive back to Mevagissey.

### Accommodation

Budget £60–150/night for local lodging, depending on standard and season. Peak summer (July–August) prices are 20–30% higher than shoulder seasons.

### Daily Budget

A day visit with parking, picnic, and travel costs approximately £15–30 per person (excluding accommodation). Overnight trips add £60–150 per night per room.

## Safety

### Water & Swimming

Hemmick Beach presents significant marine hazards:

- **Water temperature** ranges from 9–10°C in winter to 15–16°C in summer. A 5mm neoprene wetsuit is essential even in summer. Hypothermia can occur within 15–20 minutes of immersion without protective gear. **Serious swimmers should wear gloves and hoods as well as wetsuits.**
- **No lifeguards** are present. There is no rescue infrastructure. You swim entirely at your own risk.
- **Cold-water immersion shock** is a real hazard for first-time visitors. Even fit swimmers can experience involuntary breath-holding and disorientation in water this cold. Never enter alone; always use the buddy system.
- **Currents & tidal movement** are significant due to the exposed headland position. Tidal flow increases during mid-tide periods. Always be aware of the direction and strength of water movement.
- **Rocky outcrops** are present both at the waterline and submerged. Survey the beach carefully before swimming.
- **Shark incidents:** Zero recorded at Hemmick Beach. This is not a hazard.

**Swimming guidance:** Only attempt swimming if you're accustomed to cold water and properly equipped. For most visitors, paddling and beach exploration are safer alternatives.

### Access Hazards

- **The access lane** is narrow, steep, and prone to flooding in rain. Drive slowly, be prepared to reverse, and check weather forecasts before visiting in autumn or winter.
- **The car park** is small and unmanaged. During summer weekends, extremely long waits occur at passing places on the approach lane.
- **Cliff access:** The cliffs above Hemmick Beach are unstable slate. Do not walk directly on the cliff top away from the marked SW Coast Path. Cliff falls have occurred in this area.

### General Safety

Hemmick's remoteness means help is not immediately available. There is no mobile phone signal at the beach itself; ensure someone knows your plans and expected return time.

The area is safe from crime due to its remoteness, though opportunistic theft from vehicles does occur. Don't leave valuables in your car.

Sunscreen protection is essential even on cool, cloudy days; UV exposure doesn't correlate to warmth at this latitude.

## Local Tips

**Arrive before 10 AM on summer weekends.** The car park fills rapidly and the approach lane becomes congested. Arriving early is not optional if you want parking.

**Bring weather-appropriate layers at all times.** Even in summer, wind and sea spray create cold conditions away from direct sun. Winter requires full waterproof and insulated clothing even if not swimming.

**Check tide tables carefully.** The 7-meter tidal range dramatically changes the beach experience. Low tide reveals rock pools and expands the accessible sand; high tide narrows the beach considerably.

**Bring your own food and water.** There are no facilities at the beach. The nearest shop is a 10-minute drive away.

**Wear proper footwear.** The access lane has gravel and steep sections; the beach has rocky areas and tidal pools. Good hiking boots or water shoes are necessary.

**Combine with the SW Coast Path.** The Dodman Point to Nare Head section is spectacular. A 2–3 hour coastal walk incorporating Hemmick Beach makes an excellent half-day outing.

**Visit Gorran Haven village afterward.** The harbor walk and small village exploration add atmosphere and, importantly, a place to eat and warm up.

**The Lost Gardens of Heligan** (Napa Cabbage estate garden, 5 km away) makes an excellent half-day companion visit, particularly if weather deteriorates.

**Winter storm watching is exceptional.** When Atlantic storm systems arrive (November–March), Hemmick Beach's exposed position captures dramatic swell and moody light. Bring a camera and waterproof gear.

**Don't attempt the access lane in heavy rain or winter storms.** The lane becomes dangerous and may be impassable. Check forecasts before committing to a winter visit.
