# Saunton Sands Travel Guide

Saunton Sands is one of Southwest England's most iconic beaches—a raw, dramatic stretch of Atlantic coast backed by the Braunton Burrows dune system. Located in North Devon near the village of Saunton, approximately 80 kilometers from Exeter, this 3.5-mile beach combines world-class surf conditions, a UNESCO-recognized natural reserve, and deep historical significance. Whether you're seeking consistent waves, a dune ecosystem walk, or simply vast empty sand, Saunton rewards both serious pilgrims and casual day-trippers.

## Getting There

### By Air

Most international visitors fly into **Exeter Airport (EXT)**, approximately 80 kilometers south of Saunton Sands (roughly 90 minutes by car). Exeter is Southwest England's primary commercial hub with connections to London, major European cities, and beyond.

A smaller alternative is **Newquay Airport (NQY)** in Cornwall, about 120 kilometers south—primarily domestic and European flights, but useful if you're touring the broader Southwest.

From Exeter Airport, take the A30 west toward the coast, then north on the A361 (the scenic "Atlantic Highway"). This routing adds 20 minutes versus the direct route but passes through charming market towns and offers navigation landmarks.

### By Train + Bus

**Great Western Railway (GWR)** runs direct services from London Paddington to **Barnstaple Station** (5–6 hours depending on service). Barnstaple is the nearest significant rail hub, approximately 20 kilometers (30 minutes by car) south of Saunton.

From Barnstaple Station, **Stagecoach** and **Tiverton Coaches** operate bus services to nearby Braunton village (10 km) and towards the coast; journey time is 30–45 minutes. Alternatively, local taxis are available but unreliable—book in advance.

The train journey is slow but scenic, passing through the Midlands and Devon countryside. Budget 6–7 hours total from London including station time.

### By Car

**From London** (290 km): Take the M4 west toward Bristol, then continue via the M5 toward Exeter. Exit onto the A361 (Atlantic Highway) northbound; follow signs toward Barnstaple and the coast. Total time: 4.5–5.5 hours depending on traffic (avoid Friday afternoons and Sunday evenings when holiday traffic peaks).

**From Bristol** (150 km): Exit onto the M5 southbound toward Exeter, then A361 northbound. Plan 2.5–3 hours.

**From Cornwall** (variable): The A361 forms a scenic north-south spine through Devon. From Newquay or Padstow (both ~90 km away), head inland to the A39/A361 junction, then follow north. Allow 2–2.5 hours.

**Parking at Saunton**: Free car parks exist at the north and south ends of the beach access points. Summer weekends fill by 11 AM; arrive early or mid-week. Street parking along Saunton village offers overflow, a 10–15 minute walk from the sand.

### By Car Rental

International visitors can rent from Exeter Airport (all major companies represented). A small car is sufficient for coastal navigation; 4WD is not necessary except after heavy rain when rough beach access might be preferred.

**Petrol**: Budget £1.50–1.80 per liter. Nearest petrol stations are in Braunton village (5 km south) or Barnstaple town (20 km).

## Best Time to Visit

### Seasonal Overview

**Summer (June–August)** brings warm days (18–21°C), long daylight, and mellow Atlantic swell—ideal for families and beginner surfers. Expect crowds on weekends, particularly around school holidays (mid-July to end of August). Water temperature climbs to 16°C, still requiring a wetsuit for extended sessions but noticeably warmer than winter. The dune reserve is in full bloom with wildflowers.

**Spring (April–May)** offers mild weather (12–16°C), increasing daylight, and fewer crowds. Spring tides expose maximum sand. April rains are possible but decreasing. Excellent for wildlife observation—plants flowering, butterfly populations emerging. Water remains cold (10–12°C).

**Autumn (September–October)** mirrors spring with comfortable temperatures and declining crowds. September maintains summer swells but with fewer visitors. October sees increasing cloud and occasional rain; dramatic Atlantic storms begin to develop. Water temperature drops to 12–14°C.

**Winter (November–March)** is cool (6–10°C) and wet, with frequent Atlantic gales. However, this is peak swell season—consistent 4 to 8-foot waves, sometimes larger during major storm events. Winter dunes are dormant but still walkable. Serious surfers thrive; families typically avoid this season. Water temperature bottoms at 8–9°C in January.

## Where to Stay

### Saunton Village

Saunton itself is tiny—essentially the beach access point and the historic Saunton Sands Hotel perched on the southern cliff. The hotel dates to the 1890s and offers the most convenient waterfront location, though it's modest and can feel dated. Vacation rentals within walking distance (100–500 meters) are available through Airbnb and holiday rental sites, though options are limited. This setting is the closest you can stay to the beach action, ideal for surfers aiming for dawn sessions.

**Accommodation types**: The hotel, plus scattered vacation homes and cottages. Budget £80–200/night depending on season and amenities.

### Braunton Village

Braunton (5 km inland) is the nearest proper town, with better accommodation diversity and local services (shops, pubs, restaurants). It's still compact and rural but offers more choice than Saunton itself. A 10–15 minute drive gets you to the beach. Several bed-and-breakfasts, small inns, and vacation rentals operate year-round.

**Accommodation types**: B&Bs (£70–130/night), small hotels, vacation rentals. Much better supply than Saunton.

### Croyde Bay

Croyde Bay (2 kilometers north along the coast) is a slightly larger beach village with a stronger tourist infrastructure—more shops, pubs, and restaurants. Popular with UK holiday-makers and younger travelers. Only a 5–10 minute drive south to Saunton, making it a practical base. Summer crowds can be significant as Croyde is well-known among UK surfers.

**Accommodation types**: Holiday parks, vacation rentals, guesthouses, small hotels. Wide range, £60–180/night.

### Woolacombe

Woolacombe (8 kilometers north) is North Devon's primary beach resort—larger, busier, with full infrastructure including chain hotels, restaurants, and attractions. Better for those wanting town amenities alongside beach access. The 15–20 minute drive to Saunton is manageable for exploring both beaches.

**Accommodation types**: Hotels (£100–250/night), vacation rentals, holiday parks, hostels.

### Barnstaple

The county town of North Devon (20 kilometers south), with a full range of hotels, chains, and guesthouses. Useful as a fallback if coastal villages are booked, though staying here adds 30 minutes of drive time each way. Good if you're exploring multiple North Devon attractions beyond beaches.

## Costs

### General Price Level

**Beach Access**: Free. No entrance fees, parking fees, or day-use charges.

**Parking**: Free at designated car parks. Street parking in Saunton village is free but limited. Summer weekends may require paid overflow parking in nearby Braunton (£2–4/day).

### Food and Beverage

**Beach kiosks and cafes**: Limited food options directly on the beach. Summer may see a small cafe or food van at the car park; budget £6–10 for basic items (sandwich, drink).

**Nearby restaurants**: Braunton and Croyde offer fish-and-chip shops (£8–12), traditional pubs (£12–18 for a meal), and casual cafes (£10–15). Prices are moderate by UK standards.

**Accommodation rates**: See "Where to Stay" above. Budget £70–200/night depending on location and season.

**Camping**: Several caravan and camping parks exist in the region (Braunton, Croyde, Woolacombe). Budget £25–50/night for a site.

### Rentals and Activities

**Surfboard rentals**: Available at Croyde Bay and Woolacombe (10–20 kilometers away); not directly at Saunton. Budget £15–25/day for a board.

**Wetsuits**: Essential. Purchase or rent from Croyde or Woolacombe shops; £15–40 to buy, £8–12/day to rent.

**Guided dune walks**: Not formally organized, but local knowledge can be obtained through visitor information in Braunton.

**Overall daily budget**: £40–60 per person for a day visit (parking-free, picnic food, self-entertainment). £100–200+ if staying overnight with dining out.

## Safety

### Swimming and Water Conditions

Saunton's Atlantic exposure creates genuine ocean conditions:

- **Currents and rip tides**: Present, particularly on larger swells. Always observe other swimmers and ask locals about current conditions before entering the water.
- **Water temperature**: 8–16°C depending on season. Wetsuits are necessary year-round; cold shock and hypothermia are serious risks.
- **Waves**: Swell varies from 1–2 feet in summer to 4–8 feet in winter. Beginners should stick to summer sessions or supervised areas.
- **Lifeguards**: RNLI lifeguard patrols operate during summer school holidays (late July through early September) at designated zones. Winter has no formal supervision.
- **Visibility**: Atlantic conditions can create turbidity; visibility varies dramatically day to day.
- **Shark incidents**: Zero recorded shark incidents in UK waters near Devon.

Always use the buddy system, avoid swimming after dark, and check tide tables before entering—Saunton experiences significant tidal ranges (6–8 meters between high and low tide), exposing or submerging key landmarks.

### Dune Wildlife and Access

The Braunton Burrows behind the beach is a protected reserve:

- **Stay on marked paths** to protect rare plant populations and invertebrate habitats. Unauthorized trampling damages orchid populations and nesting sites.
- **Dogs**: Check current bylaws; some sections permit dogs, others restrict them during breeding season (May–August).
- **Hours**: Open daylight hours. Respect any closure notices during research or conservation work.

### General Safety

- Saunton and surrounding villages are safe, with low crime rates typical of rural Devon.
- Mobile signal can be patchy; inform someone of your plans before heading onto the beach or dunes.
- Weather changes rapidly on Atlantic coasts. Check forecasts before extended beach sessions.

## Local Tips

**Arrive with the tide.** Low tide at Saunton exposes miles of hard-packed sand and reveals rock pools in the northern sections. High tide compresses the usable beach and can trap swimmers. Check tide tables (freely available online) and plan your visit accordingly.

**The northern end (toward Baggy Point headland) is quieter and more rugged** than the main beach. A 30-minute walk along the sand at low tide reaches dramatic rocky outcrops and coastal views. This is prime photography territory.

**Braunton Burrows dune walk is mandatory.** The marked paths through the reserve offer close encounters with rare plants (pyramid orchids, Viper's Bugloss), Common Blue butterflies, and expansive dune views. Allow 60–90 minutes for the full walk. Spring and early summer offer peak wildflower displays.

**Bring layers and waterproof jackets year-round.** Even summer days can turn windy and cool. The dunes offer limited shelter; wind is constant on Atlantic beaches.

**Croyde Bay (2 km north) offers stronger, more reliable swell for experienced surfers.** Saunton is more forgiving for learners due to its exposure being slightly more sheltered. Check conditions at both before committing.

**The Saunton Sands Hotel serves a decent pub meal** and offers the best cliff-top vantage for sunset photography. Book ahead on weekends.

**The Baggy Point headland walk** (starting from Croyde or Woolacombe, not Saunton directly) offers one of North Devon's finest coastal vistas—combine with Saunton for a full day itinerary.

**Parking fills by 11 AM on summer weekends.** Arrive at 8–9 AM or plan for 2+ PM visits when early visitors leave.

**Winter storm watching is exceptional here.** Atlantic gales create 6–10 foot swell and dramatic skies. Locals and photographers consider November–February the true season.
