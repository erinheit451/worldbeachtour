# From Sewage Outfall to Light Show: How Busan Remade Gwangalli

Gwangalli is one of the most photographed beaches in South Korea, and almost nothing about why is natural. The bridge is built, the shows are programmed, the scene is commercial, and even the clean water is the work of a treatment plant. The interesting story of Gwangalli is the story of that manufacture, and of the polluted past it had to overcome first.

## A Beach That Was an Outfall

Begin with the part the night photographs never show. As Busan's residential districts expanded through the 1970s, domestic sewage from the growing city was discharged more or less directly into Gwangalli. By the accounts of the period, household wastewater could be seen flowing into the sea, and the beach was badly fouled. This was not a pristine shore that tourism discovered; it was a polluted urban edge that the city had to rescue.

The rescue was infrastructure, not charm. A pumping station and sewage-treatment system were built to divert Gwangalli's wastewater to the Yongho sewage-treatment plant instead of the bay. From that point, the water quality improved dramatically. It is now regularly monitored and meets environmental standards; in 2025, all of Busan's beaches, Gwangalli included, passed testing for contaminants and heavy metals. The single most important thing that ever happened to Gwangalli Beach was a sewage plant, and it is the thing no one comes to see.

## The Bridge That Became the Attraction

With the water fixed, Gwangalli's transformation into a spectacle could begin, and it began across the water rather than on the sand. In January 2003 the **Gwangan Bridge**, the suspension span universally called the **Diamond Bridge**, opened. It runs about 7.4 kilometers, making it the second-longest bridge in South Korea, and it is lit every single night. Gwangalli sits inside the cove the bridge crosses, and from that point the beach reoriented itself entirely around the view of it. It is a genuine inversion of the usual order: a piece of transport infrastructure that became a seaside resort's primary scenic attraction. The classic Gwangalli photograph is not of the beach at all; it is of the lit bridge over the dark bay, taken from the beach.

## Programming the Sky

Busan then did something deliberate: it began programming the sky over Gwangalli as a recurring show. The **Busan International Fireworks Festival** launched in 2005, originally tied to the APEC summit Busan hosted that year, and became an annual autumn event over the beach, growing into one of the largest pyrotechnic displays in the country and packing the shore each fall.

Then, in April 2022, came the **Gwangalli M (Marvelous) Drone Light Show**, billed as Korea's first permanent drone light show. Staged every Saturday night over the bay, it sends hundreds of drones, around 500 in the standard edition and far more for special ones, into choreographed, seasonally changing formations. Where most beaches' rhythms are set by tides, Gwangalli's are increasingly set by a show schedule. The drone show in particular has become a fixed weekly ritual and a centerpiece of Busan's branding as a tech-forward, spectacle-driven city.

## The Scene Behind the Sand

The final layer is commercial and human. The streets behind the beach filled with cafés, dessert shops, craft-beer bars, cocktail places, raw-fish houses, and nightclubs, many angled deliberately at the bridge view. This density of café-and-nightlife is what made Gwangalli the trendier, younger alternative to Busan's bigger and more touristed Haeundae Beach to the east. Just next door, the Millak waterfront serves *hoe*, Korean-style raw fish, eaten with the bridge in view. People come to Gwangalli to sit at a window as much as to step on the sand.

## A Parable of the Engineered Beach

Stack the layers and Gwangalli becomes a small, honest parable of the modern city beach. The water is clean because of a treatment plant; the view exists because of a bridge; the spectacle exists because of a fireworks festival and a drone program; the atmosphere exists because of a commercial strip. Almost every element is built, fixed, or staged. That could be told as a loss of authenticity, but it is fairer told as an achievement: a beach a city poisoned and then, through unglamorous public investment, made swimmable, then beautiful, then a nightly performance. The most honest way to enjoy the drone show over Gwangalli is to remember what used to flow into that water, and what it took to change it.
