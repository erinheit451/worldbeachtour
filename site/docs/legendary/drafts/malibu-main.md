## story

Malibu has been performing itself since 1957, and the performance has not stopped for a single summer. This is the three-point cobblestone right-hander where the longboard canon was written, where modern surf culture was packaged for export, and where the Clean Water Act grew the teeth it had been waiting thirty years to grow. A mile of sand at the mouth of Malibu Creek that accidentally became a language the rest of the planet now speaks.

The shorthand is absurd when you list it out. A 5'1" Brentwood teenager named Kathy Kohner spent the summers of 1956 through 1959 riding First Point in a one-piece and keeping a notebook. Her Austrian-emigré father read the notebook and wrote a novel. The novel sold two million copies. Columbia made a film. Then two more films. Then a TV show with Sally Field. Meanwhile a Hawthorne garage band called the Beach Boys released three albums between 1961 and 1963 that name-checked Malibu until "Malibu" no longer described a place so much as a posture. Bruce Brown pointed a 16mm camera at First Point and cut "The Endless Summer." Miki Dora trimmed the inside so cleanly that every longboarder since has been quoting him without knowing it. In August 1984, three surfers incorporated a nonprofit in response to a proposed breakwater, and within seven years the Surfrider Foundation had filed the largest Clean Water Act citizen suit in history.

That is the résumé. The wave itself is calmer than the résumé, and older, and — if you know where to stand on the bluff at six in the morning — genuinely beautiful in the way the résumé is not. So look at the wave.

## the_wave

First Point is a cobblestone point break, which is a tidy geological way of saying that Malibu Creek has spent the last several thousand years washing river rock down out of the Santa Monica Mountains and depositing it, stone by stone, into a submerged ramp that angles out from the headland. The ramp is the wave. Nothing else at Malibu is the wave. The sand on the inside is incidental, the pier is a reference point, the mountains are a windbreak — but the cobblestone ramp is the architecture, and it is the only reason any of this exists.

When a south or southwest swell generated in the Southern Hemisphere — the season runs April through October, with the cleanest pulses in June and July — reaches that ramp at the correct angle, the swell refracts across the cobblestone and peels north toward the pier as a long, shoulder-high, tapered right. It does not close out. It is almost impossible to make it close out. The shoulder travels across the stones at a pace that is, famously and accurately, slow. "Slow" here is a mechanical description, not a complaint. The wave's face moves at a speed that rewards a surfer who can trim a long board along the pocket rather than one who needs to generate thrust through short, violent turns. The wave dictates the board. This is the fact that produced everything downstream.

In 1947, three shapers — Bob Simmons, Joe Quigg, and Matt Kivlin — began building lighter balsa boards specifically for First Point. The boards became known as Malibu chips. The name survived the era. A "Malibu board" is a shaper term now, in workshops from Biarritz to Byron Bay, and it means a specific silhouette the wave at First Point asked for seventy-nine years ago.

The second gift of the geography is the wind. The Santa Monica Mountains block the prevailing afternoon onshores until mid-morning, which opens a window — offshore at dawn, sideshore by nine, soft-onshore by noon — that on a good June swell can hold glassy conditions until almost eleven. The three points line up along the same cobblestone shelf. First Point, south, breaks first and holds the smallest swell. Second Point, in the middle, is steeper and needs more size to turn on. Third Point, closest to the Colony fence, needs the biggest swell of the three to connect all the way through.

The dark side is hydrology. Malibu Creek drains a watershed that includes the Tapia wastewater plant's historic discharge zone, decades of hillside septic, and the lagoon behind First Point. The creek mouth empties directly into the surf zone. After any winter rain, the inside section becomes a bacterial plume, and Heal the Bay has listed Surfrider Beach among California's most polluted stretches of coast more years than anyone on the point would care to count. The wave and the pollution come from the same watershed. They are not separable, and the page that tries to separate them is lying.

## cultural_footprint

The list is almost embarrassing. Frederick Kohner's *Gidget: The Little Girl with Big Ideas* (1957) and its four sequels. Columbia's 1959 film *Gidget* with Sandra Dee, followed by *Gidget Goes Hawaiian* (1961) and *Gidget Goes to Rome* (1963). The ABC series from 1965 to 1966 with a nineteen-year-old Sally Field. The Beach Boys' *Surfin'* (1961), *Surfin' Safari* (1962), and *Surfin' U.S.A.* (1963), the last of which catalogues Malibu by name on the first chorus. Jan and Dean's *Surf City* (1963) and *Ride the Wild Surf* (1964). Bruce Brown's three films — *Slippery When Wet* (1958), *Barefoot Adventure* (1960), and the one that colonized a generation, *The Endless Summer* (1966). Greg MacGillivray and Jim Freeman's *Five Summer Stories* (1972). William Finnegan's *Barbarian Days* (2015), which won the Pulitzer and whose Malibu chapters are the most honest English-language prose anyone has written about the place. Matt Warshaw's *Encyclopedia of Surfing* and *History of Surfing* (2010), where the Malibu entry is the reference everyone else borrows from. Craig Stecyk and Glen E. Friedman made the photographs. Dewey Weber turned his own name into the first surfboard brand — the commercial prototype was Malibu's, too.

The shaper vocabulary is Malibu's. The "Malibu chip," after the 1947 Simmons-Quigg-Kivlin boards; the "Malibu board" silhouette that survived into the 2020s; the Malibu Surfing Association, founded 1961 and still paddling out. The archetypes are Malibu's. Gidget. Moondoggie. The Kahuna. The Black Knight. Four nicknames that became American shorthand before most of America could place the beach on a map.

Here is the observation with teeth. The beach sold an image of California to America so successfully that most of America still thinks California looks like this mile. It does not. California has 840 miles of coast; this is one of them. But the pier, the palms, the longboard peeling glass-smooth toward the Adamson House tiles — that is the image, and it has held the frame for seventy years.

## miki_dora

Every page about Malibu arrives, eventually, at Miki Dora. There is no route around him. He was born Miklós Dóra in Budapest in 1934, surfed First Point from roughly 1954 through 1973, and died of pancreatic cancer in Montecito on January 3, 2002. In between, he ran the single most contradictory career in the history of the sport.

Start with the wave. Dora trimmed the inside at First Point better than anyone who has ever done it. The trim line — that long, elongated, arms-loose drop into the pocket that every longboarder has been copying since — is Dora's line. Warshaw's *Encyclopedia* says so explicitly. Greg Noll said so. Lance Carson, who became the other defining stylist of the era, said so. He was the most photographed surfer of the longboard decades because the camera kept coming back to the same body on the same wave making the same case for what style meant.

Then the rest. Dora's feud with Johnny Fain — "the Little Prince of Malibu," six years younger and less willing to defer — produced actual on-wave fistfights through the 1960s and the soap opera that localism at First Point inherited. At the 1967 Malibu Invitational, Dora, mid-wave, dropped his trunks in full view of the beach in protest of contest-ification. The gesture became scripture. Surf anti-commercialism dated itself to that one ride. Then Dora left the country, and what he did next is not footnotes. He ran credit card fraud across Europe, South Africa, and Australia for most of two decades. He was finally arrested in France in 1995, extradited, and served time. The obituaries in 2002 — *LA Times*, *New York Times*, *Surfer* — did not pretend otherwise. They canonized him as surfing's patron anti-saint, which is a polite way of saying that the sport's most influential stylist was also a con artist whose recorded views on race do not survive clean inspection in 2026.

The contradiction is the point. Dora loved First Point and despised what the culture around First Point became, and his answer was not to leave but to poison the well he kept drinking from. He called the crowds "kooks" and took their money anyway. He wrote his farewell to Malibu in 1967 with his trunks around his ankles and then kept surfing there for six more years. The page cannot resolve this, and it will not try. Dora is the honest mirror Malibu looks into when it thinks no one is watching.

## history

In 1892, Frederick Hastings Rindge — Harvard graduate, son of Cambridge industrialists — bought Rancho Topanga Malibu Sequit, a 13,000-acre stretch of the original Spanish land grant that covered most of what is now Malibu. The Chumash had lived at the mouth of the creek for at least two thousand years; their village, Humaliwo, sits beneath and adjacent to what is now the First Point parking lot, and the site is catalogued as CA-LAN-264. The Rindge purchase is the unmarked foundation of everything.

May Rindge, Frederick's widow, fought the railroads and the state for three decades to keep the coast private. Her freight pier, built in 1905, was converted to public fishing and opened in 1926; it is still there. In 1928, Rhoda Rindge Adamson, May's daughter, completed the tile-crusted Adamson House on Vaquero Hill between First Point and the lagoon. It sits yards from the lineup today. In 1947, Bob Simmons, Joe Quigg, and Matt Kivlin began shaping the Malibu chip. In 1956, Kathy Kohner started her summers at the First Point shack, where Terry "Tubesteak" Tracy gave her the nickname. In 1957, her father published the novel. In 1959, Sandra Dee filmed the Columbia adaptation with Mickey Munoz and Mickey Dora doubling the water footage. The Beach Boys arrived in 1961, 1962, and 1963. Bruce Brown's *Endless Summer* reached national theatrical release in 1966. Dora dropped his trunks at the Malibu Invitational in 1967.

Then the 1983 El Niño. A winter of battering storms rearranged the cobblestones on the bottom of First Point and retuned the wave. Anyone on the point will still tell you there is a "before '83" and an "after '83," and they will usually tell you the after is a touch worse. In August 1984, Glen Henning, Tom Pratte, and Lance Carson — three surfers responding to a proposed breakwater and development threats at First Point — incorporated the Surfrider Foundation. In 1991, Surfrider filed a Clean Water Act citizen suit against two Humboldt County pulp mills, Louisiana-Pacific and Simpson; it was, at the time, the largest such suit in American history, and it settled for a then-record penalty. The template for the modern coastal lawsuit was written in Malibu.

Dora was arrested in France in 1995 and died in 2002. In 2010, the Save The Waves Coalition designated Surfrider Beach the first World Surfing Reserve on Earth. In 2013, after a contentious multi-year fight, the Malibu Lagoon Restoration Project completed — praised by water-quality scientists, resented by surfers who watched bulldozers work yards from the lineup. In 2018, the Woolsey Fire burned to the edge of the Malibu coast, and the ash and debris flows down the creek degraded First Point's water for months afterward. The chronology keeps running. The wave keeps peeling.

## three_points

The three points sit along the same submerged cobblestone shelf, stacked south to north, and the distance between them is shorter than most beachgoers think — you can walk First to Third in ten minutes along the sand. The wave each one produces is, nonetheless, genuinely different, and locals sort them in a hierarchy that tracks swell size rather than prestige.

First Point, breaking just north of Malibu Pier, is the wave of the canon. It is the slowest, the longest, the most forgiving, and the most crowded. It will break on almost any south swell from April through October, and on a clean two-to-four-foot morning it produces the peeling, shoulder-high right that *The Endless Summer* taught the planet to recognize. It is the longboard wave. On a busy summer Saturday, the lineup at First Point will hold sixty people without remorse.

Second Point sits directly north. It is shorter, steeper, and faster. It needs more swell to turn on — four feet minimum before it becomes rideable, and it rewards a shorter board and a surfer willing to commit to the drop rather than negotiate the trim. Historically it was where the shortboard crew went when First Point clogged. It still is.

Third Point is the northernmost, closest to the Malibu Colony fence line, and it needs the biggest south swell of the three to connect all the way through. When a real south pulse arrives — six feet, long period, the kind that shows up two or three times a season — Third Point is the wave that actually holds up, linking its sections past the Colony decks and down toward Second. It is also the least ridden, partly because it requires the conditions, and partly because the psychology of paddling out directly beneath the fence of a private celebrity enclave is a small but real tax. The fence does what fences do. The wave breaks in front of it anyway.

## honest_reckoning

Four uncomfortable truths this page will not route around.

**Humaliwo.** The Chumash word means "where the surf sounds loudly," and the village it named sat at the mouth of Malibu Creek for at least two thousand years. "Malibu" is a Spanish corruption of Humaliwo. The archaeological site, CA-LAN-264, is registered immediately adjacent to the lagoon and the First Point parking lot — which is to say, beneath and around and under the asphalt where every surfer in this essay parked. Frederick Rindge's 1892 purchase of the 13,000-acre Rancho Topanga Malibu Sequit is the ownership lineage every modern deed in the zip code traces back to, and it erased a continuous occupation older than most things Europeans like to call old. The Humaliwo spoke of this cluster treats the village on its own terms. Read it before you finish this page.

**The water.** For decades, Malibu Creek has carried Tapia wastewater plant discharge and hillside septic leachate directly across First Point. Heal the Bay has repeatedly listed Surfrider Beach among California's most polluted, and the listings are not historical footnotes — they are recent. The lagoon restoration of 2013, fought bitterly on both sides, was an attempt to re-engineer the hydrology; whether it worked depends on whom you ask and what the last storm did. The Surfrider Foundation was incorporated in 1984 specifically because of the water at First Point. The Surfrider Foundation spoke traces that lineage from breakwater fight to *Louisiana-Pacific* to the present.

**The Colony.** Surfrider Beach is a public California state beach. The Malibu Colony, founded 1926 on leased Rindge land and home since to Gloria Swanson, Gary Cooper, Barbra Streisand, and the rotating roster of whoever Hollywood is currently buying beach houses for — abuts Third Point. Colony owners have spent the better part of a century testing the California Coastal Act: signage, fence placements, guards, the whole repertoire of soft-access denial. The state has won more of these fights than it has lost, but the fights do not stop. You can walk the wet sand. You should.

**Localism.** From roughly 1975 into the late 1980s, First Point was a territorially enforced wave. Allen Sarlo is the bridge figure between Dora's Malibu and the modern lineup, and he personified the enforcement era. The unresolved question — which the point has not resolved and is not going to in this paragraph — is who belongs in the water at a wave that was marketed to the entire planet seventy years ago and now has an international lineup every summer morning. And Dora folds into this too: the figure most associated with Malibu was, by any accounting that takes the evidence seriously, a con artist and a man whose recorded views on race do not survive clean inspection in 2026.

Four paragraphs. You cannot love this wave honestly without reckoning with them. The rest of the page proceeds on that basis.
